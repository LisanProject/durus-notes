The Six Types of Shirk Explained

Defining Ibadah (Worship)

Worship, or ibadah, requires an underlying belief. It is not just an outward act on its own. Specifically, ibadah involves the belief that the entity being venerated deserves that veneration and glorification in its own right. This belief is what actually turns an action into worship, which is why the same outward action can carry a completely different ruling depending on what the person doing it actually believes.

Does Sujud Always Mean Shirk?

Ibn Taymiyyah argued that prostration (sujud) does not automatically count as shirk by itself. He pointed to the angels prostrating to Adam, and to Prophet Ya'qub prostrating to Yusuf, as cases where sujud occurred without any element of worship being intended toward the one being prostrated to.

The Prophet Muhammad made prostrating to another human being impermissible under his own Sharia, so this earlier allowance no longer applies to Muslims today. Still, prostrating in front of a grave is not automatically treated as a blanket case of shirk. It becomes shirk specifically when the person believes the one in the grave can independently harm or benefit them, apart from Allah's power. If someone prostrates at a grave out of genuine ignorance rather than that belief, it is treated as a sin to be corrected through commanding good and forbidding evil, rather than grounds for an immediate declaration of disbelief.

Within the Muslim ummah today, sujud is understood almost exclusively as an act of worship. A simple greeting gesture, of the kind permitted to earlier communities, is no longer a realistic reading of the act, which is part of why the ruling has tightened even as the underlying principle, that sujud itself is not automatically shirk, remains intact.

What Shirk Actually Requires

Shirk is defined, at its core, by the belief that some entity can affect or control reality independently of Allah. This is the line that separates an act that merely looks similar to worship from an act that actually is worship, and it is the standard the discussion keeps returning to across every example that follows.

Intercession Is Not Worship

Asking the Prophet for intercession (shafa'a) is not considered ibadah, because the believer understands that the Prophet was granted this ability by Allah rather than possessing it on his own. The request is directed at someone believed to be acting by Allah's permission, not at an independent source of power, which is exactly the distinction that separates this from shirk under the definition above.

The lecture also addressed the claim that the dead cannot hear, citing hadiths describing the deceased hearing footsteps, and the Prophet responding to the greetings sent upon him on Fridays. These reports are treated as evidence that hearing after death is a real possibility within the tradition, not something ruled out on principle.

The Soul, and Knowledge Through Dreams

The soul (ruh) is described as belonging to Allah's "Amr," or Command, a reality that leaves scientists and philosophers genuinely puzzled, since the soul departs the body without changing the body's physical functionality in any way science can fully account for.

Knowledge ('ilm) is defined as perception that actually matches reality. On this definition, dreams can serve as a valid stream of knowledge or inspiration (ilham). The early stages of the Prophet's messengership, which began with true dreams, are cited as the standard example of this.

The Six Types of Shirk

وَأَنْوَاعُ الشِّرْكِ سِتَّةٌ: شِرْكُ اسْتِقْلَالٍ، وَهُوَ إِثْبَاتُ إِلَهَيْنِ مُسْتَقِلَّيْنِ، كَشِرْكِ الْمَجُوسِ. وَشِرْكُ تَبْعِيضٍ، وَهُوَ تَرْكِيبُ الْإِلَهِ مِنْ آلِهَةٍ، كَشِرْكِ النَّصَارَى. وَشِرْكُ تَقْرِيبٍ، وَهُوَ عِبَادَةُ غَيْرِ اللَّهِ تَعَالَى لِيُتَقَرَّبَ إِلَى اللَّهِ زُلْفَى، كَشِرْكِ مُتَقَدِّمِي الْجَاهِلِيَّةِ. وَشِرْكُ تَقْلِيدٍ، وَهُوَ عِبَادَةُ غَيْرِ اللَّهِ تَعَالَى تَبَعاً لِلْغَيْرِ، كَشِرْكِ مُتَأَخِّرِي الْجَاهِلِيَّةِ. وَشِرْكُ الْأَسْبَابِ، وَهُوَ إِسْنَادُ التَّأْثِيرِ لِلْأَسْبَابِ الْعَادِيَّةِ، كَشِرْكِ الْفَلَاسِفَةِ وَالطَّبَائِعِيِّينَ وَمَنْ تَبِعَهُمْ عَلَى ذَلِكَ. وَشِرْكُ الْأَغْرَاضِ، وَهُوَ الْعَمَلُ لِغَيْرِ اللَّهِ تَعَالَى.
There are six types of shirk. Shirk of independence, which is affirming two independent gods, like the shirk of the Magians. Shirk of composition, which is assembling a god out of multiple gods, like the shirk of the Christians. Shirk of nearness, which is worshipping other than Allah in order to draw near to Allah, like the shirk of the earlier pre-Islamic Arabs. Shirk of blind following, which is worshipping other than Allah simply because others do, like the shirk of the later pre-Islamic Arabs. Shirk of causal means, which is attributing real effect to ordinary natural causes, like the shirk of the philosophers, the naturalists, and those who followed them in that. Shirk of purposes, which is acting for someone other than Allah.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat Anwa' al-Shirk, on the six types.

1. Shirk al-Istiqlal (Independence). Affirming two independent gods, as with the Magians. This and the next three types are treated as outright disbelief (kufr) by consensus (ijma').

(1)

2. Shirk al-Tab'id (Composition). Compounding a single god out of multiple gods, as with the Christian doctrine of the Trinity as understood in this context. Also kufr by consensus.

(2)

3. Shirk al-Taqrib (Nearness). Worshipping something other than Allah as a way of drawing closer to Allah, as with the earlier pre-Islamic Arabs. Also kufr by consensus.

(3)

4. Shirk al-Taqlid (Blind Following). Worshipping something other than Allah simply because one's forebears did, as with the later pre-Islamic Arabs. Also kufr by consensus.

(4)

5. Shirk al-Asbab (Causal Means). Attributing real, independent effect to an ordinary natural cause, as with the philosophers and naturalists. This one carries a real distinction depending on exactly what is believed, covered just below.

(5)

6. Shirk al-Aghrad (Purposes). Acting for the sake of someone other than Allah, also known as hidden shirk (riya), such as praying beautifully in order to impress the people watching. Unlike the first four, this one is treated as a sin rather than disbelief, by consensus.

(6)
وَحُكْمُ الْأَرْبَعَةِ الْأُوَلِ: الْكُفْرُ بِإِجْمَاعٍ. وَحُكْمُ السَّادِسِ: الْمَعْصِيَةُ، مِنْ غَيْرِ كُفْرٍ بِإِجْمَاعٍ. وَحُكْمُ الْخَامِسِ: التَّفْصِيلُ فِيهَا؛ فَمَنْ قَالَ إِنَّهَا تُؤَثِّرُ بِطَبْعِهَا فَقَدْ حُكِيَ الْإِجْمَاعُ عَلَى كُفْرِهِ، وَمَنْ قَالَ: إِنَّهَا تُؤَثِّرُ بِقُوَّةٍ أَوْدَعَهَا اللَّهُ فِيهَا، فَهُوَ فَاسِقٌ مُبْتَدِعٌ، وَفِي كُفْرِهِ قَوْلَانِ.
The ruling for the first four is disbelief, by consensus. The ruling for the sixth is sin, without disbelief, by consensus. The ruling for the fifth requires detail. Whoever says that the means has effect by its own nature, consensus has been reported on his disbelief. Whoever says it has effect through a power Allah placed within it, he is a sinful innovator, and there are two opinions on whether he disbelieves.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the rulings of the six types.

In practice, this means believing that fire burns purely by its own independent nature is disbelief by consensus, since it denies Allah's role entirely. Believing instead that Allah placed a burning power within fire, which fire then exercises, is not disbelief by consensus, though it is still considered a sinful innovation, with scholars disagreeing over whether it crosses into disbelief as well.

A Note on Frameworks and Fair Criticism

The lecture closed with some reflections on how this material gets used in contemporary debates. It addressed critics who discourage visiting the Prophet's grave ﷺ in Medina, arguing that genuine devotion is simply unmoved by criticism from those who do not share or understand it. It also raised a broader concern about frameworks that apply these categories inconsistently, shifting their own definitions mid-argument rather than holding to one consistent standard throughout a discussion.

Quranic verses that appear to describe the Prophet ﷺ as doubting, or as being at risk of shirk, were addressed as well. These are understood either as rhetorical emphasis, or as directed at the wider ummah rather than at the Prophet ﷺ personally, since the Prophet ﷺ is held to be protected from error (ma'sum) in matters of prophecy. The distinction between specific and generic Quranic address ('amm and khass) was raised as the key tool for understanding why a verse phrased in general terms is not automatically a universal rule applying to every person at every time.
