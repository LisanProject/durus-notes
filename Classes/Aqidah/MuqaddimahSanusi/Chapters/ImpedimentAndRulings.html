Wajib, Mustahil, and Jaiz Explained

The Five Legal Rulings (Al-Ahkam al-Shar'iyyah)

Before reaching al-mani', the text first defines the five legal rulings that every act falls under. Wajib (obligatory) is covered elsewhere in this primer. The remaining four are given here.

وَالنَّدْبُ: وَهُوَ طَلَبُ الْفِعْلِ طَلَباً غَيْرَ جَازِمٍ، كَصَلَاةِ الْفَجْرِ وَنَحْوِهَا. وَالتَّحْرِيمُ: وَهُوَ طَلَبُ الْكَفِّ عَنِ الْفِعْلِ طَلَباً جَازِماً، كَشُرْبِ الْخَمْرِ وَالزِّنَا وَنَحْوِهِمَا. وَالْكَرَاهَةُ: وَهِيَ طَلَبُ الْكَفِّ عَنِ الْفِعْلِ طَلَباً غَيْرَ جَازِمٍ، كَالْقِرَاءَةِ فِي الرُّكُوعِ وَالسُّجُودِ مَثَلاً. وَأَمَّا الْإِبَاحَةُ: فَهِيَ إِذْنُ الشَّرْعِ فِي الْفِعْلِ وَالتَّرْكِ مَعاً، مِنْ غَيْرِ تَرْجِيحٍ لِأَحَدِهِمَا عَلَى الْآخَرِ، كَالنِّكَاحِ وَالْبَيْعِ مَثَلاً.
Mandub (recommended) is a non-binding request to perform an act, like the dawn prayer's sunnah and similar acts. Haram (forbidden) is a binding request to refrain from an act, like drinking wine or fornication and similar acts. Makruh (disliked) is a non-binding request to refrain from an act, like reciting Qur'an during bowing or prostration, for example. As for mubah (permissible), it is the Sharia's allowance for both doing and not doing an act, without preferring either one over the other, like marriage or a sale, for example.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the five legal rulings.

Mandub (Recommended). A non-binding request to perform an act. The dawn sunnah prayer is the standard example. Leaving it is not sinful, but performing it carries reward.

(1)

Haram (Forbidden). A binding request to refrain from an act. Drinking wine and fornication are the standard examples. Performing it is sinful.

(2)

Makruh (Disliked). A non-binding request to refrain from an act. Reciting Qur'an while bowing or prostrating is the standard example. Performing it is not sinful, but avoiding it carries reward.

(3)

Mubah (Permissible). The Sharia's plain allowance to do or not do something, with no preference either way. Marriage and ordinary buying and selling are the standard examples.

(4)

Al-Wad': Sabab, Shart, and Mani'

Al-wad' (positing) is the Lawgiver's act of establishing a marker that points to one of the five rulings above. Three such markers are defined here: sabab, shart, and mani'. Each one describes a different logical relationship between something existing or not, and a ruling existing or not.

فَأَمَّا الْوَضْعُ: فَهُوَ عِبَارَةٌ عَنْ نَصْبِ الشَّارِعِ أَمَارَةً عَلَى حُكْمٍ مِنْ تِلْكَ الْأَحْكَامِ الْخَمْسَةِ، وَهِيَ: السَّبَبُ، وَالشَّرْطُ، وَالْمَانِعُ. فَالسَّبَبُ: مَا يَلْزَمُ مِنْ وُجُودِهِ الْوُجُودُ وَمِنْ عَدَمِهِ الْعَدَمُ لِذَاتِهِ، كَزَوَالِ الشَّمْسِ لِوُجُوبِ الظُّهْرِ. وَالشَّرْطُ: مَا يَلْزَمُ مِنْ عَدَمِهِ الْعَدَمُ، وَلَا يَلْزَمُ مِنْ وُجُودِهِ وُجُودٌ وَلَا عَدَمٌ لِذَاتِهِ، كَتَمَامِ الْحَوْلِ مَثَلاً لِوُجُوبِ الزَّكَاةِ. وَالْمَانِعُ: مَا يَلْزَمُ مِنْ وُجُودِهِ الْعَدَمُ، وَلَا يَلْزَمُ مِنْ عَدَمِهِ وُجُودٌ وَلَا عَدَمٌ لِذَاتِهِ، كَالْحَيْضِ لِوُجُوبِ الصَّلَاةِ.
As for al-wad': it is an expression for the Lawgiver establishing a marker for a ruling among those five rulings, namely: the cause, the condition, and the impediment. The cause is that whose existence necessitates existence, and whose non-existence necessitates non-existence, by itself, like the sun's decline for the obligation of Dhuhr. The condition is that whose non-existence necessitates non-existence, while its existence necessitates neither existence nor non-existence, by itself, like the completion of a full year, for example, for the obligation of zakah. The impediment is that whose existence necessitates non-existence, while its non-existence necessitates neither existence nor non-existence, by itself, like menstruation for the obligation of prayer.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on al-wad', sabab, shart, and mani'.

Sabab (Cause). Its existence guarantees the ruling's existence, and its non-existence guarantees the ruling's non-existence, purely by what it is. The sun's decline is the standard example: it is exactly what makes the Dhuhr prayer obligatory, and without it, that obligation is not there at all.

(1)

Shart (Condition). Its non-existence guarantees the ruling's non-existence, but its existence by itself guarantees neither the ruling's existence nor its non-existence. A full lunar year passing is the standard example for zakah: without it, zakah is not yet due, but its passing alone does not by itself bring the obligation about independent of other factors like nisab.

(2)

Mani' (Impediment). Its existence guarantees the ruling's non-existence, but its non-existence by itself guarantees neither the ruling's existence nor its non-existence. Menstruation is the standard example: its presence is exactly what removes the obligation of prayer, but its mere absence does not by itself establish that the obligation exists, since other conditions still need to be met.

(3)

Al-Hukm al-'Adi (The Customary Ruling)

A customary ruling is a connection people notice between two things because they keep happening together. The mind links them through repetition. It does not link them because one actually causes the other. Eating and feeling full is the standard example.

Causality Is Denied. In the Sunni creed these customary pairings are never treated as real causes. Eating does not "cause" fullness. Allah simply places the two events together as a habit. Because the link is not causal, it can also break. It is possible to eat and not become full. This is exactly why the category is called customary rather than necessary.

(1)

Takhalluf (Exception). Takhalluf is not just a loose word for "exception." It refers to one specific thing. It is the failure of the usual pairing to happen in a given case. Customary rulings rest on repeated observation rather than logical necessity. So takhalluf is always possible for them. It is never possible for a genuinely rational ruling.

(2)

There are four types of customary connection. Eating is paired with fullness and hunger in each example below.

وَأَقْسَامُهُ أَرْبَعَةٌ: رَبْطُ وُجُودٍ بِوُجُودٍ، كَرَبْطِ وُجُودِ الشِّبَعِ بِوُجُودِ الْأَكْلِ. وَرَبْطُ عَدَمٍ بِعَدَمٍ، كَرَبْطِ عَدَمِ الشِّبَعِ بِعَدَمِ الْأَكْلِ. وَرَبْطُ وُجُودٍ بِعَدَمٍ، كَرَبْطِ وُجُودِ الْجُوعِ بِعَدَمِ الْأَكْلِ. وَرَبْطُ عَدَمٍ بِوُجُودٍ، كَرَبْطِ عَدَمِ الْجُوعِ بِوُجُودِ الْأَكْلِ.
Its types are four. Linking existence to existence, like linking the existence of fullness to the existence of eating. Linking non-existence to non-existence, like linking the non-existence of fullness to the non-existence of eating. Linking existence to non-existence, like linking the existence of hunger to the non-existence of eating. Linking non-existence to existence, like linking the non-existence of hunger to the existence of eating.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the types of al-hukm al-'adi.

1. Eating happens and fullness happens.

(1)

2. Eating does not happen and fullness does not happen.

(2)

3. Hunger happens and eating does not happen.

(3)

4. Hunger does not happen and eating happens.

(4)

Al-Hukm al-'Aqli (The Rational Ruling)

A rational ruling comes from the intellect alone. It does not depend on custom or on Divine legislation. Unlike customary rulings, a rational ruling never allows exceptions. It falls into three categories.

وَأَمَّا الْحُكْمُ الْعَقْلِيُّ: فَهُوَ إِثْبَاتُ أَمْرٍ أَوْ نَفْيُهُ مِنْ غَيْرِ تَوَقُّفٍ عَلَى تَكَرُّرٍ وَلَا وَضْعِ وَاضِعٍ.
The rational ruling affirms or denies something. It does not depend on repetition. It does not depend on any legislator setting a rule.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the definition of al-hukm al-'aqli.

وَأَقْسَامُهُ ثَلَاثَةٌ: الْوُجُوبُ، وَالِاسْتِحَالَةُ، وَالْجَوَازُ.
Its types are three: the necessary, the impossible, and the possible.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the three types of al-hukm al-'aqli.

1. Wajib (Necessary). The mind cannot picture this as absent. A body must take up space. That example is self-evident. Allah has no beginning. That example needs reasoning to establish. Either way, the mind cannot deny it.

(1)
فَالْوَاجِبُ: مَا لَا يُتَصَوَّرُ فِي الْعَقْلِ عَدَمُهُ، إِمَّا ضَرُورَةً كَالتَّحَيُّزِ لِلْجِرْمِ مَثَلاً، وَإِمَّا نَظَراً كَوُجُوبِ الْقِدَمِ لِمَوْلَانَا جَلَّ وَعَزَّ.
The necessary is what the mind cannot picture as absent. One kind is self-evident, like a body taking up space. Another kind needs reasoning, like the necessity of Allah having no beginning, Glorified and Exalted is He.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the definition of the necessary.

2. Mustahil (Impossible). The mind cannot picture this as existing at all. A body with no motion and no stillness at the same time is the self-evident example. A partner for Allah is the example that needs reasoning. Neither one is simply rare. Both are inconceivable.

(2)
وَالْمُسْتَحِيلُ: مَا لَا يُتَصَوَّرُ فِي الْعَقْلِ وُجُودُهُ، إِمَّا ضَرُورَةً كَتَعَرِّي الْجِرْمِ عَنِ الْحَرَكَةِ وَالسُّكُونِ، وَإِمَّا نَظَراً كَالشَّرِيكِ لِمَوْلَانَا جَلَّ وَعَزَّ.
The impossible is what the mind cannot picture as existing. One kind is self-evident, like a body being free of both motion and stillness. Another kind needs reasoning, like a partner for Allah, Glorified and Exalted is He.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the definition of the impossible.

3. Jaiz (Possible). The mind can accept this as existing or not existing. Neither option creates a contradiction. Motion for us is the self-evident example. A person can move or stay still, and the mind has no trouble picturing either one. Punishing the obedient and rewarding the disobedient is the example that needs reasoning. Both outcomes are conceivable, even though neither is what Allah has actually decreed.

(3)
وَالْجَائِزُ: مَا يَصِحُّ فِي الْعَقْلِ وُجُودُهُ وَعَدَمُهُ، إِمَّا ضَرُورَةً كَالْحَرَكَةِ لَنَا، وَإِمَّا نَظَراً كَتَعْذِيبِ الْمُطِيعِ وَإِثَابَةِ الْعَاصِي.
The possible is what the mind can validly picture as existing or not existing. One kind is self-evident, like motion for us. Another kind needs reasoning, like punishing the obedient and rewarding the disobedient.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the definition of the possible.

Rational knowledge itself splits into two kinds, based on how you reach it. 'Ilm daruri is immediate knowledge. You know it instantly, with no thinking required, like a body needing space. 'Ilm nazari is reflective knowledge. It takes real contemplation to reach, like establishing Allah's eternity through a reasoned argument rather than a snap intuition.

Proof and Syllogism

Here is a simple example of reasoned argument for the world being created. Everything that changes is created. The world changes. Therefore the world is created. This shows how 'ilm nazari actually gets built. You start from premises the mind already accepts. You end at a conclusion that was not obvious on its own.

Divine Power and Rational Impossibility

A common objection asks whether Allah can create a rock so heavy He cannot lift it. The answer uses the distinction above. Allah's power relates only to what is rationally possible. An object that is both liftable and unliftable by an all-powerful being is not a real object of power at all. It works the same way "a square circle" names nothing coherent for power to act on. Rational absurdities fall outside the objects of Divine power. This is not a limit on Allah's power. These things were never real possibilities to begin with.

Ownership, Divine Justice, and Compulsion

Allah owns all of creation completely. Because of this He cannot be a wrongdoer in the true sense, even when He punishes or rewards as He wills. Wrongdoing means disposing of something that belongs to someone else without right. Nothing belongs to anyone apart from Allah's ownership. So this definition simply does not apply to Him.

Death is the standard example of Allah's compelling power. It forces itself on every person no matter what they want. No one can escape it, however much they dislike it. It shows that Allah's will acts on creation whether or not creation agrees to it.
