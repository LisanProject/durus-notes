Jabariyyah, Qadariyyah, and Ahl al-Sunnah on Kasb

The Second Muqaddimah

This second preface covers the doctrines on the voluntary actions of living beings. These are actions performed by choice. The focus here is human action and where it actually comes from. A mazhab, in this context, is a doctrine or position held by a group of learned scholars. It is not just one person's opinion.

Islamic creed splits into three areas. Ilahiyyat covers matters about Allah. Nubuwwat covers matters about the prophets. Sam'iyyat covers unseen matters known only through revelation, like the afterlife. The question of human action falls under ilahiyyat. It ultimately concerns the relationship between Allah's power and what creation does.

Three Doctrines on Human Action

وَالْمَذَاهِبُ فِي الْأَفْعَالِ ثَلَاثَةٌ: مَذْهَبُ الْجَبْرِيَّةِ، وَمَذْهَبُ الْقَدَرِيَّةِ، وَمَذْهَبُ أَهْلِ السُّنَّةِ.
There are three doctrines on actions. The doctrine of the Jabariyyah, the doctrine of the Qadariyyah, and the doctrine of Ahl al-Sunnah.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the three doctrines of human action.

1. Al-Jabariyyah. All actions exist through the eternal power alone. No created power plays any role at all. On this view a human being is fully compelled and has no real independent will. Every action belongs to Allah's power alone. The human being is reduced to a passive instrument.

(1)

2. Al-Qadariyyah. Voluntary actions exist through the created power alone. This holds whether the action happens directly or through a further effect. The eternal power plays no role in bringing the action about. On this view human choice is treated as fully independent of Allah's decree. Some in this group even denied that events are predestined at all. Ibn 'Umar, may Allah be pleased with him, dissociated himself from this group for denying qadar.

(2)

3. Ahl al-Sunnah. All actions exist through the eternal power alone. But a created power belonging to the voluntary action is present alongside it. This created power has no independent effect. It plays no causal role in bringing the action into existence.

(3)
فَمَذْهَبُ الْجَبْرِيَّةِ: وُجُودُ الْأَفْعَالِ كُلِّهَا بِالْقُدْرَةِ الْأَزَلِيَّةِ فَقَطْ، مِنْ غَيْرِ مُقَارَنَةِ قُدْرَةٍ حَادِثَةٍ. وَمَذْهَبُ الْقَدَرِيَّةِ: وُجُودُ الْأَفْعَالِ الِاخْتِيَارِيَّةِ بِالْقُدْرَةِ الْحَادِثَةِ فَقَطْ، مُبَاشَرَةً أَوْ تَوَلُّداً. وَمَذْهَبُ أَهْلِ السُّنَّةِ: وُجُودُ الْأَفْعَالِ كُلِّهَا بِالْقُدْرَةِ الْأَزَلِيَّةِ فَقَطْ، مَعَ مُقَارَنَةِ الْأَفْعَالِ الِاخْتِيَارِيَّةِ لِقُدْرَةٍ حَادِثَةٍ لَا تَأْثِيرَ لَهَا، لَا مُبَاشَرَةً وَلَا تَوَلُّداً.
The doctrine of the Jabariyyah says all actions exist through the eternal power alone, with no created power involved. The doctrine of the Qadariyyah says voluntary actions exist through the created power alone, whether directly or through a further effect. The doctrine of Ahl al-Sunnah says all actions exist through the eternal power alone, together with a created power belonging to the voluntary actions, a power with no effect at all, neither direct nor through a further effect.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the three positions in detail.

The word "only" is the hinge these three positions turn on. The Jabariyyah restrict existence to the eternal power only. The Qadariyyah restrict it to the created power only. Ahl al-Sunnah does not restrict action to just one of the two powers. They describe a relationship between them instead. The eternal power actually brings the action into existence. The created power is genuinely present and belongs to the agent, without being the cause of that existence.

Kasb and Legal Accountability

وَأَمَّا الْكَسْبُ: فَهُوَ عِبَارَةٌ عَنْ تَعَلُّقِ الْقُدْرَةِ الْحَادِثَةِ بِالْمَقْدُورِ فِي مَحَلِّهَا مِنْ غَيْرِ تَأْثِيرٍ.
Kasb is the connection of the created power to the object of power, within its locus, without that power having any effect.

Al-Sanusi, Muqaddimat al-Sanusi, on the definition of kasb.

Legal accountability is tied to kasb. This means the intention and the eagerness to act. It is not tied to whether the action actually materializes, which stays under Allah's decree. A person is judged on the internal choice they made. They are not judged simply on whether that choice happened to be realized in the outside world.

The Hadith of the Two Fighters. A hadith describes both the killer and the one killed as being in the Fire. The one who was killed had also been eager to kill his brother, even though he did not succeed. This shows the site of judgment is the internal choice and the eagerness to act. It is not the outward result. The person who was killed never actually killed anyone. Yet he receives the same judgment as the one who did.

(1)

A related question asks whether Allah can burden a soul beyond its capacity. The Ash'ari and Maturidi schools differ here. The Ash'ari position holds that this is rationally possible, though not something that actually happens. Prophet Ibrahim's command to sacrifice his son comes up in this context. It functioned as a test of submission rather than the actual execution of the act. This shows how a command can genuinely test obedience without the burdened act needing to be carried through to the end.

Free Will and Divine Decree

A natural question asks how free will makes sense if Allah has already destined an action. The answer separates two things that are easy to blur together. A human being genuinely has the choice to act. But turning that choice into an actual event requires Allah to create it. The will to do something and the ability to make it happen are not the same thing.

Everyday examples show this clearly. Someone may fully intend to play soccer, only to be stopped by rain. Someone may intend to stand up, only to be stopped by an earthquake. In both cases the intention and the choice were genuinely there. Making it happen was not guaranteed to be within the person's power. Human beings are limited and finite. Their domain of action is finite too. Allah's knowledge is infinite. It covers all of these limited choices without being limited by them.

This connects to tawfiq, or divine enablement. This is when a servant's sincere intention lines up with what Allah has actually designated to happen. Tawfiq often shows up when a person does a good deed and stays humble or quiet about it, rather than seeking recognition.

Iblis and Adam

The contrast between Adam and Iblis closes out this discussion of choice and accountability. Adam made a mistake and sought forgiveness. Iblis let pride stop him from submitting, even though he knew Allah was his Lord. The difference was not in what each one knew. It was in what each one chose to do with that knowledge.
