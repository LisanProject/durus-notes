Liban's Online Notes

Sharh al-Aqbawi شرح الأقبعاوي

These notes come from ongoing study of Imam al-Dardir's al-'Aqida al-Tawhidiyyah with Sidi Shaykh Muhammad al-Maliki, taken lesson by lesson and then broken down piece by piece here: term by term, proof by proof, so that each idea is built on the one before it rather than left as a wall of definitions to memorize.

A word on why this text, and why in this much detail. Al-Dardir's treatise belongs to the Ash'ari school, which is not a minority position or a modern innovation but the creed held, across the centuries, by the overwhelming majority of Sunni scholars — including most of the hadith masters, jurists, and theologians cited as authorities across all four madhahib. Names like al-Bayhaqi, al-Nawawi, Ibn Hajar al-'Asqalani, al-'Izz ibn 'Abd al-Salam, and countless others of that stature either wrote within this school or its close Maturidi counterpart. This is not a marginal footnote to Sunni orthodoxy; it very largely is Sunni orthodoxy, refined over more than a millennium of careful argument, counter-argument, and consensus.

In recent years, this school has been increasingly misrepresented online and in popular circles — sometimes dismissed outright as innovation, sometimes caricatured as "denying the attributes of Allah" or replacing revelation with Greek philosophy. Most of this comes from people who have not actually read the primary Ash'ari texts, engaged with the arguments on their own terms, or reckoned with the weight of scholarship standing behind them. The lessons distilled into these notes spend real time on exactly these objections — not to be defensive for its own sake, but because understanding why a doubt feels compelling, and then walking through why it does not hold up, is part of how this 'ilm is actually supposed to be learned. My hope is that these notes show something of the depth, precision, and internal coherence of the Ash'ari school on its own terms, rather than the flattened version it is sometimes reduced to by people arguing against a version of it that does not exist.

The chapters below are organized by topic rather than by the order any single lesson happened to cover them, since the lessons themselves circle back to review and deepen earlier material more than once. Each chapter ends with a set of review questions drawn from the actual lessons, meant to be worked through actively rather than just read past.

Chapters

Chapter 1: The Khutbah, the Basmala, and the Mukallaf – Why classical books open the way they do, what "Bismillah" actually means grammatically, and the five conditions that make a person legally and religiously responsible (mukallaf).

Chapter 2: Why Twenty Attributes? – How Allah's perfections can be infinite while only twenty are required knowledge for every believer, how the ninety-nine names relate back to a much smaller set of underlying attributes, and the difference between general (ijmali) and detailed (tafsili) belief.

Chapter 3: Existence, Pre-Eternity, and Everlastingness – Wujud, Qidam, and Baqa': what each means precisely, how they are proven rationally through the law of causality and the law of change, and why an infinite regress of causes is impossible.

Chapter 4: Unlike Creation, Dependent on Nothing – Mukhalafah lil-Hawadith and Qiyam bi-Nafsihi: why Allah cannot be a substance or an accident, why the language of "hand" and "descent" in scripture is handled the way it is, and what total self-sufficiency actually means.

Chapter 5: Divine Oneness in Essence, Attributes, and Acts – Wahdaniyyah worked through in full: why a composite god is impossible, why no created thing shares any divine attribute even in name, how Ash'ari occasionalism explains cause and effect, and how human moral responsibility survives all of it.

Chapter 6: The Three Rational Laws and the Proof of Life – Causality, congruity, and inseparability, and how these three laws are chained together step by step to prove, in order, Allah's existence, power, will, knowledge, and life.

Chapter 7: Life, Knowledge, Will, and Power – Hayah, 'Ilm, Iradah, and Qudrah in detail: the Seven Demands every attribute must satisfy, how "relevance" (ta'alluq) differs between them, and how divine knowledge relates to qadar without becoming its cause.

Chapter 8: Hearing, Sight, and Speech – Sam', Basar, and Kalam: what "unveiling" (inkishaf) means, and the crucial distinction between Allah's eternal, uncreated Speech (kalam nafsi) and the created recitation and writing of the Qur'an (kalam lafzi).

Chapter 9: Certainty, the Ahad Hadith, and Common Misconceptions – Why creed requires certainty and how a single-narrator hadith can or cannot supply it, and a direct look at the most common misreadings of the Ash'ari position on the divine attributes and the status of the Qur'an.

Chapter 10: The Prophets and the Unseen (Sam'iyyat) – The matn and translation for what must be affirmed of the Prophets and Messengers, and the sam'iyyat: the Last Day, the Bridge, the Scale, and the rest of what is known through revealed report.
