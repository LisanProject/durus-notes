Unlike Creation, Dependent on Nothing

Text
مخالف في ذاته لجميع الخلائق، فليس بجسم، ولا عَرَض، ولا يتصف بالمكان، ولا بالزمان، ولا باليمين، ولا بالشمال، ولا بالخلف، ولا بالأمام، القائم بنفسه
Translation
He is utterly unlike all of creation in His essence. He is not a body, nor an accident. He is not described by place, nor by time, nor by right, nor by left, nor by behind, nor by in front. He subsists by Himself.

Mukhalafah lil Hawadith: Dissimilarity to Created Things

Mukhalafah lil hawadith means Allah is unlike creation in His essence, His attributes, and His actions, without exception.

Classically this is put in a precise technical form: Allah is neither a substance (jawhar), something that exists independently, like a tree or a stone, nor an accident ('arad), a quality that exists only within some substance, like a color or a taste.

(1)

Everything in creation falls into one of these two categories, and both categories entail dependence: a substance can be composed of parts or occupy space, and an accident needs a substance to inhere in. Were Allah either, He would be contingent and would Himself require a cause, which contradicts what has already been established about Him in the previous chapter.

A modern way of putting the same point: the universe is built from matter and energy, both of which have mass, spatial extension, and physical states. Allah possesses none of these properties, since possessing any of them would place Him within the created order this attribute specifically rules Him out of.

The matn itself is explicit about what this excludes: Allah is not described by place, nor by time, nor by any of the six directions, whether right, left, behind, in front, above, or below. Every one of these is a property that belongs to bodies occupying space, and since Allah is neither a substance nor an accident, none of them can be predicated of Him. This is not merely a poetic way of saying Allah is "everywhere" or "beyond space" in some vague sense; it is a precise denial that spatial or temporal categories apply to Him at all, in any of their forms.

Shared Words, Unshared Meanings

Words like "hearing" and "seeing" are used of both Allah and His creation, which raises an obvious question: does this shared vocabulary imply a shared reality? The classical answer is no: this is a case of homonymy (ishtirak lafzi), where the same word is used for two things that are categorically different once applied to Allah versus applied to creation. Human hearing depends on ears and a mechanical process of sound waves reaching them; Allah's hearing has no such mechanism or organ, and its precise reality (kayfiyyah) is left entirely unknown to us. We affirm only that Allah hears everything that can be heard, without specifying how.

Three broad approaches to scripturally reported attributes like "hand" or "descent" follow from this same principle:

  • Ta'wil (interpretation): reading a term like "hand" as referring to "power" or "support."
  • Ithbat (affirmation): affirming the expression as a real attribute while denying any resemblance to the created trait it is named after, sharing only the word, never the meaning.
  • Tafwid (consignment): affirming that the term is true of Allah while suspending any claim to know its exact meaning or whether it belongs among the twenty classical attributes at all.

What all three approaches reject together is ta'til, emptying the attribute of any real meaning whatsoever. Denying that these terms refer to anything real about Allah is not a fourth option within the tradition; it is precisely the error the other three approaches are constructed to avoid. The tradition affirms real meaning while declining to specify the exact modality (kayfiyyah); that is the substance of the well known formula "affirmation without asking how" (ithbat bila kayf).

Qiyam bi Nafsihi: Absolute Self Sufficiency

Qiyam bi nafsihi means Allah is entirely self sufficient, needing nothing whatsoever. The proof follows directly from the categories already introduced: everything besides Allah is either a substance or an accident, and both require something else: a substance needs no maker in the sense of a locus, but it is contingent and requires a cause for its existence, while an accident specifically requires a substance to exist within. Allah is neither, and is therefore uniquely independent of any locus and any cause. Even the wealthiest human being remains dependent in countless ways, whether on food, on health, or on other people; Allah's independence is total across every one of these dimensions at once.

Example 1A verse describes Allah as having a "hand." Briefly explain the three classical approaches (ta'wil, ithbat, tafwid) a scholar might take toward this expression, and what all three reject together.

Ta'wil would interpret "hand" as referring to power or support. Ithbat would affirm "hand" as a real attribute while denying any resemblance to a created hand, sharing only the word. Tafwid would affirm the term is true of Allah while suspending any claim to know its exact meaning. What all three reject together is ta'til, treating the expression as empty of any real meaning at all; that option is not on the table in any of the three approaches.

Example 2Why can Allah not be described as existing "above" or "below," "in front of" or "behind" anything?

All of these are spatial categories that only apply to bodies occupying space. Allah is neither a substance (jawhar) nor an accident ('arad), the only two categories anything in creation falls into, so none of the properties that depend on being a body, including location relative to a direction, can be predicated of Him without placing Him within the created order this attribute specifically rules Him out of.
