The Khutbah, the Basmala, and the Mukallaf

Text
بِسْمِ الرَّحْمٰنِ الرَّحِيْمِ
يجب على المكلَّف معرفة ما يجب لله تعالى ولأنبيائه وملائكته الكرام، فيجب لله تعالى عشرون صفةً
Translation
In the name of Allah Most Merciful and Compassionate. It is obligatory upon every responsible person to know what is necessarily true of Allah Most High, of His prophets, and of His noble angels. There are twenty attributes necessarily true of Allah Most High.
The Opening of a Classical Book

Classical Islamic works do not simply begin with the first substantive line of their subject. They open with a khutbah — an introductory address that typically includes the Basmala, the Hamdalah (praise of Allah), sending prayers upon the Prophet ﷺ, an introduction of the author and the book itself, and a supplication. This is not decorative throat-clearing. A narration states that any important matter not begun with the Basmala is "cut off," meaning it is deprived of full blessing. The manner in which a student enters a subject shapes what they receive from it, which is why Imam al-Dardir, like nearly every author before him, opens this way.

Al-Dardir frames the entire purpose of his treatise in a single sentence near the start: the obligation upon every responsible person is to know what must be affirmed for Allah, for His Messengers, and for His noble Angels. Everything that follows in the text, and in these notes, unpacks that one line.

The Basmala: What "Bismillah" Actually Means

A phrase built from a preposition and a noun — "bism," in the name of — is grammatically incomplete on its own; it requires a verb or noun, stated or omitted, to attach to. In "Bismillahi," the omitted element is understood to be asta'in, "I seek help," so the full sense is: I seek Allah's help in this writing, or in whatever action follows. The letter ba' here carries the meaning of instrumentality or seeking assistance, the same function it has in a sentence like "I wrote with the pen."

A further distinction matters here: a "name" can be treated as merely a word, or as standing for the thing itself — the named (musamma). In "Bismillah," the intention is the second sense: the speaker seeks help from the divine reality Allah refers to, not from the four letters of the word. This is a small grammatical point with real theological weight, since it is part of how later objections about "worshipping a name" versus "worshipping the named" get resolved.

Both Ar-Rahman and Ar-Raheem point to Allah as the Bestower of blessings, but not identically. Ar-Rahman refers to Him as the Giver of the most fundamental blessings — faith, well-being, intellect — while Ar-Raheem refers to the blessings that branch out from those, such as increases in provision or success in good deeds. A useful way to hold the distinction: core faith itself is an essential blessing (Rahman); growth in the strength of that faith is a derived one (Raheem).

A natural question follows: why did al-Dardir not open with "Alhamdulillah," as many classical works do, before moving to his own text? The answer given in the lesson is that hamd (praise) means praising something by naming its praiseworthy qualities, and the Basmala itself already does this — describing Allah as Ar-Rahman and Ar-Raheem is itself an act of praise. Having already praised Allah through the Basmala, a separate Hamdalah becomes unnecessary for brevity.

"Obligation Upon the Morally Responsible"

The word obligation (wujub) in this context is a religious, legal obligation (shar'i) according to the Sunni majority position, though there is a classical debate over whether knowledge of Allah is obligatory by reason ('aql) alone or strictly by revelation (shar'). Those arguing for a rational basis point out that someone who is not yet a believer cannot coherently be addressed by revealed law in the first place — the law presupposes belief, so something prior to it must establish the obligation to believe.

A person is only held to this obligation once they qualify as mukallaf, legally and religiously responsible. Although the matn text itself only names three conditions explicitly, the fuller classical treatment lists five:

  1. Having reached puberty (baligh)
  2. Possessing sound intellect ('aqil)
  3. Having received the divine message
  4. Possessing at least one functioning sense, hearing or sight
  5. Being actually able to reflect and consider what has been received

The fourth condition rules out someone who is both deaf and blind, since such a person has no channel by which the message could reach them at all. The fifth is distinct from merely hearing the message: someone who heard it but died before having any real opportunity to think it over and decide is not held responsible for failing to believe. Neglecting this knowledge once all five conditions are met is what carries religious consequence.

The Knowledge Required, and the Question of Taqlid

Not every kind of belief satisfies what sacred law asks for. The knowledge required must meet three conditions at once: it must be certain (yaqini) rather than speculative, it must correspond to reality, and it must be supported by evidence. The standard example of evidence for Allah's existence is reasoning from creation to a Creator — in the same way a functioning phone implies a maker, the order and composition of the created world implies One who brought it about.

This raises the case of the muqallid, someone who accepts the truths of the religion without personally examining any evidence for them, purely on the authority of others. Three positions on this person's faith are recorded; the position adopted here is that their faith is valid, but they are sinful for failing to reflect on the evidence available to them, since some minimal reflection is itself asked of every believer.

A further distinction separates summary evidence (dalil ijmali) from detailed evidence (dalil tafsili). Summary evidence is whatever is enough to convince the individual believer personally, even if they could not elaborate on it or defend it against a real objection. Detailed, professional argumentation is the ability to actually present a case and respond to challenges to it. Holding some summary evidence is an individual obligation on every single believer; mastering detailed argumentation is a communal obligation, something the Muslim community as a whole must have some of, though it can become an individual obligation on a given person if no one else nearby is capable of it. Given how widespread religious doubts and hostile arguments have become through modern communication, the need for people capable of this detailed defense, and of safeguarding the beliefs of ordinary Muslims, has only grown.
