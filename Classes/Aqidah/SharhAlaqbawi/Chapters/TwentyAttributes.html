Why Twenty Attributes?

Text
وهي: الوجود، والقِدَم، والبقاء، والمخالفة للحوادث، والقيام بالنفس، والوحدانية، والحياة، والعلم، والإرادة، والقدرة، والسمع، والبصر، والكلام. وكونه تعالى: حياً، ومريداً، وعليماً، وقادراً، وسميعاً، وبصيراً، ومتكلماً. فهذه عشرون صفة: الأولى نفسية، والخمسة بعدها سلبية، والسبعة بعدها صفات معانٍ، والتي بعدها معنوية.
Translation
They are: Existence, Beginninglessness, Endless Permanence, Dissimilarity to Created Things, Self Subsistence, Oneness, Life, Knowledge, Will, Power, Hearing, Sight, and Speech. And that He Most High is: Living, Willing, Knowing, Powerful, Hearing, Seeing, and Speaking. These are twenty attributes. The first is the attribute of essence. The five that follow it are attributes of negation. The seven after those are attributes of meaning. And those that follow are the attributes derived from meaning.

Infinite Perfection, Finite Required Knowledge

Allah's perfections (kamalat) are without limit — there is no finite list that could exhaust everything praiseworthy that is true of Him. This is not a loophole the Ash'ari school quietly works around; it is the starting premise. What the tradition asks of every believer is not an exhaustive inventory of every divine perfection, but a specific, rationally and textually demonstrable set of twenty attributes that every mukallaf must know in detail.

This produces a two tier structure of belief. General belief (ijmal) covers every perfection Allah possesses, known or unknown to us in its specifics. Detailed belief (tafsil) covers the twenty attributes specifically proven by revelation and sound reasoning, which every believer is expected to know by name and be able to explain. The standard analogy is belief in the prophets: Muslims are obligated to believe in every prophet Allah ever sent, in general, while being obligated to know only the twenty five named in the Qur'an in detail. Nobody treats the general obligation as satisfied merely by naming the twenty five, and nobody treats the unnamed prophets as somehow less real for being unnamed. The twenty attributes work the same way relative to Allah's limitless perfection.

A Common Misconception

A frequent accusation is that the Ash'ari school teaches Allah has "only twenty attributes," as though the tradition were artificially capping the divine. This is a misreading. Every perfection is affirmed in general; the twenty are simply the ones proven in enough detail, by revelation and reason together, to be required knowledge. Nothing in the framework denies or limits what Allah is in Himself — it describes what a finite human mind is obligated, and able, to know about Him with precision.

The Ninety Nine Names Are Not Ninety Nine Attributes

The hadith mentioning Allah's ninety nine names is sometimes read as implying ninety nine distinct attributes, which is not how the classical tradition understands it. Many names trace back to the same underlying attribute. العَلِيم (the All Knowing), العَلَّام (the Ever Knowing), and العَالِم (the Knower) all return to the single attribute of knowledge ('ilm). Similarly, القَدِير (the All Powerful), القَوِيّ (the Strong), and المَتِين (the Firm) all trace back to power (qudrah). In the same way, names like "Creator" (al Khaliq), "Provider" (al Raziq), and "Giver of Death" (al Mumit) each describe a different application of that same power to a different effect, not three separate powers.

So the number of names vastly outstrips the number of essential attributes they collectively point to. Every one of the twenty attributes underlies numerous divine names; the reverse is not true.

The Four Categories the Matn Actually Names

Al Dardir's own text divides the twenty into four categories, not two, and it is worth being precise about all four rather than collapsing them into a simple "negative versus positive" split.

1. The Attribute of Essence (al nafsiyyah) — a single attribute, Wujud (Existence). It stands apart from the rest because it is not really a quality Allah's essence has so much as the fact of that essence's reality itself; every other attribute presupposes it.

2. The Negating Attributes (al salbiyyah) — five in number: Qidam, Baqa', Mukhalafah lil Hawadith, Qiyam bi Nafsihi, and Wahdaniyyah. Each of these rules out a specific kind of imperfection (a beginning, an end, resemblance to creation, dependency, or multiplicity) rather than asserting a positive quality on its own. Chapters 3 through 5 of these notes work through these five in detail.

3. The Attributes of Meaning (sifat al ma'ani) — seven: Hayah, 'Ilm, Iradah, Qudrah, Sam', Basar, and Kalam. These are real, positive qualities genuinely subsisting in Allah's essence. Chapters 7 and 8 of these notes cover this set.

4. The Attributes Derived From Meaning (al ma'nawiyyah) — also seven, and easy to miss if you only skim the list: Being Living (hayyan), Willing (muridan), Knowing ('aliman), Powerful (qadiran), Hearing (sami'an), Seeing (basiran), and Speaking (mutakalliman). These are not a fifth independent set of realities alongside the seven ma'ani — each ma'nawi attribute is simply the state or description that necessarily follows from its corresponding ma'na attribute. Allah is described as "Knowing" (a ma'nawi state) precisely because the attribute of Knowledge (a ma'na) subsists in Him; the two are distinguished conceptually, as the quality and the description that quality entails, without being two separate additional realities to prove.

Add these up and the count is exact: 1 (nafsiyyah) + 5 (salbiyyah) + 7 (ma'ani) + 7 (ma'nawiyyah) = 20.

The Structure of the Forty One

Alongside these twenty stand their twenty logical opposites, which are impossible (mustahil) for Allah, plus one further attribute that is merely possible (ja'iz) for Him — that He may or may not do a given contingent act, such as creating one particular thing rather than another. Twenty obligatory, twenty impossible, and one possible make forty one together, and the declaration لَا إِلَٰهَ إِلَّا اللَّه is understood, in this tradition, to imply belief in all forty one together with their proofs.

This treats "detailed knowledge required" as though it meant "the sum total of what Allah is." Allah's perfections are limitless; the twenty are simply the ones proven in enough detail, by revelation and reason together, to be required knowledge of every believer. Every other perfection is still affirmed in general, the same way Muslims believe in every prophet Allah sent while only being required to know twenty five by name.

Example 2Explain why "Al-'Alim," "Al-'Allam," and "Al-'Alam" do not count as three separate attributes.

All three names trace back to the same single underlying attribute, knowledge ('ilm). They describe the same reality from slightly different angles of emphasis, the way "Strong," "Firm," and "All Powerful" all trace back to the single attribute of power. The number of divine names vastly outstrips the number of essential attributes they point to; a name is not automatically a new attribute.
