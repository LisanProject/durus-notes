Divine Oneness in Essence, Attributes, and Acts

Text
واحد في ذاته وصفاته وأفعاله
Translation
He is one in His essence, His attributes, and His acts.

Wahdaniyyah: Linguistic and Technical Meaning

Linguistically, wahdaniyyah means unifying or making something one, as in "unifying our word" (reaching agreement) or "unifying the ranks" in prayer.

Technically, in 'ilm al-'aqidah, wahdaniyyah means believing Allah is one in three distinct respects at once: His Essence (Dhat), His Attributes (Sifat), and His Actions (Af'al).

(1)

Each of the three needs its own argument, since each rules out a different kind of multiplicity.

Oneness of Essence: No Parts, No Peer

Tawhid al dhat means Allah is not composed of parts and has no equal. A composite being would depend on its own components in order to exist at all, in the same way a car needs every one of its parts to function, and ceases to function the moment a necessary part is missing. Anything that depends on its parts is contingent, not necessary, and so cannot be divine. There is a further argument from the attributes specifically: if Allah's attributes were somehow distributed across different parts, He would need those parts to be complete; if instead every part carried every attribute in full, each part would itself be a complete god, directly contradicting the very oneness being argued for. Qur'anic material reinforces the same conclusion: the story of the golden calf (Q7:148) and the discussion of Jesus and Mary (Q5:75) are both read as establishing that Allah is not a body and has none of the needs a created, composite thing would have. As for a peer: if some other entity fully matched Allah's essence, there would simply be two gods, which is incoherent given that necessary existence, by definition, cannot be shared.

Oneness of Attributes: Unique and Unshared

Tawhid al sifat means every divine attribute, such as knowledge, power, hearing, or sight, is single and absolute, with nothing like it anywhere in creation, even where the same word is used. Qur'an 42:11 states plainly, "There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the Hearing, the Seeing," the very same terms, Sami' and Basir, applied to Allah and, elsewhere, to human beings, while the underlying realities remain completely different. A common but rejected formulation claims "the meaning is the same, only the manner (kayfiyyah) differs." The classical Sunni position denies even this: human knowledge is acquired, limited, and sequential; Allah's knowledge is uncreated, unlimited, and simultaneous. It is not the same kind of thing differing only in degree or manner; it is categorically unlike it, while the word used to name it happens to be shared. If a multiplicity of independent powers or knowledges existed in Allah, some would turn out to be redundant and others deficient relative to each other, both of which are impossible for a perfect being; unity of attributes is therefore rationally required, not just textually reported.

Oneness of Acts, and How Ash'ari Theology Explains Causation

Tawhid al af'al is the most philosophically distinctive of the three: Allah alone is the true actor behind every event, and everything that looks like cause and effect in the created world is, in reality, Allah associating two created things together in time, not one of them genuinely producing the other by its own power. Fire does not burn by its own essence; Allah creates the burning at the moment fire touches fuel. Food does not satisfy hunger by itself; Allah creates the satisfaction when it is eaten. Flipping a switch does not itself create light; the entire power system behind it is needed, and the switch's motion is only the occasion, not the true cause. This is temporal conjunction, not intrinsic causation: the succession we observe between fire and burning, or medicine and healing, reflects Allah's consistent pattern of associating them, not any actual power residing in the fire or the medicine themselves.

This raises an obvious question about moral responsibility: if Allah is the true creator of every human act, good or evil, how is a person praised or blamed for what they do? The answer given is that a person is described by the quality of an act, whether obedient or sinful, because that act genuinely occurred in them, in the same way a wall is called green because green paint covers it, not because the builder who applied the paint is green. A further distinction narrows real accountability: involuntary processes like digestion or blood circulation are not subject to moral responsibility at all, since they are not chosen; only voluntary, freely chosen actions like speaking or eating are the basis for reward or punishment. Verses such as "Allah created you and what you do" (Q37:96) and "You did not throw when you threw, but Allah threw" (Q8:17) are read as direct scriptural statements of this same principle of sole divine agency operating through, not alongside, genuine human choice.

An Objection, and Its Answer

A common atheist or skeptical challenge runs: "If Allah willed me to disbelieve, why am I held accountable for it?" The analogy offered in response is a police sting operation: a person is given the opportunity to act and is caught doing so of their own choice; being permitted the opportunity to act does not remove the fact that the choice made within it was genuinely theirs. Allah merely allows the outcome of a person's own free choice to unfold, whether for the sake of testing or for reasons of wisdom not always apparent to us; He does not compel the choice itself. This is paired with an emphasis that Allah's mercy precedes and follows every offense, offering repeated openings for repentance, so that accountability is always tied to what was freely chosen, never to what was imposed.

A Critique of the Tripartite Division of Tawhid

A framework popularized by Ibn Taymiyyah, and associated today with Wahhabi/pseudo-Salafi thought, divides tawhid into three categories: Rububiyyah (Lordship), Uluhiyyah (Divinity), and Asma' wa Sifat (Names and Attributes). The classical critique of this division is that the categories overlap in ways the framework itself cannot cleanly separate, and that it lacks clear precedent in earlier linguistic or classical theological usage. In Arabic, "rabb al dar" (lord of the house) simply means its possessor, not necessarily its creator, so "Rabb" and "Ilah" both function, at bottom, as divine names, meaning they already belong under Names and Attributes rather than constituting independent categories of tawhid alongside it. Classical texts such as al-'Aqida al Tahawiyyah organize belief around essence, attributes, and acts, exactly the three part structure worked through in this chapter, rather than this later three way division.

Example 1Use the wall color analogy to explain how a human being can be truly called "sinful" for an act, even though Allah is its true creator.

A wall is called green because green paint genuinely covers it, not because whoever applied the paint is green. In the same way, a person is described as sinful because the sinful act genuinely occurred in them, even though Allah is the true creator of that act. The description attaches to where the act took place, not to who ultimately brought it about.

Example 2An atheist objects: "If Allah willed me to be a disbeliever, why am I still held accountable?" Answer this using the police sting analogy.

In a sting operation, a person is given an opportunity to act and is caught doing so of their own free choice; being given the opportunity does not mean the choice made within it was not genuinely theirs. In the same way, Allah allows the outcome of a person's own free choice to unfold without compelling the choice itself, so accountability remains tied to what was actually chosen rather than to what was merely permitted to occur.
