Existence, Pre-Eternity, and Everlastingness

Text
فهو سبحانه وتعالى: واجب الوجود، قديم باقٍ
Translation
He, Glorified and Exalted be He, is necessarily existent, beginningless, and endlessly permanent.
Wujud: Existence

The first attribute, wujud (existence), affirms simply that Allah exists. Classical theologians have actually debated whether existence should count as an attribute distinct from the essence at all: some argue that "existence" just names the very reality of a thing rather than describing some additional quality it has, so that the existent and its existence are not two separate things. Others treat it as an attribute in the ordinary sense, since we do meaningfully report of Allah that "He exists," the same way we would report any other true statement about Him. Either way, the content affirmed is the same.

Qidam: Pre-Eternity

Qidam can be understood in two different senses, and conflating them causes real confusion. Temporal pre-eternity just means something has existed for a long time — an old book is "qadim" in this loose sense, but it plainly had a beginning. Essential pre-eternity means something else entirely: existence with no beginning whatsoever, no initiation, no prior state of non-existence that it emerged from. Only Allah's existence is pre-eternal in this essential sense.

Baqa': Everlastingness

Baqa' is the mirror image of qidam, pointed toward the future rather than the past. Temporal everlastingness would just mean lasting a long time into the future; essential everlastingness means no end at all is even conceivable. A governing principle ties qidam and baqa' together: whatever is truly pre-eternal can never cease to exist, since ceasing to exist would itself be a kind of change, and a truly pre-eternal being's existence does not depend on anything that could introduce such a change.

Proving the Three Together

These three attributes are established by a small set of interlocking rational arguments.

The law of causality states that anything that exists and has a beginning cannot have simply come from nothing; it requires a cause. The law of change (dalil al-taghyir) adds a further point: any change implies a beginning, since whatever is changing must have started to exist at some point in order to be changing now. Modern cosmology's account of the universe having a beginning — often invoked here as a contemporary illustration — is treated as an example of this same principle applied to the cosmos as a whole, not as the foundation of the argument itself, which stands on the rational laws independently of any particular scientific theory.

Two further logical problems close off the only alternatives to a first, uncaused cause. An infinite regress of causes, each waiting on the one before it forever, would mean nothing ever actually gets caused at all — the classical illustration is a chain of signatures on a document, each requiring a prior signature: if the chain is genuinely infinite, no signature is ever actually made. Circular causality, something causing itself through a loop, is equally impossible — a grandfather cannot become his own grandchild. Since neither an infinite chain nor a closed loop of causes is coherent, there must be a first cause that itself has no cause: an uncaused, necessarily existing being. That being is what Ash'ari theology means by Allah's wujud, qidam, and baqa' taken together.

Review Questions
  1. What is the attribute of Existence (al-Wujud), and why do some theologians debate whether it counts as a distinct attribute at all?
  2. Define pre-eternity (al-Qidam) and everlastingness (al-Baqa'). How do these differ from simply lasting a long time?
  3. Explain the law of causality and give an example of it outside of theology.
  4. Explain the law of change (dalil al-taghyir) and how it strengthens the argument from causality.
  5. What logical problems arise from an infinite regress of causes? Use the signature analogy in your answer.
  6. Why is circular causality impossible, and what everyday analogy makes this clear?
  7. What does the principle "the truly pre-eternal can never cease to exist" actually establish, and why does it follow necessarily?
  8. How does contemporary cosmology (e.g. the Big Bang) relate to this argument, and why is it treated as illustration rather than foundation?
