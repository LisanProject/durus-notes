The Three Rational Laws and the Proof of Life

This chapter sets out the toolkit of rational laws classical Kalam uses to move from bare observation of the created world to a full list of Allah's core attributes, without relying on any premise a non Muslim interlocutor would not already grant.

The Three Rational Laws

1. The Law of Causality holds that every effect or change necessarily has a cause.

(1)

This is treated as a basic metaphysical axiom running through Ibn Sina, al Ghazali, and Fakhr al Din al Razi: if the universe was once in one state and is now in another, that change cannot have occurred without something external bringing it about.

2. The Law of Congruity (munasabah) holds that an effect must be proportionate to, and compatible with, its cause.

(2)

If you hear a phone ringing, you look for a phone, not a lion. The effect's character must match the capabilities of whatever is proposed as its cause. A person who has never studied Japanese cannot spontaneously produce a flawless Japanese poem, since the effect (the poem) must match the cause (the requisite knowledge and skill).

3. The Law of Inseparability (talazum) holds that if one thing is established, something else necessarily accompanies it, as a matter of logical entailment rather than direct causation.

(3)

If a room's light is on, someone switched it on; if you hear knocking, a knocker exists even if unseen.

Chaining the Laws Into a Single Proof

These three laws are applied in sequence to build up, one attribute at a time, the profile of the being responsible for the universe:

  1. Existence of a cause. By causality (1), the universe's existence and its changes demand an external cause, call it the Maker (mujid).
  2. Externality. By congruity (2), since the effect (the real, external universe) is genuinely external and real, its cause must likewise be a real, external thing, not a mere idea or abstraction.
  3. Possession of power. By congruity (2) again, the scale and order of the universe requires a cause capable of originating something of that magnitude, i.e. possessing power (qudrah).
  4. Will. By congruity (2) and inseparability (3) together, mere ability is not enough: a cause with power alone would either produce its effect necessarily, always, or never at all. Since the universe is one particular arrangement among countless possible ones, the cause must also have will (iradah), selecting one outcome among the possibilities its power could have produced.
  5. Knowledge. By inseparability (3), a cause that wills a particular outcome must first know what it is willing; will and power together entail knowledge ('ilm).
  6. Life. By inseparability (3) once more, only a living thing can possess ability, will, and knowledge at all, so life (hayah) is a necessary attribute of this first cause.

Why Start Here, Methodologically

The lessons emphasize that engaging seriously with skeptics or atheists benefits from working through this chain deliberately, existence, then power, then will, then knowledge, then life, each step following necessarily from what came before, rather than jumping straight to scripture, which a non Muslim interlocutor has no independent reason yet to accept. A caution repeated here by Sidi Shaykh Muhammad al-Maliki is that leaning on "scientific miracles" in the Qur'an as a primary line of defense is discouraged since rigorous rational argumentation of the kind outlined above is both more universal and less vulnerable to being undermined by future scientific revision.

Example 1A finely made watch is found on a beach. Walk through the law of congruity to explain what this implies about its cause.

The watch is intricate, ordered, and clearly the product of design and skill. By the law of congruity, an effect must be proportionate to and compatible with its cause, so whatever produced the watch must itself possess the relevant knowledge and capability, not be a random or unrelated process. Erosion or wave action, for instance, would not be a congruent cause for something this precisely constructed.

Example 2Explain, in order, why the rational chain proves existence, then power, then will, then knowledge, then life, and not some other order.

Causality first establishes that a cause must exist at all. Congruity then shows that cause must have power sufficient to produce so ordered an effect. Since power alone would either always or never produce its effect, congruity and inseparability together require will, to select one outcome among the possibilities power could produce. Inseparability then requires knowledge, since willing a particular outcome presupposes knowing what is being willed. Finally, only a living thing can possess power, will, and knowledge together, so life follows last, as what makes the other three possible in the first place.
