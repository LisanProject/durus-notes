Life, Knowledge, Will, and Power

Text
حي، عليم بكل شيء، وما كان، وما يكون، وما لم يكن. مريد لكل شيء وَرَدَ من العوالم، وما لم يكن منها. قادر على كل شيء من الممكنات وعلى إعدامها، لا يشاركه في ذلك موجود.
Translation
He is Living. He knows all things: what was, what will be, and what has never been. He wills everything that proceeds from the created realms and everything that has not come to be from them. He has power over all possible things and over their annihilation, and no existing thing shares in that with Him.
The Seven Demands

Every one of the affirmed attributes covered in this chapter and the next must satisfy the same set of requirements, referred to as the Seven Demands (al-matalib al-sab'ah): the attribute must (1) genuinely exist, (2) be real rather than metaphorical, (3) be pre-eternal, (4) be everlasting, (5) be unique, with no multiplicity within Allah, (6) be unmatched by anything in creation, and (7) be inseparable from Allah's essence and itself uncreated. Rather than re-deriving these seven demands for every attribute individually, each of the following sections simply notes anything distinctive about how that particular attribute relates to them.

Hayah: Life

Hayah is more than the mere absence of death. For Allah it means complete awareness and capacity, though its precise inner reality is not accessible to us — we know its effect (that Allah is aware, capable, willing) without knowing the "how" of it. Life is what renders its possessor capable of being described as knowing, willing, and able; without it, none of the following three attributes could be predicated of a thing at all, which is exactly why life was established first in the rational chain of the previous chapter.

A further conceptual distinction matters here: some things are real existents, with independent existence, like color; others are merely conceptual (i'tibari), existing only as a mental relationship, like "fatherhood" or a nationality. The Mu'tazila were reported to treat Allah's attributes as merely conceptual relationships rather than real qualities inhering in the essence; the Sunni position, including the Ash'aris, insists they are real.

Each attribute also has what is called its relevance (ta'alluq) — whatever else, beyond bare existence in the essence, that attribute requires in order to be meaningfully applied. Life is unusual in requiring none: it does not need some further object the way "knowing" needs something known. It is complete within the essence on its own, and in turn makes the other three attributes possible.

'Ilm: Knowledge

'Ilm is the pre-eternal attribute by which absolutely everything — past, present, and future — is disclosed to Allah with complete clarity, all at once, not as a process unfolding over time the way human knowing is. Its relevance (ta'alluq) is total: it covers every existent (mawjud), whether eternal (Allah and His own attributes) or created, and every non-existent (ma'dum) as well, whether impossible (like a partner for Allah) or merely possible but not yet actualized. An alternative classical breakdown covers the same ground through three categories: the necessary (wajib), the possible (ja'iz), and the impossible (mumtani') — Allah's knowledge encompasses all three fully and simultaneously.

The specific type of relation knowledge has to its objects is called unveiling (inkishaf): knowledge does not create or bring about the things it knows, it simply discloses them as they already are, the way knowing the answer to a math problem does not create that answer. This distinction resolves a recurring worry about predestination (qadar): Allah's knowledge is a record of what will occur, not the cause of it. Its being written in the Preserved Tablet is a reflection of Allah's prior knowledge, not an independent causal act forcing the outcome into being.

Iradah: Will

Iradah selects, among genuinely possible alternatives, which one becomes actual. Its relevance attaches only to what is possible (ja'iz); we do not say "Allah wills the possible and does not will the impossible" as though both were live options being weighed — rather, Allah's will simply does not relate to the impossible at all, since it is not a real alternative to begin with. The classical illustration lists six paired alternatives a thing's will can specify between: existence or non-existence, one attribute-pair or another (such as a particular size or color), a particular time, place, direction, and quantity. Iradah is the "chooser" among these; its relation to its object is one of specification, actively determining which possibility gets realized.

Qudrah: Power

Qudrah, like will, relates only to what is possible — never to what is logically necessary or logically impossible. Allah's inability to create a "square circle" is not a deficiency in power; a square circle is not a coherent possibility for power to act on in the first place, so the question misdescribes what power even is. Questions like "can Allah make one U.S. state bigger than the whole country" fail for the same reason: they propose an incoherence and then treat the failure to actualize it as a limitation. Qudrah is the attribute by which something is actually brought into or kept out of being, in line with what will has already selected; where will chooses, power actualizes.

Review Questions
  1. List and briefly explain the Seven Demands, applying them specifically to the attribute of Life.
  2. How is the difference between real (haqiqi) and conceptual (i'tibari) existence relevant to the debate over whether Allah's attributes are real?
  3. Define "relevance" (ta'alluq), and explain why Life has none while Knowledge, Will, and Power each do.
  4. Describe the three classical categories of being — necessary, possible, and impossible — and give an example of each.
  5. Explain "unveiling" (inkishaf) as the type of relation Knowledge has to what it knows, and why this matters for the debate over qadar.
  6. What does it mean that Will relates only to the possible, and what are the "six alternatives" it is said to specify among?
  7. Why can't Allah's power relate to a logical impossibility like a "square circle"? What does this reveal about what power actually is?
  8. Explain the difference between Will as "chooser" and Power as "actualizer."
  9. Why does the tradition insist Allah's attributes are real rather than merely conceptual relationships, against the Mu'tazili position?
