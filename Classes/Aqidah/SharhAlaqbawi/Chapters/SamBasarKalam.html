Hearing, Sight, and Speech

Text
سميع وبصير لكل موجود. متكلم بكلام أزلي منزه عن الصوت والحرف.
Translation
He hears and sees all that exists. He speaks with a speech that is eternally transcendent above sound and letter.
Sam': Hearing

Allah's attribute of hearing (as-sam') attaches only to actual existents (mawjudat), never to non-existents (ma'dumat) — Allah hears everything that exists, but there is nothing to "hear" in what does not exist. Existents themselves split into two kinds: eternal (Allah's own essence and attributes) and created (everything else). Allah's hearing covers both. The particular kind of relation this attribute has to its objects is called unveiling (ta'alluq inkishaf), the same term used for knowledge in the previous chapter: nothing is hidden from Allah, and His awareness of what is heard is total and immediate rather than built up through any process.

This hearing is real, not metaphorical, unlike an idiom such as "the walls have ears," which nobody takes as a literal claim about hearing. Like every affirmed attribute, hearing must satisfy the Seven Demands introduced in the previous chapter: eternity, everlastingness, uniqueness, independence from creation, subsistence in Allah's essence rather than anywhere in creation, literal (not metaphorical) reality, and freedom from any imperfection or limitation.

Basar: Sight

Everything said of hearing applies equally to sight (al-basar): it too attaches to all existents through an unveiling relation, and satisfies the same Seven Demands. What distinguishes sight from hearing is only the mode of unveiling — sight unveils through vision, hearing through the perception of sound, in the same way a sighted person perceives a car by seeing it while a blind person perceives the same car by hearing it, both arriving at a real awareness of the same object through a different channel. The exact difference between how knowledge, hearing, and sight each "unveil" their objects is acknowledged as something known fully only to Allah Himself; human language can name the distinction without being able to fully specify it.

The primary proof for both hearing and sight is revelation — the divine names As-Sami' and Al-Basir occurring throughout the Qur'an and Sunnah. A supplementary rational argument is also offered: the absence of hearing would imply deafness, and the absence of sight would imply blindness, both of which are deficiencies, and Allah is necessarily free of every deficiency. Revelation remains the primary basis; the rational argument is offered as reinforcement, not as the foundation these attributes actually rest on.

Kalam: Speech

Kalam (Speech) is closely tied to knowledge: knowledge uncovers facts to Allah Himself, while speech is how Allah makes what He knows known to others, in the same way a person's inner thoughts become accessible to others only once spoken. Divine Speech is eternal (azali), without beginning, and satisfies the same Seven Demands as every other affirmed attribute. Like hearing and sight, it is real and literal, not a figure of speech — unlike, say, the English idiom "history speaks to us," which nobody takes as a literal claim about history having speech.

The single most important distinction in this chapter is between two things both called "the Speech of Allah":

  • Kalam nafsi (essential Speech) — the eternal, uncreated attribute itself, which is not composed of letters, sounds, or sequence, and which has always been true of Allah.
  • Kalam lafzi (verbal speech) — the created expression of that attribute: the specific letters and sounds recited, and the ink and paper of a physical mushaf. These point to, and signify, the eternal attribute, without themselves being that attribute.

Qur'an 9:6, "until he hears the Words of Allah," is read in this light as referring to hearing the recitation — the created, verbal form — rather than direct contact with the eternal attribute itself. The essential Speech is uncreated; the recitation, the act of reciting, and the physical copy of the mushaf are all created, while nonetheless genuinely pointing to and conveying that uncreated attribute. Verbal speech unfolds through sequence, one letter and sound after another; essential Speech involves no such sequence or passage of time at all.

The case of Musa (peace be upon him) illustrates the distinction concretely: he "heard the ancient Speech," meaning Allah unveiled His eternal Speech to him directly, not through the ordinary human mechanism of letters and audible sound. Qur'an 42:51 supports this reading: "It is not possible for any human being that Allah should speak to him except by revelation, or from behind a veil, or by sending a messenger" — a verse that itself shows that divine speech reaching a human being need not occur through audible letters and sounds in the ordinary sense at all.

For Further Reflection
These go beyond simple recall — work through them as short written answers, not just mentally.
  1. Someone says: "If the Qur'an is uncreated, then the ink and paper of my mushaf must be uncreated too." Write a short reply distinguishing kalam nafsi from kalam lafzi that resolves this.
  2. Explain, in your own words, how Musa's hearing of Allah's Speech differed from how you or I hear a recitation of the Qur'an today, and why that difference matters for the doctrine of kalam nafsi.
  3. Write two or three sentences explaining why "unveiling" (inkishaf) is the same type of relation for knowledge, hearing, and sight, while still being three genuinely distinct attributes.
Review Questions
  1. Explain the difference between actual existents (mawjudat) and non-existents (ma'dumat), and how Allah's hearing relates to each.
  2. What does "unveiling attachment" (ta'alluq inkishaf) mean, and how does it apply to hearing, sight, and knowledge alike?
  3. Using the analogy of a sighted versus a blind person perceiving a car, explain how sight and hearing differ in their mode of unveiling despite both being real perception.
  4. Why is Divine Hearing said to be "real" rather than "metaphorical"? Give an example of a metaphorical use of "hearing" to contrast it with.
  5. What are the proofs offered for Allah's attributes of Hearing and Sight, and which is primary?
  6. Differentiate between "Essential Speech" (kalam nafsi) and "Verbal Speech" (kalam lafzi).
  7. Why are the recitation of the Qur'an and its physical written form considered created, while the attribute of Speech itself is not?
  8. How did Prophet Musa's experience of Allah's Speech differ from ordinary human hearing of created sound?
  9. What does Qur'an 42:51 establish about how divine speech can reach a human being?
  10. Why is it significant that verbal speech involves sequence while essential Speech does not?
