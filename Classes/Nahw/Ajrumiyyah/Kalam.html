Lesson I: Introduction to Grammar

The Basmala البسملة

Text
بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَنِ الرَّحِيمِ
Vocabulary
  • بِسْمِin the name of
  • اللَّهAllah
  • الرَّحْمَنthe Most Merciful; general mercy encompassing all creation
  • الرَّحِيمthe Most Compassionate; mercy specific to the believers
Translation
In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate.
Commentary

Sidi Shaykh Abdullahi begins every session with the Basmala because knowledge is sacred and not merely technical. The manner in which you enter study shapes what you receive from it. A narration states that every important matter not begun with the Basmala is cut off.

Sidi Shaykh Abdullahi explains that the legal status of the Basmala varies with context:

  • Obligatory in prayer at the opening of Surat al-Fatiha
  • Recommended before eating and before study
  • Permissible before a neutral act
  • Disliked in the bathroom
  • Forbidden before a sinful act

Grammatically, as explained by Sidi Shaykh Abdullahi, the Basmala is a prepositional phrase. The particle بِ attaches to اسم and requires a point of attachment (ta'alluq), i.e, a verb or verbal idea, stated or implied. Before eating the implied verb is "I eat." The verb is omitted for a reason of eloquence (balagha): leaving it unspecified makes the formula suitable for every action. Scholars differ on the meaning of بِ here. The majority hold it expresses seeking help (isti'ana). Others, including al-Zamakhshari, read it as accompaniment (musahaba) or involvement (mulabasa). All readings are grammatically valid.

Types of Speech أنواع الكلام

Text
الْكَلَامُ هُوَ اللَّفْظُ الْمُرَكَّبُ الْمُفِيدُ بِالْوَضْعِ
Vocabulary
  • الْكَلَامspeech; the technical term for a grammatically complete utterance
  • اللَّفْظutterance; any sound formed from the letters of Arabic
  • الْمُرَكَّبcomposed; consisting of at least two words
  • الْمُفِيدmeaningful; giving the listener a complete, satisfying idea
  • بِالْوَضْعaccording to established usage; belonging to actual Arabic and spoken with intent
Translation
Speech is a composed, meaningful utterance according to established usage.
Commentary

The definition has four conditions, each of which rules something out.

The first, utterance (lafz), rules out gestures and signs in the primary technical sense. Scholars note that Prophet Zakariyya, peace be upon him, communicated by sign and the Quran counts it as speech in a broader linguistic sense, but technically lafz requires sound formed from the Arabic letters.

The second, composed (murakkab), rules out single words: محمد alone is not speech in the technical sense.

The third, meaningful (mufid) rules out incomplete constructions. محمدٌ فِي الْمَسْجِدِ is mufid because the listener receives a complete idea. محمدٌ الَّذِي is not, because the listener waits for more.

Lastly, according to established usage (bil-wad') rules out two things: words spoken without intent such as speech in sleep or madness where intent is absent, and invented or nonsense sounds that do not belong to established Arabic.

The Three Divisions of the Arabic Word أقسام الكلمة

Text
وَأَقْسَامُهُ ثَلَاثَةٌ: اسْمٌ، وَفِعْلٌ، وَحَرْفٌ جَاءَ لِمَعْنًى
Vocabulary
  • أَقْسَامdivisions; plural of قِسْم
  • اسْمnoun; a word with meaning in itself, not bound to a time
  • فِعْلverb; a word with meaning in itself, bound to a time
  • حَرْفparticle; a word with meaning only in relation to others
  • جَاءَ لِمَعْنًىthat carries a meaning; distinguishing meaningful particles from mere letters
Translation
Its divisions are three: noun, verb, and particle that carries a meaning.
Commentary

Every word in Arabic is one of three things: a noun, a verb, or a particle. This division was arrived at by methodical study (istiqra') of the entire Arabic corpus and has no valid exception in classical usage.

A noun is a word with meaning in itself, not bound to a time. Examples: محمد, كتاب.

A verb is a word with meaning in itself, bound to a time. Examples: كَتَبَ (he wrote), يَكْتُبُ (he writes).

A particle is a word with meaning only in relation to others. Examples: فِي (in), مِنْ (from).

Text
فَالِاسْمُ يُعْرَفُ بِالْخَفْضِ وَالتَّنْوِينِ، وَدُخُولِ الْأَلِفِ وَاللَّامِ عَلَيْهِ، وَحُرُوفِ الْخَفْضِ، وَهِيَ مِنْ، وَإِلَى، وَعَنْ، وَعَلَى، وَفِي، وَرُبَّ، وَالْبَاءُ، وَالْكَافُ، وَاللَّامُ، وَحُرُوفُ الْقَسَمِ، وَهِيَ الْوَاوُ، وَالْبَاءُ، وَالتَّاءُ
Vocabulary
  • الْخَفْضthe genitive case; also called jarr; indicated by kasra or its substitute at the end of the noun
  • التَّنْوِينnunation; the doubled ending sound appearing as ً ٌ ٍ on indefinite nouns
  • الْأَلِف وَاللَّامthe definite article al; attaches only to nouns
  • حُرُوف الْخَفْضthe prepositions; particles that govern the genitive case
  • حُرُوف الْقَسَمthe letters of oath; a subset of the letters that attach only to nouns in oaths
Translation
The noun is identified by the genitive case, nunation, the entry of the definite article upon it, and the prepositions, which are: from, to, away from, upon, in, perhaps, the ba, the kaf, and the lam, and the letters of oath, which are the waw, the ba, and the ta.
Commentary

The noun is identified by accepting any one of four things: the genitive case, nunation, the definite article, or the prepositions upon it.

The genitive case (khafd) means the noun can take a kasra or its substitute at its end when governed by a preposition or by another noun in annexation (idafa). The ability to take this case marker is exclusive to nouns.

Nunation (tanwin) is the doubled ending sound that appears on indefinite nouns. It takes three forms depending on the case of the noun: dammatan in the nominative (rafa), fathatan in the accusative (nasb), and kasratan in the genitive (khafd). Its presence on a word confirms that word is a noun.

The definite article ال attaches only to nouns and is therefore a positive identifier. When ال enters upon a word, that word is a noun.

The prepositions (huruf al-khafd) govern only nouns and so their presence identifies the word that follows them as a noun. Ibn Ajurrum lists nine: مِنْ (from), إِلَى (to), عَنْ (away from), عَلَى (upon), فِي (in), رُبَّ (perhaps), ب (the ba), ك (the kaf), and ل (the lam).

Also included are the letters of oath (huruf al-qasam): و (the waw), ب (the ba), and ت (the ta). These are included here because they can only attach to nouns, making them additional identifiers of the noun even though they are not prepositions in the primary sense.

Text
وَالْفِعْلُ يُعْرَفُ بِقَدْ، وَالسِّينِ، وَسَوْفَ، وَتَاءِ التَّأْنِيثِ السَّاكِنَةِ. وَالْحَرْفُ مَا لَا يَصْلُحُ مَعَهُ دَلِيلُ الِاسْمِ وَلَا دَلِيلُ الْفِعْلِ
Vocabulary
  • قَدْthe particle qad; attaches only to verbs; with the past tense it means "indeed" or confirms the action occurred; with the present tense it expresses expectation or possibility
  • السِّينthe sin; a single letter prefix indicating the near future, e.g. سَيَكْتُبُ (he will write, soon)
  • سَوْفَsawfa; a separate word indicating the more distant future, e.g. سَوْفَ يَكْتُبُ (he will write, eventually)
  • تَاء التَّأْنِيث السَّاكِنَةthe silent feminine ta; the sukun-bearing ta that attaches to the end of a past tense verb to mark a feminine subject, e.g. كَتَبَتْ (she wrote); called silent because it carries a sukun, not a vowel
  • دَلِيلsign; identifier; the marker that confirms a word belongs to a given category
  • يَصْلُحis compatible; fits; is appropriate for
Translation
The verb is identified by qad, the sin, sawfa, and the silent feminine ta. The particle is that with which neither the sign of the noun nor the sign of the verb is compatible.
Commentary

The verb is identified by accepting any of four markers. None of these can attach to a noun or a particle, so their presence on a word confirms it is a verb.

Qad (قَدْ) is a particle that attaches only to verbs. With a past tense verb it conveys confirmation, قَدْ كَتَبَ means "he has indeed written." With a present tense verb it conveys expectation or possibility, قَدْ يَكْتُبُ means he may write. Because qad can only attach to verbs it serves as a clear identifier.

The sin (س) and sawfa (سَوْفَ) are both future markers and attach only to present tense verbs. The sin indicates the near future and sawfa the more distant future. Both are exclusive to verbs and so confirm a word's verbal identity when present.

The silent feminine ta (تَاء التَّأْنِيث السَّاكِنَة) attaches to the end of past tense verbs to indicate a feminine subject. It is called silent because it carries a sukun rather than a vowel, كَتَبَتْ (she wrote). This ta is distinct from the ta of the second person masculine, كَتَبْتَ (you wrote) which carries a fatha and is not a sign of the verb in this sense.

The particle has no positive signs of its own. It is identified purely by exclusion: if a word accepts neither the signs of the noun nor the signs of the verb, it is a particle. Ibn Ajurrum does not list signs for the particle because there are none to list. The particle is what remains after the noun and verb have been accounted for.
