Kalam

الْكَلاَمُ هُوَ اللَّفْظُ الْمُرَكَّبُ الْمُفِيدُ بِالْوَضْعِ
Kalam is composite speech that conveys a complete meaning, by convention.

Vocabulary

  • اللفظan utterance, a sound produced by the speech organs
  • المركبcomposite, made up of more than one word
  • المفيدconveying a complete, self standing meaning
  • بالوضعby convention, meaning the Arabic language convention specifically

Commentary

Ibn Ajurrum opens with a definition rather than examples, and the definition does four things at once. It tells you kalam has to be an utterance, so writing or gesture on its own does not count. It has to be composite, meaning at least two words. It has to give a complete meaning, so that the listener is not left waiting for more. And it has to follow the convention of the Arabic language specifically, which is why the definition includes "bi-l-wad'" at the end.

This last condition is the one students usually miss on a first read. It is what excludes a meaningful sentence in a language other than Arabic from counting as kalam in the sense this text is using the word. The scholars after ibn Ajurrum debated whether this fourth condition was even necessary, since some considered it already contained in what came before it, but the standard teaching keeps it as a separate, fourth condition.

قَامَ زَيْدٌ ("Zayd stood") is composite, made of two words, and gives a complete meaning, so it satisfies all four conditions and counts as kalam. A single word like زَيْدٌ on its own does not, since it fails the composite condition.
