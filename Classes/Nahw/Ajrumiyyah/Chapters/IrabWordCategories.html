I'rab, Definition and the Word Categories

Introduction to the Eight Word Categories مقدمة في أقسام الكلمة الثمانية

Before the signs of each case are detailed, Sidi Shaykh Abdullahi says it helps to know the eight word categories that have their own dedicated signs. These are the categories that behave differently from the basic singular noun, and they come up repeatedly in everything that follows.

The singular noun (ism mufrad) is any noun that refers to one thing and is not dual, plural, or one of the Five Nouns.
For example زَيْدٌ، كِتَابٌ

The broken plural (jamu taksir) is a plural formed by altering the internal structure of the word rather than adding an ending.
For example كُتُبٌ (books), رِجَالٌ (men)

The sound feminine plural (jamu muannath salim) is a plural formed by adding ەَاتٌ to a noun.
For example مُسْلِمَاتٌ، سَمَاوَاتٌ

The sound masculine plural (jamu mudhakkar salim) is a plural formed by adding ـُونَ in the nominative (rafa) or ـِينَ in the accusative (nasb) and genitive (khafd).
For example مُسْلِمُونَ، مُسْلِمِينَ

The dual (muthanna) is formed by adding ـَانِ in the nominative (rafa) or ـَيْنِ in the accusative (nasb) and genitive (khafd).
For example رَجُلَانِ، كِتَابَانِ

The Five Nouns (al-asma al-khamsa) are أَبُوكَ (your father), أَخُوكَ (your brother), حَمُوكَ (your father-in-law), فُوكَ (your mouth), and ذُو مَالٍ (one possessing wealth). They take letters rather than vowels as their case signs and must be in annexation (idafa) to do so.

The Five Verbs (al-afal al-khamsa) are present tense verbs with a dual, plural, or feminine second-person pronoun attached.
For example يَفْعَلَانِ، تَفْعَلَانِ، يَفْعَلُونَ، تَفْعَلُونَ، تَفْعَلِينَ
Their case is marked by the presence or absence of the nun rather than by a vowel.

The present tense verb with nothing attached (fil mudari lam yattasil bi-akhirihi shay) is a verb in its base form with no pronoun suffix.
For example يَكْتُبُ، يَذْهَبُ
It takes the standard vowel signs for rafa and nasb and drops the nun for jazm only when it is one of the Five Verbs.

Bab al-I'rab باب الإعراب

Text
اَلْإِعْرَابُ هُوَ تَغيِيرُ أَوَاخِرِ اَلْكَلِمِ لِاخْتِلَافِ اَلْعَوَامِلِ اَلدَّاخِلَةِ عَلَيْهَا لَفْظًا أَوْ تَقْدِيرًا. وَأَقْسَامُهُ أَرْبَعَةٌ: رَفْعٌ، وَنَصْبٌ، وَخَفْضٌ، وَجَزْمٌ، فَلِلْأَسْمَاءِ مِنْ ذَلِكَ اَلرَّفْعُ، وَالنَّصْبُ، وَالْخَفْضُ، وَلَا جَزْمَ فِيهَا، وَلِلْأَفْعَالِ مِنْ ذَلِكَ اَلرَّفْعُ، وَالنَّصْبُ، وَالْجَزْمُ، وَلَا خَفْضَ فِيهَا.
Vocabulary
  • اَلْإِعْرَابi'rab. The technical term for the change that occurs at the ends of words due to governing factors
  • تَغيِيرchange. Alteration
  • أَوَاخِرends. The final letters of words. Singular آخِر
  • اَلْكَلِمwords. Plural of كَلِمَة
  • اَلْعَوَامِلgoverning factors. The elements that cause case changes. Singular عَامِل
  • لَفْظًاpronounced. Audibly present
  • تَقْدِيرًاestimated. Implied but not audibly present
  • رَفْعrafa. The nominative case
  • نَصْبnasb. The accusative case
  • خَفْضkhafd. The genitive case
  • جَزْمjazm. The jussive case, exclusive to verbs
Translation
I'rab is the change that occurs at the ends of words due to the different governing factors that enter upon them, whether that change is pronounced or only estimated. Its divisions are four. They are rafa, nasb, khafd, and jazm. Nouns take from these rafa, nasb, and khafd, and there is no jazm for them. Verbs take from these rafa, nasb, and jazm, and there is no khafd for them.
Commentary

Al-I'rab is the change that occurs at the ends of words due to the different governing factors (awamil) that enter upon them, whether that change is pronounced or only estimated. Its four states are rafa (nominative), nasb (accusative), khafd or jarr (genitive), and jazm (jussive). Nouns take the first three. Verbs take the first, second, and fourth. Nouns do not take jazm. Verbs do not take khafd.

(1)

The commentary's own illustration of this is worth walking through slowly, since it shows the whole point of i'rab in a single name carried across three sentences.

Example 1State whether the underlined name or verb is marfu', mansub, or majzum, and explain why.حَضَرَ مُحَمَّدٌ

"Muhammad arrived." Work through it in steps.

Step 1. Identify the governing factor (the 'amil). Here it is the verb حَضَرَ.
Step 2. Determine what case that governing factor requires. A verb followed by its fa'il (the doer of the action) always requires that fa'il to be marfu'.
Step 3. Check the ending of مُحَمَّدٌ against that requirement. It carries a damma with tanwin, exactly the sign of raf' for a singular noun.

Conclusion. مُحَمَّدٌ is marfu', because it is the fa'il of حَضَرَ, and a fa'il is always marfu'.

Example 2State whether the underlined name or verb is marfu', mansub, or majzum, and explain why.رَأَيْتُ مُحَمَّدًا

"I saw Muhammad."

Step 1. The name مُحَمَّد itself is unchanged from Example 1. It is the same word, with the same root letters.
Step 2. What changed is the governing factor. Here it is the verb رَأَيْتُ, and مُحَمَّدًا is now its maf'ul bihi (object), not its fa'il.
Step 3. A maf'ul bihi requires nasb, so the ending shifts from damma to fatha (with tanwin). مُحَمَّدًا.

Conclusion. The word's case, not its identity, has changed. This is the entire point of i'rab. The same noun takes different endings depending only on what governs it.

Example 3State whether the underlined name or verb is marfu', mansub, or majzum, and explain why.حَظِيتُ بِمُحَمَّدٍ

"I was fortunate to have Muhammad."

Step 1. Identify what precedes مُحَمَّد this time. The preposition بِ.
Step 2. Prepositions are governing factors that always require khafd (the genitive) on the noun that follows them.
Step 3. Check the ending. مُحَمَّدٍ now carries a kasra with tanwin, exactly khafd's sign.

Conclusion. مُحَمَّدٍ is majrur, governed by بِ. Notice that across all three examples the root letters م ح م د never change. Only the final vowel changes, which is exactly what i'rab means.

The commentary then shows the same logic applied to a verb.

Example 4State whether the underlined name or verb is marfu', mansub, or majzum, and explain why.يُسَافِرُ إِبْرَاهِيمُ

"Ibrahim travels."

Step 1. Look for any governing factor entering upon the verb يُسَافِرُ. There is none here. No particle of nasb (like لَنْ) or jazm (like لَمْ) precedes it.
Step 2. A present tense verb with no governing factor defaults to raf', its base case.
Step 3. Confirm the ending. يُسَافِرُ carries a damma, the sign of raf' for a present tense verb.

Conclusion. يُسَافِرُ is marfu', simply because nothing has entered to change it from its default state.

Example 5State whether the underlined name or verb is marfu', mansub, or majzum, and explain why.لَنْ يُسَافِرَ إِبْرَاهِيمُ

"Ibrahim will not travel."

Step 1. Compare to Example 4. It is the same verb and subject, but now لَنْ has been added before the verb.
Step 2. لَنْ is a nasib, a particle that governs the present tense verb into nasb.
Step 3. Check the ending. The damma of يُسَافِرُ has shifted to a fatha, يُسَافِرَ.

Conclusion. The verb is mansub because of لَنْ. As with the noun examples above, only the ending changed, not the verb's root letters.

Example 6State whether the underlined name or verb is marfu', mansub, or majzum, and explain why.لَمْ يُسَافِرْ إِبْرَاهِيمُ

"Ibrahim did not travel."

Step 1. Again the same verb and subject, but now لَمْ precedes it instead of لَنْ.
Step 2. لَمْ is a jazim, a particle that governs the present tense verb into jazm.
Step 3. Check the ending. The damma has dropped entirely, leaving a sukun, يُسَافِرْ.

Conclusion. The verb is majzum because of لَمْ. Across Examples 4 through 6, the same verb took three different endings (damma, fatha, sukun) purely because of three different governing factors. Raf' by default, nasb by لَنْ, and jazm by لَمْ.

Finally, the commentary distinguishes a change that is pronounced (lafzi) from one that is only estimated (taqdiri), which is why the matn's definition includes "whether pronounced or estimated" in the first place.

Example 7State whether the underlined name or verb is marfu', mansub, or majzum, and explain why.يَدْعُو الْفَتَى وَالْقَاضِي وَغُلَامِي

"The young man, the judge, and my servant call out."

Step 1. Identify the governing relationship for each of the three nouns. All three are fa'il of يَدْعُو, so all three should be marfu'.
Step 2. Try to locate a visible damma on each. الْفَتَى ends in alif maqsura, الْقَاضِي ends in ya, and غُلَامِي ends in a possessive ya, and none of these letters can physically carry a damma.
Step 3. Since the case cannot be pronounced, it is estimated (taqdiri) instead. The raf' is still grammatically present and governs the sentence's meaning, it simply has no audible or written mark.

Conclusion. All three nouns are marfu' as fa'il, but the raf' is taqdiri (estimated) rather than lafzi (pronounced), unlike مُحَمَّدٌ in Example 1, whose damma is fully visible.
