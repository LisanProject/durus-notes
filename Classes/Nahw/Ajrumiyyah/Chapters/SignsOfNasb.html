Signs of Nasb علامات النصب

Text
وَلِلنَّصْبِ خَمْسُ عَلَامَاتٍ: الْفَتْحَةُ، وَالْأَلِفُ، وَالْكَسْرَةُ، وَالْيَاءُ، وَحَذْفُ النُّونِ. فَأَمَّا الْفَتْحَةُ، فَتَكُونُ عَلَامَةً لِلنَّصْبِ فِي ثَلَاثَةِ مَوَاضِعَ: فِي الِاسْمِ الْمُفْرَدِ، وَجَمْعِ التَّكْسِيرِ، وَالْفِعْلِ الْمُضَارِعِ إِذَا دَخَلَ عَلَيْهِ نَاصِبٌ وَلَمْ يَتَّصِلْ بِآخِرِهِ شَيْءٌ. وَأَمَّا الْأَلِفُ، فَتَكُونُ عَلَامَةً لِلنَّصْبِ فِي الْأَسْمَاءِ الْخَمْسَةِ، نَحْوَ: رَأَيْتُ أَبَاكَ وَأَخَاكَ وَمَا أَشْبَهَ ذَلِكَ.
Vocabulary
  • الْفَتْحَةthe fatha; the primary sign of nasb; the short vowel a at the end of a word
  • الْكَسْرَةthe kasra; the short vowel i; used as the sign of nasb for the sound feminine plural
  • الْيَاءthe ya; the letter ي used as the sign of nasb for the dual and sound masculine plural
  • حَذْف النُّونdropping the nun; the removal of the nun as the sign of nasb and jazm for the Five Verbs
  • نَاصِبa nasb particle; a particle that governs the accusative case in a following verb
Translation
Nasb has five signs: the fatha, the alif, the kasra, the ya, and dropping the nun. The fatha is a sign of nasb in three places: the singular noun, the broken plural, and the present tense verb when a nasb particle enters upon it and nothing is attached to its end. The alif is a sign of nasb for the Five Nouns, such as: I saw your father and your brother and whatever resembles that.
Commentary

Nasb, the accusative case, marks direct objects among other roles, and covers five distinct signs rather than the four rafa has: one root sign, the fatha, and four subsidiary ones that substitute for it in particular categories.

1. Al-Fatha does most of the work, appearing on the singular noun, the broken plural, and the present tense verb once a nasb particle enters and nothing else is attached to its end. On the singular noun it is usually explicit, but becomes implicit whenever the noun ends in alif maqsura, since that letter cannot physically carry a visible vowel.

(1)

Let's take a look at some examples.

Example 1Identify the sign of nasb in this example and explain whether it is explicit or implicit.لَقِيتُ عَلِيًّا

"I met Ali."

Step 1: Identify the governing relationship: عَلِيًّا is the maf'ul bihi (object) of لَقِيتُ, which requires nasb.
Step 2: Identify the category of the word: عَلِيًّا is a singular noun, the first of the categories in this lesson that takes fatha as its sign of nasb.
Step 3: Check whether the ending can physically carry a fatha: the word ends in a regular consonant, so the vowel is fully pronounceable.

Conclusion: عَلِيًّا is mansub with a visible (explicit) fatha, since nothing about its ending blocks the vowel from being pronounced.

Example 2Identify the sign of nasb in this example and explain whether it is explicit or implicit.لَقِيتُ الْفَتَى

"I met the young man."

Step 1: Identify the governing relationship: الْفَتَى is again the maf'ul bihi of لَقِيتُ, requiring nasb, exactly as in Example 1.
Step 2: Identify the category: الْفَتَى is also a singular noun.
Step 3: This time check the ending closely: the word ends in alif maqsura (ى), a letter that cannot physically carry any vowel marking at all.

Conclusion: الْفَتَى is mansub, but with an implicit fatha: the case is grammatically present but has no visible mark, purely because of what letter the word happens to end in.

The broken plural follows the same pattern.

Example 3Identify the sign of nasb in this example and explain whether it is explicit or implicit.صَاحَبْتُ الرِّجَالَ

"I accompanied the men."

Step 1: Identify the governing relationship: الرِّجَالَ is the maf'ul bihi of صَاحَبْتُ, requiring nasb.
Step 2: Identify the category: الرِّجَالَ is a broken plural (jam' taksir), a category that also takes fatha as its sign, just like the singular noun.
Step 3: Check the ending: the word ends in a regular consonant, so the vowel can be fully pronounced.

Conclusion: الرِّجَالَ is mansub with a visible fatha. The broken plural does not get its own special nasb sign; it simply follows the singular noun's rule.

The Quran supplies two clean examples of the implicit case for a broken plural.

Example 4Identify the sign of nasb in this example and explain whether it is explicit or implicit.وَتَرَى النَّاسَ سُكَارَىٰ

"And you will see the people intoxicated" (al-Hajj 22:2).

Step 1: Identify the governing relationship: تَرَى is a verb of perception that takes two objects here, and سُكَارَىٰ is the second one, requiring nasb.
Step 2: Identify the category: سُكَارَىٰ is a broken plural, following the same fatha rule as Example 3.
Step 3: Check the ending: the word ends in alif maqsura, which cannot physically carry a vowel, exactly like الْفَتَى in Example 2.

Conclusion: سُكَارَىٰ is mansub, with an implicit fatha, because it is a broken plural ending in alif maqsura.

Example 5Identify the sign of nasb in this example and explain whether it is explicit or implicit.وَأَنكِحُوا الْأَيَامَىٰ مِنكُمْ

"And marry the unmarried among you" (al-Nur 24:32).

Step 1: Identify the governing relationship: الْأَيَامَىٰ is the maf'ul bihi of أَنكِحُوا, requiring nasb.
Step 2: Identify the category: الْأَيَامَىٰ is a broken plural, the same category as سُكَارَىٰ in Example 4.
Step 3: Check the ending: it too ends in alif maqsura, blocking any visible vowel.

Conclusion: الْأَيَامَىٰ is mansub with an implicit fatha, for exactly the same reason as سُكَارَىٰ above. The Quran gives two clean parallel examples of this same pattern.

On the present tense verb, the same explicit/implicit split applies.

Example 6Identify the sign of nasb in this example and explain whether it is explicit or implicit.لَن نَّبْرَحَ عَلَيْهِ عَاكِفِينَ

"We will never cease being devoted to it" (Taha 20:91).

Step 1: Identify the governing factor: لَنْ precedes the present tense verb نَّبْرَحَ, and لَنْ always governs a following mudari' into nasb.
Step 2: This moves us from the noun categories (Examples 1-5) to the fourth category: the present tense verb.
Step 3: Check the ending: nothing is attached to نَّبْرَحَ, meaning no alif of duality, no waw of a masculine plural subject, and no confirming nun, so the fatha can be pronounced normally.

Conclusion: نَّبْرَحَ is mansub with a visible fatha, since it is a bare mudari' verb governed by لَنْ with nothing attached to its end.

Example 7Identify the sign of nasb in this example and explain whether it is explicit or implicit.يُرْضِينِي أَنْ تَسْعَى إِلَى الْمَجْدِ

"It pleases me that you strive toward glory."

Step 1: Identify the governing factor: أَنْ precedes the present tense verb تَسْعَى, and like لَنْ in Example 6, it governs the mudari' into nasb.
Step 2: Confirm the verb category is the same as Example 6: a present tense verb with a nasib entering upon it.
Step 3: Check the ending: تَسْعَى ends in alif maqsura, the same letter that blocked a visible vowel back in Examples 2, 4, and 5.

Conclusion: تَسْعَى is mansub, with an implicit fatha. The mudari' verb follows the exact same "alif maqsura blocks the vowel" logic as the nouns did, even though it belongs to a completely different category.

2. Al-Alif is the sign of nasb for the Five Nouns, which simply swap letters depending on the case: waw for rafa, alif for nasb. رَأَيْتُ أَبَاكَ، رَأَيْتُ أَخَاكَ follow the same annexation requirement noted in the previous section.

(2)

3. Al-Kasra is the sign of nasb for the sound feminine plural, which breaks the pattern slightly, taking kasra instead of the fatha you would otherwise expect, purely for ease of pronunciation: رَأَيْتُ الْمُسْلِمَاتِ، رَأَيْتُ السَّمَاوَاتِ.

(3)

4. Al-Ya covers two forms at once, the dual and the sound masculine plural, both of which used alif or waw for rafa and now converge on the same letter for nasb and khafd alike: رَأَيْتُ رَجُلَيْنِ، رَأَيْتُ الْمُسْلِمِينَ.

(4)

5. Hadhf al-Nun (Dropping the Nun) marks nasb for the Five Verbs, which are marked by what disappears rather than what appears. If the verb has the alif of duality, the waw of a masculine plural subject, or the ya of feminine address attached to its end, fatha cannot appear at all, and the sign shifts to dropping the nun instead: لَنْ يَضْرِبَا، لَنْ تَضْرِبُوا، لَنْ تَضْرِبِي, exactly as it does for jazm, لَنْ يَفْعَلَا، لَمْ يَفْعَلُوا, so the nominative is the only state in which these verbs keep it. If instead a confirming nun (heavy or light) is attached, the verb becomes fixed on fatha regardless of its case, وَاللَّهِ لَنْ تَذْهَبَنَّ، وَاللَّهِ لَنْ تَذْهَبَنْ; and if the nun of the feminine plural is attached, it becomes fixed on sukun instead, لَنْ تُدْرِكْنَ الْمَجْدَ إِلَّا بِالْعَفَافِ.

(5)

The examples above and the commentary structure follow al-Tuhfat al-Saniyyah bi Sharh al-Muqaddimat al-Ajrumiyyah, by Muhammad Muhi al-Din 'Abd al-Hamid, translated into English here directly from the Arabic rather than from any existing published translation.

For drilling these signs, Sidi Shaykh Abdullahi suggested the classical exercise book portion of al-Tuhfa al-Saniyya and Quranic parsing (i'rab al-Quran) as the two most effective tools. Working the case endings out on a real ayah, rather than an invented sentence, forces you to actually apply the rule above rather than just recognize it.
