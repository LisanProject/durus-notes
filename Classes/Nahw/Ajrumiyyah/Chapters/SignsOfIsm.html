Signs of the Ism علامات الاسم

Text
فَالِاسْمُ يُعْرَفُ بِالْخَفْضِ وَالتَّنْوِينِ، وَدُخُولِ الْأَلِفِ وَاللَّامِ عَلَيْهِ، وَحُرُوفِ الْخَفْضِ، وَهِيَ مِنْ، وَإِلَى، وَعَنْ، وَعَلَى، وَفِي، وَرُبَّ، وَالْبَاءُ، وَالْكَافُ، وَاللَّامُ، وَحُرُوفُ الْقَسَمِ، وَهِيَ الْوَاوُ، وَالْبَاءُ، وَالتَّاءُ
Vocabulary
  • الْخَفْضthe genitive case; also called jarr; indicated by kasra or its substitute at the end of the noun
  • التَّنْوِينnunation; the doubled ending sound appearing as ً ٌ ٍ on indefinite nouns
  • الْأَلِف وَاللَّامthe definite article al; attaches only to nouns
  • حُرُوف الْخَفْضthe prepositions; particles that govern the genitive case
  • حُرُوف الْقَسَمthe letters of oath; a subset of the letters that attach only to nouns in oaths
Translation
The noun is identified by the genitive case, nunation, the entry of the definite article upon it, and the prepositions, which are: from, to, away from, upon, in, perhaps, the ba, the kaf, and the lam, and the letters of oath, which are the waw, the ba, and the ta.
Commentary

The noun is identified by accepting any one of four things: the genitive case, nunation, the definite article, or the prepositions upon it.

1. Al-Khafd (The Genitive Case) means the noun can take a kasra or its substitute at its end when governed by a preposition or by another noun in annexation (idafa). The ability to take this case marker is exclusive to nouns.

(1)

2. Al-Tanwin (Nunation) is the doubled ending sound that appears on indefinite nouns. It takes three forms depending on the case of the noun: dammatan in the nominative (rafa), fathatan in the accusative (nasb), and kasratan in the genitive (khafd). Its presence on a word confirms that word is a noun.

(2)

3. Al-Alif wal-Lam (The Definite Article) attaches only to nouns and is therefore a positive identifier. When ال enters upon a word, that word is a noun.

(3)

4. Huruf al-Khafd (The Prepositions) govern only nouns and so their presence identifies the word that follows them as a noun. Ibn Ajurrum lists nine: مِنْ (from), إِلَى (to), عَنْ (away from), عَلَى (upon), فِي (in), رُبَّ (perhaps), ب (the ba), ك (the kaf), and ل (the lam).

(4)

Also included are the letters of oath (huruf al-qasam): و (the waw), ب (the ba), and ت (the ta). These are included here because they can only attach to nouns, making them additional identifiers of the noun even though they are not prepositions in the primary sense.

The commentary illustrates each sign with its own examples.

Example 1Identify the sign that shows this word is a noun.مَرَرْتُ بِبَكْرٍ

"I passed by Bakr."

Step 1: Look at what precedes بَكْرٍ: the preposition بِ, one of the huruf al-khafd listed in the text.
Step 2: Since prepositions govern only nouns, anything following بِ must be a noun.
Step 3: Confirm by checking the ending: بَكْرٍ carries a kasra, exactly the case marking khafd predicts.

Conclusion: بَكْرٍ is a noun, identified by the preposition governing it and by the khafd ending that preposition produces.

Example 2Identify the sign that shows this word is a noun.مُحَمَّدٌ، كِتَابٌ، مُسْلِمَاتٍ

Step 1: Check each word's ending for one of the four positive signs of the noun.
Step 2: مُحَمَّدٌ ends in dammatan, كِتَابٌ also ends in dammatan, and مُسْلِمَاتٍ ends in kasratan, so all three carry tanwin.
Step 3: Tanwin is one of the four signs listed in the text, and its presence alone, regardless of which case it marks, is enough to confirm a word is a noun.

Conclusion: All three words are nouns, identified by tanwin.

Example 3Identify the sign that shows this word is a noun.الرَّجُلُ، الْكِتَابُ، الْمَدْرَسَةُ

Step 1: Check each word for one of the four signs of the noun.
Step 2: الرَّجُلُ, الْكِتَابُ, and الْمَدْرَسَةُ each begin with ال, the definite article.
Step 3: Since ال attaches only to nouns and never to verbs or particles, its presence alone identifies the word that carries it.

Conclusion: All three words are nouns, identified by the definite article.

Example 4Identify the sign that shows this word is a noun.ذَهَبْتُ مِنَ الْبَيْتِ إِلَى الْمَدْرَسَةِ

"I went from the house to the school."

Step 1: Look at what precedes الْبَيْتِ and الْمَدْرَسَةِ: the prepositions مِنَ and إِلَى respectively, both huruf al-khafd.
Step 2: That alone is enough to confirm both words are nouns, since prepositions govern only nouns.
Step 3: Notice a second, independent sign on both words as well: each also carries the definite article ال.

Conclusion: الْبَيْتِ and الْمَدْرَسَةِ are both nouns, identified twice over, once by the governing preposition and once by the definite article each carries.

Example 5Identify the sign that shows this word is a noun.وَاللَّهِ لَأَجْتَهِدَنَّ

"By Allah, I will surely strive."

Step 1: Look at what precedes اللَّهِ: the letter و, one of the huruf al-qasam (letters of oath) listed in the text.
Step 2: The waw of oath attaches only to an explicit noun, never to a pronoun, so its presence here confirms اللَّهِ must be a noun.
Step 3: This is a separate identifier from khafd, tanwin, or the definite article; it is included specifically because, unlike the ordinary prepositions, the letters of oath attach in this one narrow grammatical context.

Conclusion: اللَّهِ is identified as a noun by the waw of oath.

The commentary's own exercise for this section asks the student to identify every noun in a string of ayat and phrases, naming the sign that identifies each one, including بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَنِ الرَّحِيمِ, الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ, and وَالْعَصْرِ إِنَّ الْإِنسَانَ لَفِي خُسْرٍ among others, translated here from the Arabic rather than any published edition.
