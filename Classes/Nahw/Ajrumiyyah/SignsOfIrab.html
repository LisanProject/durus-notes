Lesson III: Signs of I'rab

The Signs of Rafa علامات الرفع

Text
لِلرَّفْعِ أَرْبَعُ عَلَامَاتٍ: الضَّمَّةُ، والوَاوُ وَالْأَلِفُ، وَالنُّونُ. فَأَمَّا الضَّمَّةُ، فَتَكُونُ عَلَامَةً لِلرَّفْعِ فِي أَرْبَعَةِ مَوَاضِعَ: فِي الِاسْمِ الْمُفْرَدِ، وَجَمْعِ التَّكْسِيرِ، وَجَمْعِ الْمُؤَنَّثِ السَّالِمِ، وَالْفِعْلِ الْمُضَارِعِ الَّذِي لَمْ يَتَّصِلْ بِآخِرِهِ شَيْءٌ. وَأَمَّا الْوَاوُ، فَتَكُونُ عَلَامَةً لِلرَّفْعِ فِي مَوْضِعَيْنِ: فِي جَمْعِ الْمُذَكَّرِ السَّالِمِ، وَفِي الْأَسْمَاءِ الْخَمْسَةِ، وَهِيَ: أَبُوكَ، وَأَخُوكَ، وَحَمُوكَ، وَفُوكَ، وَذُو مَالٍ. وَأَمَّا الْأَلِفُ، فَتَكُونُ عَلَامَةً لِلرَّفْعِ فِي تَثْنِيَةِ الْأَسْمَاءِ خَاصَّةً. وَأَمَّا النُّونُ، فَتَكُونُ عَلَامَةً لِلرَّفْعِ فِي الْفِعْلِ الْمُضَارِعِ، إِذَا اتَّصَلَ بِهِ ضَمِيرُ تَثْنِيَةٍ، أَوْ ضَمِيرُ جَمْعٍ، أَوْ ضَمِيرُ الْمُؤَنَّثَةِ الْمُخَاطَبَةِ.
Vocabulary
  • الضَّمَّةthe damma; the primary sign of rafa; the short vowel u at the end of a word
  • الْوَاوthe waw; the letter و used as a case sign in the sound masculine plural and the Five Nouns
  • الْأَلِفthe alif; the letter ا used as a case sign in the dual
  • النُّونthe nun; the letter ن whose presence marks rafa in the Five Verbs
  • الِاسْم الْمُفْرَدthe singular noun; any noun referring to one thing, not dual or plural
  • جَمْع التَّكْسِيرthe broken plural; a plural formed by altering the internal structure of the word
  • جَمْع الْمُؤَنَّث السَّالِمthe sound feminine plural; a plural formed by adding ـَاتٌ
  • الْفِعْل الْمُضَارِعthe present tense verb
  • جَمْع الْمُذَكَّر السَّالِمthe sound masculine plural; a plural formed by adding ـُونَ or ـِينَ
  • الْأَسْمَاء الْخَمْسَةthe Five Nouns: أَبُوكَ، أَخُوكَ، حَمُوكَ، فُوكَ، ذُو مَالٍ
  • تَثْنِيَةthe dual; the form indicating two of something
Translation
Rafa has four signs: the damma, the waw, the alif, and the nun. The damma is a sign of rafa in four places: the singular noun, the broken plural, the sound feminine plural, and the present tense verb that has nothing attached to its end. The waw is a sign of rafa in two places: the sound masculine plural, and the Five Nouns, which are: your father, your brother, your father-in-law, your mouth, and one possessing wealth. The alif is a sign of rafa in the dual noun exclusively. The nun is a sign of rafa in the present tense verb when a dual pronoun, a plural pronoun, or the feminine second-person pronoun is attached to it.
Commentary

Of the four signs, the damma carries the most weight, since it governs the widest range of forms: the singular noun, the broken plural, the sound feminine plural, and the present tense verb with nothing attached to its end. It need not always be pronounced. Where pronunciation is simply impossible, as with a noun ending in alif like مُوسَى, or where it would be phonologically awkward, as with a noun ending in ya like الْقَاضِي, the damma is understood rather than heard. Grammarians call this estimation (taqdir), distinguishing it from the ordinary, audible case (zahira).

The waw takes over in exactly two places: the sound masculine plural, as in مُسْلِمُونَ, and the Five Nouns, أَبُوكَ، أَخُوكَ، حَمُوكَ، فُوكَ، ذُو مَالٍ, though only once they stand in annexation (idafa). Outside of annexation the Five Nouns lose this special treatment and simply decline like any other noun.

The alif, by contrast, marks only the dual, رَجُلَانِ، مُحَمَّدَانِ, and nothing else.

With verbs the logic shifts: it is the nun's presence, not its shape, that signals rafa in the Five Verbs, يَفْعَلَانِ، تَفْعَلَانِ، يَفْعَلُونَ، تَفْعَلُونَ، تَفْعَلِينَ. Once these verbs move into the accusative or jussive, the nun simply falls away, so it is often easier to remember the rule as an absence rather than a presence: no nun, no rafa.

The Signs of Nasb علامات النصب

Text
وَلِلنَّصْبِ خَمْسُ عَلَامَاتٍ: الْفَتْحَةُ، وَالْأَلِفُ، وَالْكَسْرَةُ، وَالْيَاءُ، وَحَذْفُ النُّونِ. فَأَمَّا الْفَتْحَةُ، فَتَكُونُ عَلَامَةً لِلنَّصْبِ فِي ثَلَاثَةِ مَوَاضِعَ: فِي الِاسْمِ الْمُفْرَدِ، وَجَمْعِ التَّكْسِيرِ، وَالْفِعْلِ الْمُضَارِعِ إِذَا دَخَلَ عَلَيْهِ نَاصِبٌ وَلَمْ يَتَّصِلْ بِآخِرِهِ شَيْءٌ. وَأَمَّا الْأَلِفُ، فَتَكُونُ عَلَامَةً لِلنَّصْبِ فِي الْأَسْمَاءِ الْخَمْسَةِ، نَحْوَ: رَأَيْتُ أَبَاكَ وَأَخَاكَ وَمَا أَشْبَهَ ذَلِكَ.
Vocabulary
  • الْفَتْحَةthe fatha; the primary sign of nasb; the short vowel a at the end of a word
  • الْكَسْرَةthe kasra; the short vowel i; used as the sign of nasb for the sound feminine plural
  • الْيَاءthe ya; the letter ي used as the sign of nasb for the dual and sound masculine plural
  • حَذْف النُّونdropping the nun; the removal of the nun as the sign of nasb and jazm for the Five Verbs
  • نَاصِبa nasb particle; a particle that governs the accusative case in a following verb
Translation
Nasb has five signs: the fatha, the alif, the kasra, the ya, and dropping the nun. The fatha is a sign of nasb in three places: the singular noun, the broken plural, and the present tense verb when a nasb particle enters upon it and nothing is attached to its end. The alif is a sign of nasb for the Five Nouns, such as: I saw your father and your brother and whatever resembles that.
Commentary

Nasb, the accusative case, marks direct objects among other roles, and covers five distinct signs rather than the four rafa has: one root sign, the fatha, and four subsidiary ones that substitute for it in particular categories.

Fatha does most of the work, appearing on the singular noun, the broken plural, and the present tense verb once a nasb particle enters and nothing else is attached to its end. On the singular noun it is usually explicit, but becomes implicit whenever the noun ends in alif maqsura, since that letter cannot physically carry a visible vowel. Let's take a look at some examples.

Example 1 لَقِيتُ عَلِيًّا

"I met Ali." عَلِيًّا is a singular noun, mansub as the object of لَقِيتُ. Its sign is a visible fatha.

Example 2 لَقِيتُ الْفَتَى

"I met the young man." الْفَتَى is a singular noun, mansub as the object of لَقِيتُ. Its sign is an implicit fatha, since a word ending in alif maqsura cannot physically carry a visible vowel.

The broken plural follows the same pattern.

Example 3 صَاحَبْتُ الرِّجَالَ

"I accompanied the men." الرِّجَالَ is a broken plural, mansub as the object of صَاحَبْتُ. Its sign is a visible fatha.

The Quran supplies two clean examples of the implicit case for a broken plural.

Example 4 وَتَرَى النَّاسَ سُكَارَىٰ

"And you will see the people intoxicated" (al-Hajj 22:2). سُكَارَىٰ is a broken plural, mansub as the second object of تَرَى. Its sign is an implicit fatha on the alif maqsura.

Example 5 وَأَنكِحُوا الْأَيَامَىٰ مِنكُمْ

"And marry the unmarried among you" (al-Nur 24:32). الْأَيَامَىٰ is a broken plural, mansub as the object of أَنكِحُوا. As with سُكَارَىٰ above, the fatha is implicit because the word ends in alif maqsura.

On the present tense verb, the same explicit/implicit split applies.

Example 6 لَن نَّبْرَحَ عَلَيْهِ عَاكِفِينَ

"We will never cease being devoted to it" (Taha 20:91). نَّبْرَحَ is a present tense verb, made mansub by the particle لَنْ. Since nothing is attached to its end, its sign is a visible fatha.

Example 7 يُرْضِينِي أَنْ تَسْعَى إِلَى الْمَجْدِ

"It pleases me that you strive toward glory." تَسْعَى is a present tense verb, made mansub by أَنْ. Its sign is an implicit fatha, since it ends in alif maqsura.

If the verb has the alif of duality, the waw of a masculine plural subject, or the ya of feminine address attached to its end, fatha cannot appear at all, and the sign shifts to dropping the nun instead: لَنْ يَضْرِبَا، لَنْ تَضْرِبُوا، لَنْ تَضْرِبِي. If instead a confirming nun (heavy or light) is attached, the verb becomes fixed on fatha regardless of its case, وَاللَّهِ لَنْ تَذْهَبَنَّ، وَاللَّهِ لَنْ تَذْهَبَنْ; and if the nun of the feminine plural is attached, it becomes fixed on sukun instead, لَنْ تُدْرِكْنَ الْمَجْدَ إِلَّا بِالْعَفَافِ.

The Five Nouns simply swap letters depending on the case: waw for rafa, alif for nasb. رَأَيْتُ أَبَاكَ، رَأَيْتُ أَخَاكَ follow the same annexation requirement noted in the previous lesson.

The sound feminine plural breaks the pattern slightly, taking kasra instead of the fatha you would otherwise expect, purely for ease of pronunciation: رَأَيْتُ الْمُسْلِمَاتِ، رَأَيْتُ السَّمَاوَاتِ.

Ya covers two forms at once, the dual and the sound masculine plural, both of which used alif or waw for rafa and now converge on the same letter for nasb and khafd alike: رَأَيْتُ رَجُلَيْنِ، رَأَيْتُ الْمُسْلِمِينَ.

And as with rafa, the Five Verbs are marked by what disappears rather than what appears: the nun drops for nasb exactly as it does for jazm, لَنْ يَفْعَلَا، لَمْ يَفْعَلُوا, so the nominative is the only state in which these verbs keep it.

The examples above and the commentary structure follow al-Tuhfat al-Saniyyah bi Sharh al-Muqaddimat al-Ajrumiyyah, by Muhammad Muhi al-Din 'Abd al-Hamid, translated into English here directly from the Arabic rather than from any existing published translation.

For drilling these signs, Sidi Shaykh Abdullahi suggested the classical exercise book portion of al-Tuhfa al-Saniyya and Quranic parsing (i'rab al-Quran) as the two most effective tools. Working the case endings out on a real ayah, rather than an invented sentence, forces you to actually apply the rule above rather than just recognize it.
