Introduction to the Shafi'i Madhhab

Adapted from Taqrirat al-Sadidah, as taught by Shaykh Mahdi Lock.

The Founder of the School

Muhammad ibn Idris al-Shafi'i al-Muttalibi is the founder of the Shafi'i school. His lineage connects with that of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ at their common ancestor, 'Abd Manaf. He was born in Gaza in the year 150 AH and was brought to Makkah, where he began his pursuit of knowledge, studying under Imam Khalid ibn Muslim al-Zanji, the Mufti of Makkah, along with al-Fudayl ibn 'Iyad, Sufyan ibn 'Uyaynah, and others.

He traveled to Madinah at the age of twelve, memorized the entire Muwatta' in nine days, and closely accompanied Imam Malik, studying under him until he became one of his top students. He also took knowledge from the scholars of Madinah and Makkah, and became qualified to give fatwa at the age of fifteen. He engaged deeply in Arabic linguistics and poetry and was praised for his mastery; even al-Asma'i, a transmitter of Arab poetry, benefited from him, and he learned from the ancient poetry of the Banu Hudhayl tribe.

Yemen and Iraq, and the Old Madhhab. He traveled to Yemen and took knowledge from scholars including Mutarrif ibn Mazin, Hisham ibn Yusuf the judge, 'Amr ibn Abi Salamah, and Yahya ibn Hasan. He then traveled to Iraq and studied with Waki' ibn al-Jarrah and Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani, the jurist of Iraq. There he composed the book Al-Hujjah and established what became known as the old school of thought (al-madhhab al-qadim). Major scholars took knowledge from him during this period, including Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal and Abu Thawr.

(1)

Egypt and the New Madhhab. He then turned toward Egypt, revising many of his earlier legal opinions. There he founded his new legal school (al-madhhab al-jadid). He is regarded as the renewer (mujaddid) of the second century, having combined the sciences of hadith with the intellectual reasoning of opinion-based jurisprudence. He laid down the foundations of usul al-fiqh, authoring Al-Risalah, the first book on legal theory, and authored Al-Umm during this period. His knowledge extended across hadith, Qur'an, Arabic grammar, history, poetry, and theology. He lived a life of asceticism and worship, and passed away in Egypt in the year 204 AH.

(2)

Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal said of him: "Imam al-Shafi'i was like the sun for the world and like health for the body. Can either of these be replaced or substituted?" He also said: "Knowledge of jurisprudence was locked up with its people until Allah opened it through al-Shafi'i." Imam Abu Zur'ah said: "I do not know of anyone who had a greater impact on the people of Islam than al-Shafi'i."

Imam al-Shafi'i once said: "I wish that the people would learn this knowledge and none of it be attributed to me." His du'a was, in a sense, accepted: the enduring character of the Shafi'i school is that it was polished, codified, and brought to legal maturity by the hands of the scholars who came after him, most notably al-Ghazali, al-Rafi'i, and al-Nawawi, who preserved the madhhab in its clearest form.

The Five Phases of the Madhhab's History

The history of the madhhab can be summarized in five phases, spanning from Imam al-Shafi'i's own lifetime through the works still relied upon in fatwa today.

1. The Founding Phase. This began with the establishment of the school and ended with the death of Imam al-Shafi'i. During this phase he authored the foundational work al-Umm.

(1)

2. The Transmission Phase. His students and companions began spreading the madhhab, most notably al-Muzani, al-Buwayti, al-Rabi' al-Muradi, Harmalah, al-Rabi' al-Jizi, and Yunus ibn 'Abd al-A'la. Al-Muzani's Mukhtasar became a foundational reference for later scholars. His nephew, Abu Ja'far al-Tahawi, initially studied under him in the Shafi'i school; al-Muzani is reported to have remarked that al-Tahawi would never attain a high rank in knowledge. Feeling dismissed, al-Tahawi left the Shafi'i school for the Hanafi tradition, and in time rose to become one of its most authoritative jurists, authoring works such as Sharh Ma'ani al-Athar and Aqidah al-Tahawiyyah, still studied across madhhab lines today.

(2)

3. The Expansion and Branching Phase. This stage saw the documentation and expansion of legal issues within the madhhab, and two major methodological approaches emerged: the Iraqi approach, led by Abu Hamid al-Isfarayini and followed by al-Mawardi, Abu al-Tayyib al-Tabari, and others; and the Khurasani (Persian) approach, led by al-Qaffal al-Saghir Abu Bakr al-Marwazi and followed by Abu Muhammad al-Juwayni, al-Furani, al-Qadi Husayn, and others.

(3)

4. The Codification Phase. Carried out by al-Rafi'i and al-Nawawi. Al-Rafi'i produced al-Muharrar, al-Sharh al-Saghir, and al-Sharh al-Kabir, all based on al-Ghazali's al-Wajiz; al-Nawawi produced al-Minhaj, al-Majmu' Sharh al-Muhadhdhab, and Rawdat al-Talibin, likewise drawing on al-Wajiz. Between them, they refined and systematically arranged the issues of the madhhab, clarified the evidences, and reconciled the varying narrations and positions within the school.

(4)

5. The Consolidation Phase. Marked by the efforts of Ibn Hajar al-Haytami, author of Tuhfat al-Muhtaj bi Sharh al-Minhaj, and al-Shams al-Ramli, author of Nihayat al-Muhtaj ila Sharh al-Minhaj. Both compiled and analyzed rulings across the school, clarified points left unaddressed by Nawawi and Rafi'i, and organized the madhhab into a coherent system covering the full breadth of fiqh.

(5)

Once the madhhab was firmly established through the works of al-Nawawi and al-Rafi'i, later scholars accepted the writings of Ibn Hajar and al-Ramli as authoritative in fatwa, following a clear hierarchy: when Nawawi and Rafi'i agree on a matter, their position is the relied-upon (mu'tamad) one; when they differ, Nawawi's opinion is preferred; when both are silent on a matter, one may choose between the opinions of Ibn Hajar or al-Ramli. In that last case, the people of the Hijaz and Hadramawt generally prefer Ibn Hajar's Tuhfah, while Egyptians and Syrians generally favor al-Ramli's Nihayah.

The Most Important Books of the Madhhab

Books of fiqh in the madhhab are generally divided into foundational texts (mutun), commentaries (shuruh), supercommentaries (hawashi), and legal responses (fatawa), among others.

Foundational Texts (Mutun). Al-Risalah al-Jami'ah by Ahmad ibn Zayn al-Habashi; Safinat al-Najah by Salim ibn Sumayr al-Hadrami; al-Muqaddimah al-Hadramiyyah by 'Abd al-Rahman Ba Fadl; Matn al-Ghayah wal-Taqrib by Abu Shuja' al-Asfahani; Safwat al-Zubad by Shihab al-Din Ahmad ibn Raslan; and 'Umdat al-Salik by Ibn al-Naqib.

(1)

Commentaries (Shuruh). The major commentaries on Imam al-Nawawi's al-Minhaj alone include Kanz al-Raghibin by al-Mahalli, Mughni al-Muhtaj by al-Khatib al-Shirbini, Nihayat al-Muhtaj by al-Ramli, and Tuhfat al-Muhtaj by Ibn Hajar al-Haytami. These commentaries are not merely explanatory; they clarify ambiguous expressions in the mutun, reconcile variant opinions, and preserve the reasoning of the madhhab's foremost authorities, forming the backbone of how the school is actually taught and applied in fatwa.

(2)

In institutions like al-Azhar, it is common for two or three major commentaries to be read alongside the mutun during lessons with the teacher, emphasizing layered analysis and discussion. In the Hijaz, and particularly in Yemen at institutions like Dar al-Mustafa in Tarim, teachers focus primarily on the mutun in class, with students expected to study the commentaries independently after mastering the texts, fostering precision and retention in the early stages of learning.

Distinct Merits of the Shafi'i Madhhab

1. Strong Foundation on Textual Proof. Imam al-Shafi'i grounded the school firmly in the Qur'an and Sunnah, having studied directly under Imam Malik and the followers of the Companions. Many leading hadith scholars, including al-Bayhaqi and Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani, belonged to the Shafi'i school.

(1)

2. Groundbreaking Work in Legal Theory. Imam al-Shafi'i was the first to codify usul al-fiqh in a structured work, and his students' students developed major reference works, such as those of al-Juwayni and al-Ghazali, that became foundational across madhhab lines.

(2)

3. Balanced Methodology. The madhhab strikes a balance between the rationalist approach (ahl al-ra'y) and the traditionalist approach (ahl al-hadith).

(3)

4. Widespread Ijtihad, Rich Literature, and Global Reach. A large number of independent jurists emerged within the school across every era, from al-'Izz ibn 'Abd al-Salam to al-Suyuti, producing an unusually rich body of literature. Followers of the madhhab are found across the Muslim world, from Southeast Asia to India, Persia, the Levant, the Gulf, Hijaz, Hadramawt, Yemen, Egypt, and East Africa.

(4)

5. Successive Revivers. At the start of nearly every century, a notable reviver (mujaddid) of the madhhab has emerged: Imam al-Shafi'i for the 2nd century, Abu al-'Abbas ibn Surayj for the 3rd, Abu al-Tayyib Sahl al-Sa'luki for the 4th, Abu Hamid al-Ghazali for the 5th, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi for the 6th, Imam al-Nawawi for the 7th, al-Isnawi for the 8th, Ibn Hajar al-Haytami for the 9th, and al-Suyuti for the 10th.

(5)
