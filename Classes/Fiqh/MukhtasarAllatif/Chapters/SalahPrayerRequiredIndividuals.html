Who Prayer Is Obligatory Upon مَن تَجِبُ عَلَيْهِ الصَّلَاةُ

Text
تَجِبُ الصَّلَاةُ عَلَى كُلِّ مُسْلِمٍ بَالِغٍ عَاقِلٍ طَاهِرٍ، وَيَجِبُ عَلَى الْوَلِيِّ أَنْ يَأْمُرَ الصَّبِيَّ بِالصَّلَاةِ لِسَبْعِ سِنِينَ، وَيَضْرِبَهُ عَلَى تَرْكِهَا لِعَشْرٍ، وَالصَّبِيَّةُ كَالصَّبِيِّ.
Prayer is obligatory upon every Muslim who is of age, sane, and free of ritual impurity. A guardian must command a child to pray at seven years old, and strike the child for neglecting it at ten. A girl follows the same ruling as a boy.

Mukhtasar al-Latif, on who prayer is obligatory upon.

The Four Conditions

Prayer becomes obligatory on a person only once four conditions are met together.

1. Being Muslim.

(1)

2. Being of age (baligh).

(2)

3. Being sane ('aqil).

(3)

4. Being free of major ritual impurity (tahir).

(4)

Being free of major impurity here refers to the condition that makes prayer obligatory in the first place, not the separate purification that must actually be performed before praying. Someone in a state of major impurity, such as janabah, is still obligated to pray. They must purify themselves first for that prayer to actually be valid.

Shart Wujub vs. Shart Sihha

Two categories of condition come up constantly in fiqh, and prayer is a clear place to see the difference between them.

Shart Wujub (Condition of Obligation). A condition that determines whether an act becomes obligatory on a person in the first place. Puberty is the standard example for prayer. A child is not yet obligated to pray, regardless of whether they are physically capable of performing the prayer correctly.

(1)

Shart Sihha (Condition of Validity). A condition that determines whether an act, once performed, actually counts as sound. Purification before prayer is the standard example. It does not determine whether prayer is obligatory on someone. It only determines whether a given performance of the prayer is valid.

(2)

The two categories can overlap for a given ruling, but they answer genuinely different questions: whether an act is owed at all, and whether a particular performance actually satisfies that obligation. A child who prays before puberty is not fulfilling an obligation that existed yet, which is why prayers performed before puberty are not made up later, unlike Hajj, where the ruling is different and repetition after maturity is required.

Teaching and Disciplining a Child to Pray

A guardian is required to command a child to pray starting at seven years old, and to discipline the child for neglecting prayer starting at ten. This applies equally to boys and girls. The obligation to pray does not fall on the child directly during these years. The responsibility to train the child falls on the guardian.

Maturity itself is recognized through specific signs: menstruation for a female, and nocturnal emission for a male, with a default age applied if neither sign has appeared by then. Once these signs occur, or the default age is reached, the obligation shifts onto the person directly.

وَإِذَا بَلَغَ الصَّبِيُّ أَوْ طَهُرَتِ الْحَائِضُ أَوِ النُّفَسَاءُ أَوْ أَفَاقَ الْمَجْنُونُ قَبْلَ خُرُوجِ الْوَقْتِ بِقَدْرِ تَكْبِيرَةٍ وَجَبَ قَضَاءُ تِلْكَ الصَّلَاةِ، وَيَجِبُ أَيْضاً قَضَاءُ الْفَرْضِ الَّذِي قَبْلَهَا إِذَا كَانَ ظُهْراً أَوْ مَغْرِباً، وَإِذَا دَخَلَ الْوَقْتُ وَمَضَى مَا يُؤَدَّى فِيهِ الْفَرْضُ ثُمَّ حَاضَتِ الْمَرْأَةُ أَوْ نَفِسَتْ قَبْلَ الصَّلَاةِ، أَوْ جُنَّ قَبْلَ الْوَقْتِ وَجَبَ قَضَاءُ تِلْكَ الصَّلَاةِ.
If a child reaches puberty, or a menstruating or postpartum woman becomes pure, or an insane person recovers, with the amount of time remaining in the prayer window equal to at least one takbir, that prayer must be made up. The obligatory prayer before it must also be made up if it was Dhuhr or Maghrib. And if the prayer time began, and enough time passed for the obligatory prayer to have been performed, and then the woman menstruated or began postpartum bleeding before praying, or the person became insane before the time ended, that prayer must be made up.

Mukhtasar al-Latif, on the make-up rules at the edges of a prayer window.

Make-Up Rules at the Edges of a Prayer Window

Two situations require a missed prayer to be made up (qada'), both tied to how much time was actually available within the prayer window.

1. An Obstacle Lifts Near the End of the Window. If a child reaches puberty, a menstruating or postpartum woman becomes pure, or an insane person recovers, and at least the amount of time needed for one takbir remains before the window closes, that prayer must be made up. If that prayer is Dhuhr or Maghrib, the obligatory prayer paired with it, 'Asr or 'Isha, must also be made up.

(1)

2. An Obstacle Arrives After Enough Time Has Already Passed. If the prayer window began, and enough time passed within it for the prayer to have actually been performed, and only then did a woman begin menstruating or postpartum bleeding, or a person become insane, the prayer that could have been performed in that window must be made up.

(2)

Both rules rest on the same underlying idea: what matters is whether the person had a real opportunity to fulfill the obligation within the window, not simply whether they were obligated at some single instant during it.

A Note on Apostasy

A person born outside Islam is not required to make up prayers missed before ever becoming Muslim. An apostate is treated differently. Someone who leaves Islam and later returns is required to make up all the prayers and zakah missed during the period of apostasy, since they were already bound by the obligations of Islam before leaving it.
