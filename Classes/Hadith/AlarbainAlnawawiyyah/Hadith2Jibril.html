Hadith 2: The Hadith of Jibril

Session with Sidi Shaykh Muhyiddin Lashen.

Text
بَيْنَمَا نَحْنُ عِنْدَ رَسُولِ اللَّهِ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ ذَاتَ يَوْمٍ إِذْ طَلَعَ عَلَيْنَا رَجُلٌ شَدِيدُ بَيَاضِ الثِّيَابِ شَدِيدُ سَوَادِ الشَّعَرِ لَا يُرَى عَلَيْهِ أَثَرُ السَّفَرِ وَلَا يَعْرِفُهُ مِنَّا أَحَدٌ حَتَّى جَلَسَ إِلَى النَّبِيِّ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ فَأَسْنَدَ رُكْبَتَيْهِ إِلَى رُكْبَتَيْهِ وَوَضَعَ كَفَّيْهِ عَلَى فَخِذَيْهِ وَقَالَ: يَا مُحَمَّدُ أَخْبِرْنِي عَنِ الْإِسْلَامِ. فَقَالَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ: الْإِسْلَامُ أَنْ تَشْهَدَ أَنْ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ وَأَنَّ مُحَمَّدًا رَسُولُ اللَّهِ، وَتُقِيمَ الصَّلَاةَ، وَتُؤْتِيَ الزَّكَاةَ، وَتَصُومَ رَمَضَانَ، وَتَحُجَّ الْبَيْتَ إِنِ اسْتَطَعْتَ إِلَيْهِ سَبِيلًا. قَالَ: صَدَقْتَ. فَعَجِبْنَا لَهُ يَسْأَلُهُ وَيُصَدِّقُهُ. قَالَ: فَأَخْبِرْنِي عَنِ الْإِيمَانِ. قَالَ: أَنْ تُؤْمِنَ بِاللَّهِ وَمَلَائِكَتِهِ وَكُتُبِهِ وَرُسُلِهِ وَالْيَوْمِ الْآخِرِ، وَتُؤْمِنَ بِالْقَدَرِ خَيْرِهِ وَشَرِّهِ. قَالَ: صَدَقْتَ. قَالَ: فَأَخْبِرْنِي عَنِ الْإِحْسَانِ. قَالَ: أَنْ تَعْبُدَ اللَّهَ كَأَنَّكَ تَرَاهُ، فَإِنْ لَمْ تَكُنْ تَرَاهُ فَإِنَّهُ يَرَاكَ. قَالَ: فَأَخْبِرْنِي عَنِ السَّاعَةِ. قَالَ: مَا الْمَسْئُولُ عَنْهَا بِأَعْلَمَ مِنَ السَّائِلِ. قَالَ: فَأَخْبِرْنِي عَنْ أَمَارَاتِهَا. قَالَ: أَنْ تَلِدَ الْأَمَةُ رَبَّتَهَا، وَأَنْ تَرَى الْحُفَاةَ الْعُرَاةَ الْعَالَةَ رِعَاءَ الشَّاءِ يَتَطَاوَلُونَ فِي الْبُنْيَانِ. ثُمَّ انْطَلَقَ فَلَبِثْتُ مَلِيًّا، ثُمَّ قَالَ: يَا عُمَرُ أَتَدْرِي مَنِ السَّائِلُ؟ قُلْتُ: اللَّهُ وَرَسُولُهُ أَعْلَمُ. قَالَ: فَإِنَّهُ جِبْرِيلُ أَتَاكُمْ يُعَلِّمُكُمْ دِينَكُمْ.
While we were sitting with the Messenger of Allah ﷺ one day, a man appeared before us in extremely white clothing and with extremely black hair, showing no sign of travel, and none of us recognized him. He sat down facing the Prophet ﷺ, resting his knees against his knees and placing his hands on his thighs, and said: "O Muhammad, tell me about Islam." The Messenger of Allah ﷺ said: "Islam is to testify that there is no god but Allah and that Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah, to establish prayer, to give zakah, to fast Ramadan, and to perform Hajj to the House if you are able to find a way to it." He said: "You have spoken the truth." We were amazed that he would ask and then confirm the answer. He said: "Then tell me about Iman." He said: "It is to believe in Allah, His angels, His books, His messengers, and the Last Day, and to believe in divine decree, its good and its bad." He said: "You have spoken the truth." He said: "Then tell me about Ihsan." He said: "It is to worship Allah as though you see Him, and if you do not see Him, then indeed He sees you." He said: "Then tell me about the Hour." He said: "The one asked about it knows no more than the one asking." He said: "Then tell me about its signs." He said: "That the slave girl will give birth to her mistress, and that you will see the barefoot, naked, destitute shepherds competing in constructing tall buildings." Then he left, and I stayed a while, then he (the Prophet ﷺ) said: "O 'Umar, do you know who the questioner was?" I said: "Allah and His Messenger know best." He said: "That was Jibril; he came to teach you your religion."

Narrated by 'Umar ibn al-Khattab; recorded in Sahih Muslim. Also called Ummu al-Sunnah, "the mother of the Sunnah," on account of how much it contains.

Linguistic Subtleties in the Narration

Baynama is a compound adverb of time carrying a built-in element of surprise, and idh ("suddenly," "then") marks the unexpected entrance of Jibril right at the point baynama sets up. The two words work together grammatically to stage the interruption: baynama opens a scene already in progress, and idh is the hinge where something unforeseen breaks into it.

'Umar ibn al-Khattab's description of Jibril, "extremely white clothes, extremely black hair" (shadidu bayad al-thiyab, shadidu sawad al-sha'r), uses idafa lafdhiyya (verbal annexation), a construction that intensifies the description while keeping it grammatically light. The rhetorical effect matters here: the extremity of both colors, without any softening, is precisely what preserves the mysteriousness of a figure who showed no sign of travel and was recognized by no one present. Nothing about him fit the ordinary explanations available to the Companions in the room.

The choice of white clothing specifically is symbolic rather than incidental: white represents purity and cleanliness, fitting for a scholarly gathering or a Friday sermon, in contrast to the darker colors classically worn in times of war. Jibril's appearance is already communicating something about the register of the encounter before he says a single word.

The Four Sciences Derived from the Hadith

The hadith's three central questions map directly onto three classical Islamic sciences, and the teacher adds a fourth that is easy to miss on a first reading.

1. The Question About Islam points to Fiqh (jurisprudence), concerned with outward acts and rulings.

(1)

2. The Question About Iman points to Aqidah ('Ilm al-Kalam, theology), concerned with inward belief.

(2)

3. The Question About Ihsan points to Tasawwuf (spirituality, self-purification), concerned with inner excellence.

(3)

4. The Signs of the Hour, discussed later in the same hadith, point to what Shaykh Abu Bakr al-Mashhur termed the 'Ilm al-Hikmah (the Science of Wisdom): a practical methodology for acting wisely during times of turmoil and transition, not a science of prediction for its own sake.

(4)

The Prophet ﷺ taught the signs of the Hour to lay out a practical way of confronting coming challenges, not merely to forecast them.

Islam, Iman, and Ihsan Explained

The shahada functions as a legal shield: it dictates the application of community rulings generally, not merely a private condition for an individual's entrance into Islam. Among the prerequisites for Hajj: provision for the road, means of travel, safety, and ensuring the financial security of one's dependents; Hajj becomes impermissible if performing it jeopardizes the rights of those who depend on the pilgrim.

Iman's essential validity is constant, but its strength increases with acts of obedience and decreases with disobedience. The faith of the Companions sits at a higher degree than that of ordinary people, though the core elements involved are the same for both.

1. Mushahadah (Witnessing) is the higher station of Ihsan, where the heart perceives realities with such clarity that it is as though one sees Allah directly.

(1)

2. Muraqabah (Vigilance) is the lower station, being consistently aware and conscious that Allah sees you in every action, without that direct clarity of perception.

(2)

The Signs of the Hour

"The slave girl gives birth to her mistress" carries more than one accepted reading: a historical interpretation ties it to conquests in which enslaved women bore children who became free rulers, while a more contemporary reading applies it to moral decay and children turning against their own mothers. "Barefoot, naked, destitute shepherds competing in massive construction" was read as socially and spiritually unqualified people rising to power and wealth, a sign with an obvious contemporary echo in celebrities being glorified while scholars are pushed to the margins: outward advancement in wealth or architecture sitting alongside real inner spiritual decline.

Jibril's repeated "you have spoken the truth" (sadaqt) after the Prophet's ﷺ correct answers was a deliberate pedagogical device, drawing the attention of the listening Companions and prompting them to retain every detail of the exchange.

Question 1Explain the grammatical role of "baynama" and "idh" as used in the Hadith of Jibril.

Baynama is a compound adverb of time that carries a built-in element of surprise, opening a scene already in progress. Idh ("suddenly," "then") marks the unexpected entrance of Jibril right at that point. Together they stage the interruption: baynama sets the scene, idh is the hinge where something unforeseen breaks into it.

Question 2Discuss the rhetorical effect of describing Jibril as "shadidu bayad al-thiyab, shadidu sawad al-sha'r," and explain how idafa lafdhiyya contributes to it.

Idafa lafdhiyya (verbal annexation) intensifies the description while keeping it grammatically light. The extremity of both colors, stated without any softening, preserves the mysteriousness of a figure who showed no sign of travel and was recognized by no one present, reinforcing that nothing about him fit the Companions' ordinary explanations.

Question 3What are the four Islamic sciences alluded to in the Hadith of Jibril, and to which questions do they each correspond?

The question about Islam corresponds to Fiqh. The question about Iman corresponds to Aqidah ('Ilm al-Kalam). The question about Ihsan corresponds to Tasawwuf. The later discussion of the signs of the Hour corresponds to a fourth science, termed by Shaykh Abu Bakr al-Mashhur as 'Ilm al-Hikmah, the Science of Wisdom, a practical methodology for acting wisely through times of turmoil.

Question 4In what way is the shahada considered a "legal shield" in Islamic law, rather than simply a personal declaration?

The shahada dictates the application of community rulings, not merely a private condition for an individual's own entrance into Islam. It functions at the level of the community's legal treatment of a person, which is why it is described as a shield rather than only a personal statement of belief.

Question 5Compare the stations of Mushahadah and Muraqabah in the context of Ihsan.

Mushahadah (witnessing) is the higher station, where the heart perceives realities with such clarity it is as though one sees Allah directly. Muraqabah (vigilance) is the lower station: being consistently conscious that Allah sees you in every action, without that same direct clarity of perception.

Question 6Offer two interpretations, one historical and one contemporary, for the sign "the slave girl gives birth to her mistress."

The historical reading ties it to conquests in which enslaved women bore children who became free rulers. The contemporary reading applies it to moral decay, specifically children turning against and rebelling against their own mothers.

Question 7Why did Jibril confirm the Prophet's answers with "sadaqt" before the listening Companions, and what was the pedagogical benefit?

The repeated confirmation was a deliberate pedagogical device that drew the attention of the listening Companions to what had just been said, prompting them to retain every detail of the exchange rather than let a dense, rapid-fire dialogue slip past them.
