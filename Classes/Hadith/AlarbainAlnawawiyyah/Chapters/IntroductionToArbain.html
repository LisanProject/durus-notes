Introduction to Al-Arba'in al-Nawawiyyah

Session with Shaykh Ibrahim.

Methodology: Al-'Ardh and Al-Qira'ah

Classical hadith study recognizes two complementary methods of recitation. In al-'ardh, the student recites the text aloud while the teacher listens and corrects; in al-qira'ah, the teacher reads while the students listen. Shaykh Ibrahim favored al-'ardh for this course specifically because it actively engages students and sharpens their ability to read difficult texts and pronounce narrators' names correctly, a method with precedent in the Maliki tradition: Imam Malik himself is described as listening to his own students recite al-Muwatta' back to him rather than always reading it to them.

The course plan follows the isnad first, then Imam Nawawi's own introduction, then the hadiths themselves in order, with commentary touching language, grammar, rhetoric, etymology, fiqh, aqidah, and logic as each hadith calls for it.

Beginning with Mercy: The Isnad and Musalsal bil-Awwaliyyah

Before reaching Nawawi's own text, the class opened with the isnad connecting Shaykh Ibrahim back through his own chain of teachers to the book's author, following the classical practice of tracing exactly who transmitted what to whom. The specific hadith used to open the chain is known as al-Musalsal bil-Awwaliyyah, "the first hadith," a hadith on mercy that has become the traditional first hadith a student hears from a new teacher, precisely because of its content:

"The merciful are shown mercy by the All-Merciful. Be merciful to those on earth, and the One above the heavens will be merciful to you."

By convention, each teacher in the chain states "this is the first hadith I heard from my shaykh," and this formula repeats at every link until the chain reaches Sufyan ibn 'Uyaynah, after which point the musalsal (chained) formula is no longer repeated. Scholars have written dedicated works cataloging their own teachers and chains this way, called thabat or athbat (registers); al-Qattani's Fihris al-Faharis is a well-known example of the genre. At the close of this chain, the class itself received an ijazah, a formal authorization to relate the hadith onward exactly as it was received.

The Ten Principles for Introducing Any Science

Classical scholarship treats a formal introduction (muqaddimah) as obligatory before teaching any science or any book, and distinguishes between introducing the science itself (muqaddimat al-'ilm) and introducing a specific book on that science (muqaddimat al-kitab). For the science, ten principles are traditionally covered, gathered in verse by earlier scholars and known as al-mabadi' al-'ashara.

Al-Mabadi' al-'Ashara (The Ten Principles): al-hadd (definition), al-mawdu' (subject matter), al-thamarah (objective/fruit), al-fadl (virtue), al-nisbah (its relationship to other sciences), al-wadi' (its founder), al-ism (its name), al-istimdad (its sources), hukm al-shar' (its legal ruling, i.e. whether studying it is obligatory), and al-masa'il (its issues/questions).

Why Forty? The Tradition Behind the Number

The choice of exactly forty hadiths is not arbitrary, and Nawawi's collection was far from the first of its kind. The practice traces back to a hadith, widely transmitted but classified as weak (da'if), narrated on the authority of several Companions including Abu al-Darda, Ibn Mas'ud, Ibn 'Umar, Ibn 'Abbas, Anas ibn Malik, and Mu'adh, which promises a special reward on the Day of Judgment to whoever preserves forty hadiths for the ummah.

A weak hadith is not the same as a fabricated one; weakness means the chain falls short of the full conditions for authenticity, not that the report was invented. The majority of scholars permit acting on weak hadiths specifically in the domain of encouraging good deeds (fada'il al-a'mal), particularly when multiple weak chains reinforce one another, so long as no matter of belief or law is built on the hadith alone. This principle is what allowed the "forty hadith" hadith, despite its weak chain, to inspire an entire genre of scholarship.

'Abdullah ibn al-Mubarak (118-182 AH) is credited as the first to compile a dedicated "forty hadith" collection, with other early compilers including Ibn Jurayj, Hushaym, Malik, and Ma'mar. The genre grew to include highly specific thematic collections: forty hadiths on Allah's names and attributes (al-Dhahabi), forty on the Prophet's ﷺ wives (Ibn 'Asakir), forty collected from forty different teachers across forty different cities (al-arba'in al-buldaniyyah, compiled independently by both Abu Tahir al-Silafi and Ibn 'Asakir), and even a collection on the Prophet's ﷺ sense of humor.

How Nawawi's "Forty" Became Forty-Two

In classical Arabic usage, round numbers like "forty" are often used loosely to mean "roughly forty," the same convention by which the Prophet's ﷺ age at death is sometimes given as sixty rather than the more precise sixty-three. Nawawi's own collection reflects this: it contains forty-two hadiths, not exactly forty, and the book's title should be understood in that spirit rather than as a strict count.

The collection was not assembled by Nawawi from nothing. Twenty-six of the forty-two were first publicly dictated by Ibn al-Salah in scholarly dictation sessions (majalis al-imla') held at Dar al-Hadith al-Ashrafiyyah in Damascus. Nawawi preserved these twenty-six in his own work Bustan al-'Arifin, then added three more of his own. In a separate work, al-Adhkar, he added a further hadith qudsi narrated from Abu Dharr, bringing the running total to thirty. After performing istikharah (seeking Allah's guidance through prayer), Nawawi added twelve more hadiths, completing the collection at forty-two, finished in 668 AH when he was thirty-seven years old.

Composition of the Forty-Two: 26 from Ibn al-Salah's original dictation sessions, 3 added by Nawawi in Bustan al-'Arifin, 1 hadith qudsi added in al-Adhkar, and 12 more added after istikharah, completed in 668 AH. Of the 42, 29 are found in both Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim, 5 more are otherwise authentic (sahih), and the remaining 8 are classified hasan.

A generation later, Ibn Rajab al-Hanbali judged the collection incomplete on one specific point and extended it to fifty hadiths in his own commentary, adding in particular a hadith on inheritance law (al-fara'id), an entire branch of fiqh he felt was conspicuously absent from Nawawi's original forty-two.

Ranks Among Hadith Scholars

Classical hadith scholarship recognizes a hierarchy of specialists, each rank defined by a combination of how many hadiths and chains a scholar has memorized, how deeply they have studied the sciences of hadith criticism, and how actively they teach and verify chains. From lower to higher, the ranks generally recognized are musnid (one who narrates hadith with its chain, without necessarily having deep critical knowledge of it), muhaddith (a scholar with broad knowledge of hadith texts, chains, and the narrators involved), hafiz (a muhaddith of considerably greater memorized scope and critical mastery), and Amir al-Mu'minin fi'l-Hadith ("Commander of the Faithful in Hadith"), an honorific reserved for a handful of scholars across history recognized as unmatched authorities in the field.

A Brief Biography of Imam al-Nawawi

Imam al-Nawawi, kunya Abu Zakariyya, was born in 631 AH in the town of Nawa, from which his nisbah derives. Some manuscripts differ on the correct vocalization of his grandfather's name, recorded as either Mira or Muri, a small but genuine point of classical scholarly debate. His kunya, Abu Zakariyya, does not imply he had a son named Zakariyya; the practice of assigning a kunya independent of actual children has precedent in hadith, including the Prophet's ﷺ own habit of calling a child "Abu 'Umayr" affectionately.

Nawawi moved to Damascus at nineteen and was known for an extraordinary work ethic even by the standards of classical scholarship: he is described as attending twelve lessons a day and rarely sleeping lying down after the age of eighteen. He briefly studied medicine, working through Ibn Sina's al-Qanun, but reported a sense of darkness or confusion settling over his heart during that period, which he took as a sign to devote himself entirely to fiqh and hadith instead.

Nawawi's scholarly career coincided with the Mongol invasions and the accompanying destruction of libraries and centers of learning across the Muslim world; Ibn Kathir describes the scale of intellectual loss in stark terms. Nawawi's response was an extraordinarily prolific output of writing, effectively working to replace what had been destroyed. His major works include al-Majmu' (his expansive Shafi'i fiqh commentary), Rawdat al-Talibin, Riyad al-Salihin, his commentary on Sahih Muslim, a commentary on Sunan Abi Dawud, and this collection, al-Arba'in. He died in 676 AH at the age of forty-five, having never married, a detail notable enough that later scholars, including Abu Ghuddah, wrote entire works cataloging the tradition of unmarried ("'uzzab") scholars, of whom Nawawi is among the most famous examples.

Here are a few questions to check your grasp of the introduction before moving into the hadiths themselves.

Question 1Nawawi's collection is called "the Forty" but actually contains forty-two hadiths. Explain both why classical usage allows this, and how the extra two hadiths came to be included.

Classical Arabic often uses round numbers loosely, the same way the Prophet's ﷺ age at death is sometimes rounded to sixty rather than sixty-three, so "forty" was never meant as an exact count. As for the extra hadiths specifically: Nawawi inherited 26 from Ibn al-Salah's dictation sessions, added 3 himself in Bustan al-'Arifin, added 1 more (a hadith qudsi) in al-Adhkar, bringing the total to 30, and then, after istikharah, added 12 more, landing on 42 in total.

Question 2The hadith encouraging Muslims to preserve forty hadiths is classified as weak (da'if). Explain why scholars still consider it acceptable grounds for an entire genre of "forty hadith" books, without contradicting the general caution around weak hadith.

Weakness in a hadith's chain means it falls short of the full conditions of authenticity, not that it was fabricated. The majority position allows acting on weak hadith specifically in the domain of encouraging good deeds (fada'il al-a'mal), especially where multiple weak chains reinforce one another, provided no point of belief or law is built on it alone. Compiling and memorizing forty hadiths is exactly this kind of encouraged, non-obligatory good deed, which is why scholars considered the hadith usable as inspiration for the genre despite its weak chain.

Question 3Ibn Rajab al-Hanbali extended Nawawi's forty-two hadiths to fifty. What specific gap was he addressing, and what does his choice suggest about how he understood the purpose of a "forty hadith" collection?

Ibn Rajab added a hadith specifically addressing inheritance law (al-fara'id), a branch of fiqh he felt was conspicuously missing from Nawawi's original forty-two. This suggests he understood the collection's purpose as more than a set of memorable, spiritually weighty hadiths; he saw it as aiming for a kind of comprehensive coverage of essential Islamic knowledge, comprehensive enough that a glaring gap in a major legal topic was worth correcting even at the cost of breaking from the traditional "forty" framing entirely.
