Hadith 3: Islam Is Built on Five

Text
عَنْ أَبِي عَبْدِ الرَّحْمَنِ عَبْدِ اللَّهِ بْنِ عُمَرَ بْنِ الْخَطَّابِ رَضِيَ اللَّهُ عَنْهُمَا قَالَ: سَمِعْتُ رَسُولَ اللَّهِ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ يَقُولُ: بُنِيَ الْإِسْلَامُ عَلَى خَمْسٍ: شَهَادَةِ أَنْ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ وَأَنَّ مُحَمَّدًا رَسُولُ اللَّهِ، وَإِقَامِ الصَّلَاةِ، وَإِيتَاءِ الزَّكَاةِ، وَحَجِّ الْبَيْتِ، وَصَوْمِ رَمَضَانَ. رَوَاهُ الْبُخَارِيُّ وَمُسْلِمٌ.
'Abdullah ibn 'Umar ibn al-Khattab said: I heard the Messenger of Allah ﷺ say: "Islam is built on five: testifying that there is no god but Allah and that Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah, establishing prayer, giving zakah, Hajj to the House, and fasting Ramadan." Narrated by al-Bukhari and Muslim.

Narrated by 'Abdullah ibn 'Umar; recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim.

The Tent Analogy

The five pillars are structured like a tent, not like five equally weighted items on a list. The shahada is the central pole: if it collapses, the entire structure collapses with it, and nothing else can stand independently of it. The remaining four pillars function as the side stakes securing the tent's edges. Losing one of them weakens the structure and leaves it exposed, but it does not bring the whole tent down the way losing the central pole would.

This is why the shahada is placed first and given the fullest wording in the hadith, while the other four are named more briefly: the analogy tracks a real structural asymmetry in how these five obligations relate to the basic reality of being Muslim, not simply the order someone happened to list them in.

Three Levels Required for Prayer

Establishing prayer (iqamat al-salah), the second pillar named in the hadith, is itself layered into three distinct levels, each with a different relationship to whether the prayer is considered complete or spiritually alive.

1. External Conditions (Shurut): matters that must be in place before the prayer even begins, such as ritual purity (taharah) and facing the qiblah. These are prerequisites to the prayer rather than parts of the prayer itself.

(1)

2. Physical Integrals (Arkan): the required physical components performed during the prayer itself, such as bowing (ruku') and prostration (sujud), carried out with calmness and composure (tuma'ninah) rather than rushed through.

(2)

3. Presence of Heart (Khushu'): the inward spiritual humility and attentiveness that gives the prayer its living quality, distinct from simply completing the outward motions correctly.

(3)

A prayer can satisfy the first two levels, the external conditions and the physical integrals, and still be legally valid while missing the third. The three levels are not redundant with each other: they answer three separate questions about what makes a prayer valid, complete, and spiritually alive, respectively.

A Grammatical Note on "Shahada"

The word shahadati (testifying) at the start of the hadith's enumeration can be analyzed with more than one valid i'rab (grammatical case), depending on how the underlying sentence structure is reconstructed: nominative (raf'), genitive (jarr), or accusative (nasb) are all defensible readings. This is not a discrepancy in the text itself, but a reflection of the fact that "buniya al-Islamu 'ala khamsin" can be grammatically unpacked in more than one way while preserving exactly the same meaning, a reminder that classical Arabic sentence analysis often supports multiple valid parses of a single authentic text.

Ibn 'Umar's Insistence on the Exact Order

In the wording of this hadith as 'Abdullah ibn 'Umar transmitted it, Hajj to the House is named before the fast of Ramadan, the reverse of the order these two pillars are usually recited in today. When a listener pointed out this apparent reordering to Ibn 'Umar, he insisted on preserving the sequence exactly as he had heard it directly from the Prophet ﷺ, rather than adjusting it to match the more familiar recitation order. The lesson here is about precision in transmission: a narrator's responsibility is to preserve the exact wording as received, not to silently correct it toward what sounds more expected, even when the correction would be harmless in content.

Question 1A person neglects zakah for years but never abandons the shahada. Another person recites the shahada outwardly while privately rejecting it. Using the tent analogy, explain the structural difference between these two situations.

Neglecting zakah is like losing a side stake: it weakens the tent and leaves it exposed, but the structure as a whole still stands, since the shahada, the central pole, remains intact. Privately rejecting the shahada while reciting it outwardly is a collapse of the central pole itself; nothing else in the structure can remain standing independently of it, no matter how well the other stakes are secured.

Question 2Someone completes every physical movement of the prayer correctly, in a state of ritual purity and facing the qiblah, but their mind wanders the entire time with no attentiveness to what they are doing. Which of the three levels does this satisfy, and which is missing?

This satisfies the external conditions (shurut), ritual purity and facing the qiblah, and the physical integrals (arkan), the correctly performed movements. What is missing is khushu', presence of heart, the inward spiritual attentiveness that gives the prayer its living quality. The prayer can still be legally valid on the first two levels while lacking the third.

Question 3A listener tells Ibn 'Umar that he must have misspoken, since everyone recites the fast of Ramadan before Hajj. Why does Ibn 'Umar refuse to change his wording, and what principle of hadith transmission does his refusal illustrate?

Ibn 'Umar refuses because he is reporting the exact order he heard directly from the Prophet ﷺ, and a narrator's duty is to preserve the wording as received, not to adjust it toward what sounds more familiar or expected. This illustrates the principle that fidelity to the source outweighs conformity to convention, even when the convention is harmless and the correction would change nothing in substance.
