Hadith 1: Actions Are Judged by Intentions

Session with Sidi Shaykh Muhyiddin Lashen.

Text
إِنَّمَا الْأَعْمَالُ بِالنِّيَّاتِ، وَإِنَّمَا لِكُلِّ امْرِئٍ مَا نَوَى، فَمَنْ كَانَتْ هِجْرَتُهُ إِلَى اللَّهِ وَرَسُولِهِ فَهِجْرَتُهُ إِلَى اللَّهِ وَرَسُولِهِ، وَمَنْ كَانَتْ هِجْرَتُهُ لِدُنْيَا يُصِيبُهَا أَوِ امْرَأَةٍ يَنْكِحُهَا فَهِجْرَتُهُ إِلَى مَا هَاجَرَ إِلَيْهِ.
Actions are only judged according to intentions, and every person will have only what he intended. So whoever emigrated for the sake of Allah and His Messenger, his emigration was for Allah and His Messenger, and whoever emigrated for worldly gain he might attain, or a woman he might marry, his emigration was for that which he emigrated for.

Narrated by Amir al-Mu'minin Abu Hafs 'Umar ibn al-Khattab; recorded by both al-Bukhari and Muslim in their respective Sahihs, the opening hadith of both collections and of this one.

The Emigration Illustration

The hadith does not stop at the general principle; it immediately grounds it in a concrete case. Hijrah, emigration for the sake of Allah and His Messenger, and hijrah undertaken for some worldly end, such as a business opportunity or a marriage, can look identical from the outside: the same physical relocation, the same route, the same destination. What separates one from the other entirely is the intention behind it. A person who outwardly performs hijrah but inwardly seeks only worldly gain has, in the terms of this hadith, only attained that worldly gain; the reward attached to hijrah for Allah's sake does not attach to it at all. The example was chosen specifically because it is a case where the external act gives no clue whatsoever about its true nature; only the intention decides it.

The Linguistics of "Innama"

Innama is not one particle but a compound of two: inna, used for emphasis, roughly "indeed" or "certainly," and ma, which here functions for exclusivity, "only." Combined, innama al-a'malu bi'l-niyyat means "actions are only, or exclusively, judged according to intentions." The exclusivity carried by ma is doing real work in this hadith: it is not merely saying intentions matter among other factors, it is restricting validity to intention specifically.

The Definition of Niyyah

Al-Niyyah (Intention), linguistically, refers to the determination of the heart: the underlying motive or purpose behind any action. In Islamic legal theory it is defined more precisely as "intending an action while simultaneously initiating it" (qasdu al-shay' muqtarinan bi'l-fi'l), tying the intention directly to the moment the act begins rather than to some earlier or later thought about it.

(1)

Performing prayer out of obligation versus performing the same physical motions out of habit illustrates the distinction: only the former, carrying the correct intention at the moment of initiation, is considered valid worship.

Why This Hadith Carries Such Weight

An identical physical act, giving charity, for instance, may be rewarded or not depending entirely on whether the intention behind it is sincere (for the sake of Allah) or self interested (for reputation). This is why the hadith is considered one third, or by some accounts even one half, of all Islamic knowledge: every human action divides into an internal component (the heart, its beliefs and intentions) and an external component (deeds and words), and this hadith governs the internal component that determines the value of everything external. The principle reaches across ritual practice, law, and ethics alike, not just acts of worship narrowly defined.

Mustalah Note: Is This Hadith Mutawatir?

Despite its fame and its position as the opening hadith of Sahih al-Bukhari, this hadith is classified as ahad: transmitted through a single chain for several generations before it reached a wider audience, not through the multiple independent chains tawatur requires. Its fame among later generations should not be mistaken for tawatur at the source; the two are judged by different things; see Mutawatir, Mashhur, Aziz, and Gharib for the full classification. As a related note: "Amir al-Mu'minin" was a title first officially carried by 'Umar, the narrator of this hadith, who died at the age of 63, the same age at which the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr also passed away.

Question 1Compare "al-a'malu bi'l-niyyat" (actions are by intentions) with "innama al-a'malu bi'l-niyyat" (actions are only by intentions). What single word creates the difference, and what would be lost theologically if the hadith had used the first phrasing instead?

The word "ma," combined with "inna" to form "innama," is what creates the difference; it supplies exclusivity ("only"). Without it, the hadith would merely say intentions are one factor among others relevant to an action's judgment, leaving room for an act to be valid partly on its own terms. With "innama," validity is restricted to intention specifically, which is exactly the stronger claim the hadith is making.

Question 2Two people perform the exact same physical motions of prayer side by side. One initiates it intending to fulfill the obligation of Dhuhr; the other is simply mimicking the movements out of habit, with no such intention at the moment he begins. Applying the legal definition of niyyah given here, does either performance count as valid worship?

Only the first counts as valid worship. Niyyah is defined as intending an action while simultaneously initiating it (qasdu al-shay' muqtarinan bi'l-fi'l); the first person meets this exactly, intending to fulfill Dhuhr at the moment he begins. The second person's identical physical motions carry no such intention at initiation, so despite looking the same from the outside, it does not count as worship at all under this definition.

Question 3Two people give the same amount of charity to the same cause. Using the "internal versus external" framework this hadith establishes, explain how their reward could differ even though the external act is identical.

Every action divides into an internal component, the heart's intention, and an external component, the deed itself. The external component, the money given, is identical for both. The internal component is what this hadith governs, and it can differ completely: one may give sincerely for the sake of Allah while the other gives for reputation. Since the hadith makes intention the determinant of an action's value, their reward differs entirely despite the outward act being the same, which is exactly why this single hadith is treated as governing so much of Islamic law and ethics at once.

Question 4This hadith opens Sahih al-Bukhari and is arguably the most famous hadith in the entire tradition. A student assumes fame at this level must mean it is mutawatir. Check this assumption against the actual chain, and explain what corrects for the gap if the assumption turns out wrong.

The assumption is wrong. Despite its fame and its place as the opening hadith of Sahih al-Bukhari, it is classified as ahad, transmitted through a single chain for several generations before reaching a wider audience, not through the multiple independent chains tawatur requires. What corrects for the gap is reception by the entire Muslim community without objection, which supplies the corroboration needed to elevate an ahad report like this one to practical certainty, even though it never meets the technical definition of mutawatir.
