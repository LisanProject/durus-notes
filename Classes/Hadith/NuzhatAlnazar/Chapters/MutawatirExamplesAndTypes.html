Real Examples of Mutawatir, and Its Two Forms

Mutawatir Lafzi and Mutawatir Ma'nawi

Once a report is established as mutawatir, it can still be mutawatir in either of two ways, depending on whether the wording itself or only the underlying meaning is what reaches us through every chain.

1. Mutawatir Lafzi (Verbal Tawatur): the exact wording of the report is transmitted identically through every one of the numerous chains. This is the stricter and rarer of the two forms, since it requires that the large number of independent narrators agree not just on the content but on the precise phrasing.

(1)

2. Mutawatir Ma'nawi (Tawatur of Meaning): the wording differs somewhat from chain to chain, but every chain converges on the same underlying meaning or event. This form is considerably more common than mutawatir lafzi, since large numbers of independent narrators are far more likely to agree on what happened than on the exact words used to describe it.

(2)

Real Examples from the Hadith Literature

These examples are useful precisely because they show tawatur is not just a theoretical category, but a real property some hadith actually have, verifiable by counting the independent chains a report was carried through.

The Hadith of the Prophet's Basin (al-Hawd): transmitted by around fifty Companions, describing the basin the Prophet ﷺ will stand at on the Day of Judgment.

(1)

The Hadith on Wiping Over Leather Socks (al-Mash 'ala al-Khuffayn): transmitted by around seventy Companions, establishing the permissibility of wiping over leather socks during wudu' rather than removing them.

(2)

The Hadith on the Qur'an Being Revealed in Seven Modes (Sab'at Ahruf): transmitted by around twenty-seven Companions.

(3)

In each case, the large number of independent Companion-narrators is exactly what the conditions of tawatur are checking for: a number wide enough that ordinary custom rules out the possibility of collusion or coincidental agreement on a fabrication.

Technical Mashhur vs. Ordinary Fame

A hadith being widely known or frequently quoted, whether among ordinary people or even among jurists, does not by itself make it mashhur in the technical mustalah sense, and it certainly does not make it authentic. These are two separate questions entirely: how socially familiar a report is, and what its actual chain of transmission can support.

"Haste Is from the Shaytan" is extremely well known in everyday speech, but its chain is actually weak (da'if). Its popularity tracks nothing about its technical grade.

(1)

"The Most Disliked of Permissible Things to Allah Is Divorce" is well known specifically among the fuqaha (jurists), who cite it constantly in discussions of divorce, but it does not meet the technical criteria to be classified as mashhur in the science of hadith.

(2)

The lesson to hold onto: fame in general usage, or even fame within a specialized discipline like fiqh, is a sociological fact about how often a report gets quoted. Technical mashhur is a claim about the actual structure of the chain, specifically that three or more narrators carry it at every single generation. The two can come apart in either direction, a report can be technically mashhur without being a household phrase, or a household phrase without being technically mashhur, or even without being authentic at all.

Question 1A hadith is transmitted with the exact same wording, word for word, by sixty independent narrators at every generation. Another hadith describing the same event is transmitted by sixty independent narrators as well, but each one phrases the report somewhat differently while agreeing on what happened. Classify each as mutawatir lafzi or mutawatir ma'nawi.

The first is mutawatir lafzi, since the exact wording itself is what reaches us identically through every chain. The second is mutawatir ma'nawi, since the wording varies from chain to chain while every chain converges on the same underlying meaning or event, which is the more common of the two forms.

Question 2A student says: "Haste is from the Shaytan" must be at least a decent hadith, since everyone quotes it and it sounds exactly like something the Prophet ﷺ would say. What is wrong with this reasoning?

The reasoning confuses social familiarity with chain-based grading. How often a report is quoted, or how plausible it sounds, says nothing about the actual reliability of its narrators. In this specific case, "Haste is from the Shaytan" is extremely well known in everyday speech, but its actual chain is weak (da'if). Popularity and authenticity are independent variables.

Question 3A hadith is cited constantly by jurists in fiqh discussions of a particular ruling, to the point that every student of fiqh knows it by heart. A classmate assumes this automatically makes it technically mashhur. Is the classmate necessarily right?

Not necessarily. "The most disliked of permissible things to Allah is divorce" is exactly this case: well known among the fuqaha and cited constantly in divorce rulings, yet it does not meet the technical criteria for mashhur in the science of hadith. Fame within a specialized discipline is still a separate question from whether three or more narrators actually carry the report at every generation of its chain.
