Scholarly Integrity, Ijazah, and the Chain of Transmission

Teaching and Authorial Respect

There is a real contradiction in teaching a text while consistently disparaging its author's fundamental beliefs, such as their aqidah. Teaching cannot amount to constant opposition to the very source being taught; if it does, the authority of the text and the chain of learning (sanad) behind it are undermined at the root.

What Ijazah Actually Requires

Al-Ijazah (Authorization) is more than bare permission to teach or transmit a text. It affirms a chain of understanding, intellectual and not only literal, linking the student back to the original author. A valid ijazah requires broad agreement in belief, method, and intention. Where constant disagreement or disparagement exists instead, the ijazah that results is only "literal," conveying the words of the text without conveying the actual knowledge behind them.

Multidisciplinary Mastery

Classical scholars, such as those from the Subki family or Ibn Rushd al-Jadd, were routinely authorities across multiple disciplines at once, fiqh, hadith, and aqidah among them. The absence of a specific book by a given scholar in one of those sciences does not imply a lack of expertise in it. Imam al-Nawawi is a perfect example of this. He never authored a standalone aqidah text, but his breadth in the subject was well acknowledged by his peers regardless.

Question 1A teacher has a genuine, unbroken chain of narration back to a book's author, and technically has permission to transmit it, but spends every session telling students the author's core beliefs are wrong. Does this teacher's chain of transmission still carry real weight? Explain using the distinction this section draws.

No, not in the fuller sense. Teaching cannot amount to constant opposition to the very source being taught without undermining the authority of the text and the chain of learning (sanad) behind it at the root. This teacher may hold a literal chain of permission, but disparaging the author's fundamental beliefs while presenting himself as a transmitter of the work is a direct contradiction, exactly the case this section warns against.

Question 2Compare two ijazahs for the same book: one from a teacher who agrees with the author in belief, method, and intention, and one from a teacher who has permission to narrate the words but rejects much of what the author teaches. What is the practical difference between these two ijazahs?

The first ijazah affirms a real intellectual chain of understanding back to the author, not just a chain of words, since it rests on broad agreement in belief, method, and intention. The second is only "literal": it conveys permission to repeat the words without conveying the actual knowledge and understanding behind them, since that underlying agreement is missing.

Question 3Does the absence of a standalone aqidah text by Imam al-Nawawi imply he lacked expertise in aqidah?

No. Classical scholars were routinely authorities across multiple disciplines at once, and the absence of a specific book in a given science does not imply a lack of expertise in it. Al-Nawawi's breadth in aqidah was well acknowledged by his peers even without a dedicated text on the subject.
