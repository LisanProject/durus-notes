Scholarly Integrity, Ijazah, and the Chain of Transmission

Session with Sidi Shaykh Muhyiddin Lashen.

Teaching and Authorial Respect

There is a real contradiction in teaching a text while consistently disparaging its author's fundamental beliefs, such as their aqidah. Teaching cannot amount to constant opposition to the very source being taught; if it does, the authority of the text and the chain of learning (sanad) behind it are undermined at the root.

What Ijazah Actually Requires

Al-Ijazah (Authorization) is more than bare permission to teach or transmit a text. It affirms a chain of understanding, intellectual and not only literal, linking the student back to the original author. A valid ijazah requires broad agreement in belief, method, and intention. Where constant disagreement or disparagement exists instead, the ijazah that results is only "literal," conveying the words of the text without conveying the actual knowledge behind them.

(1)

An analogy makes the point concrete: certifying a math teacher who openly disagrees with the main results of the textbook on every page would produce a questionable certification. Students in that position cannot actually trust the chain of authority back to the core conventions the textbook is meant to transmit, even if the teacher is technically permitted to hand them the book.

Multidisciplinary Mastery

Classical scholars, such as those from the Subki family or Ibn Rushd al-Jadd, were routinely authorities across multiple disciplines at once, fiqh, hadith, and aqidah among them. The absence of a specific book by a given scholar in one of those sciences does not imply a lack of expertise in it; Imam al-Nawawi, for instance, never authored a standalone aqidah text, but his breadth in the subject was well acknowledged by his peers regardless.

Question 1Why is it considered problematic for a teacher to teach a book while consistently criticizing its author's core beliefs?

Teaching cannot amount to constant opposition to the very source being taught without undermining the authority of the text and the chain of learning (sanad) behind it at the root. The contradiction is direct: disparaging the author's fundamental beliefs while presenting yourself as a transmitter of their work.

Question 2What does a valid ijazah require beyond literal permission to narrate a text, and what happens when that requirement is not met?

A valid ijazah requires broad agreement in belief, method, and intention, affirming an intellectual chain of understanding back to the original author, not merely a literal chain of words. Where constant disagreement or disparagement exists instead, the resulting ijazah is only "literal," conveying the words without conveying the actual knowledge behind them.

Question 3Does the absence of a standalone aqidah text by Imam al-Nawawi imply he lacked expertise in aqidah?

No. Classical scholars were routinely authorities across multiple disciplines at once, and the absence of a specific book in a given science does not imply a lack of expertise in it. Al-Nawawi's breadth in aqidah was well acknowledged by his peers even without a dedicated text on the subject.
