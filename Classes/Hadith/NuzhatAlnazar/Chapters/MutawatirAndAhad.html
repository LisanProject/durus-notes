Mutawatir, Mashhur, Aziz, and Gharib

Definition of Mutawatir

Al-Mutawatir is a report transmitted through so many independent chains that it is absolutely inconceivable for all of the transmitters to have conspired on a falsehood. Such a report produces certain, definitive knowledge ('ilm yaqini, also called 'ilm daruri) in the mind of the one who receives it, compelling assent without any need for further rational inference, the same way a mathematical axiom or a universally evident truth is accepted.

(1)

The Four Conditions of Mutawatir

These four conditions are what to actually check before calling a report mutawatir.

فإذا جُمِعَ هذه الشروط الأربعة وهي: (١) عدد كثير أحالت العادة تواطؤهم أو توافقهم على الكذب. (٢) رَوَوا ذلك عن مثلهم من الابتداء إلى الانتهاء. (٣) وكان مستندُ انتهائهم الحسّ. (٤) وانضاف إلى ذلك أن يصحب خبرهم إفادة العلم لسامعه، فهذا هو المتواتر.
If these four conditions come together: (1) a large number whose agreement or collusion on a lie custom rules out; (2) they narrated it from others like themselves from the beginning to the end; (3) the basis of their report at its end is sense perception; and (4) added to that, their report is accompanied by producing knowledge for the one who hears it, then this is the mutawatir.

Ibn Hajar, Nuzhat al-Nazar, on the conditions of tawatur.

1. Kathra (Large Number): the report must be narrated by a substantial group of narrators, not just one or two.

(1)

2. Impossibility of Collusion: it must be inconceivable, by ordinary custom (al-'adah), for all of those narrators to have agreed together on a lie.

(2)

3. Continuity Across Generations: that same multiplicity of narrators must hold at every single layer (tabaqa) of the chain, from the original source all the way to the person receiving the report, not just at one point in the chain.

(3)

4. Basis in Sense Perception: the content of the report must be grounded in something directly witnessed, seen or heard, rather than in a rational or theoretical proposition someone reasoned their way to.

(4)

Some scholars proposed specific numeric thresholds for the "large number" condition, four, five, seven, or ten, drawing analogies from other legal standards, such as the number of witnesses required in a case of hadd zina, or the number of oaths required in li'an. Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani and other authorities argue against fixing the count this precisely: the exact number narrating is less important than whether the report actually succeeds in producing certainty in the mind of the recipient. A number that produces certainty in one case might not in another, depending on context, so the numeric thresholds are treated as practical guides at most, not as a strict legal requirement.

Ahad and Its Subtypes

A report that falls short of the mutawatir threshold is called ahad (solitary). By itself, an ahad report yields only probable knowledge (zann), not certainty, however reliable its individual narrators are. Ahad splits further into three subtypes, graded by how many narrators carry the report at any given stage, and it is worth being precise about what actually separates them, since the three are easy to blur together.

وهو أوّلُ أقسامِ الآحادِ - ما لَهُ طرقٌ محصورةٌ بأكثرَ من اثنينِ، وهو المَشْهورُ عندَ المُحَدِّثينَ. والثَّالثُ: العَزيزُ، وهو أنْ لا يَرْوِيَهُ أقلُّ من اثنينِ عن اثنينِ. والرَّابعُ: الغَريبُ، وهو ما يَنْفَرِدُ بروايَتِهِ شخصٌ واحدٌ في أيِّ موضعٍ وقَعَ التفرُّدُ به مِنَ السَّنَدِ.
The first division of ahad is what has channels limited to more than two, and this is al-mashhur among the muhaddithun. The third is al-aziz, which no fewer than two narrate from two. The fourth is al-gharib, which a single person narrates alone, at whatever point in the chain that solitary narration occurs.

Ibn Hajar, Nuzhat al-Nazar, on the divisions of ahad.

1. Mashhur (Well-Known) is narrated by three or more narrators at every generation, but without meeting the further conditions required for tawatur, such as full continuity of that multiplicity across every single layer or the practical impossibility of collusion.

(1)

2. Aziz (Rare) is narrated by exactly two narrators at some stage in the chain, no more and no fewer.

(2)

3. Gharib (Strange, Singular) is narrated by only one person at any given stage in the chain.

(3)

The Golden Rule: the lowest number of narrators at any single level in the chain determines the classification of the entire report, not the highest. If ten people narrate a report at one generation but only one person narrates it at another generation, the whole chain is classified as gharib, since that one narrow point is where the chain is actually weakest.

(4)

Ibn Hajar's own commentary gives a striking real example of just how counterintuitive this can look: the Hadith of Intentions, "innama al-a'malu bi'l-niyyat," which opens both Sahih al-Bukhari and Al-Arba'in al-Nawawiyyah, was delivered by 'Umar as a khutbah from the pulpit before a large gathering of Companions. Despite that public setting, the chain narrating it is gharib at its origin: only 'Alqamah is known to have transmitted it from 'Umar, then only Muhammad ibn Ibrahim from 'Alqamah, then only Yahya ibn Sa'id from Muhammad, before it finally widens. A hadith can be publicly delivered before hundreds and still be gharib in mustalah terms, because the classification tracks the chain of transmission, not the size of the original audience. (See: Hadith 1: Actions Are Judged by Intentions)

How Mutawatir and Mashhur Relate

Mutawatir Implies Mashhur, Not the Reverse: every mutawatir report is necessarily mashhur, since it is by definition widely transmitted. Not every mashhur report reaches the level of mutawatir, though, because a report can be widely circulated at later generations while tracing back to only one or two narrators at some earlier point, which fails the continuity condition even though it looks widespread on the surface.

(1)

A report can also be technically ahad in its chain, such as gharib at one narrow point, and still be elevated to practical certainty through strong corroborating evidence, most notably reception by the entire Muslim community (talaqqi al-ummah bi al-qabul) without serious objection, or clear scholarly consensus (ijma') affirming its soundness. This is exactly why hadiths found in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim are generally treated with the strongest assurance of authenticity available short of tawatur itself, even though many individual hadiths within them are technically ahad in their chains: the community's reception supplies what the isolated chain alone could not.

Question 1A report is narrated by forty companions in the first generation, thirty tabi'in in the second, and twenty five in the third, all in different cities with no contact with each other, and it reports something the narrators directly witnessed the Prophet ﷺ doing. Is this mutawatir? Check it against all four conditions.

Yes. Kathra is satisfied at every generation (forty, then thirty, then twenty five, all well above what any scholar would dispute). Impossibility of collusion is satisfied since the narrators are spread across different cities with no contact, making coordination on a lie inconceivable. Continuity is satisfied since the large number holds at every layer, not just at the source. Basis in sense perception is satisfied since the narrators directly witnessed the act rather than reasoning their way to it. All four conditions are met, so this is mutawatir.

Question 2A report has exactly six narrators at every single generation from the Companion down to the compiler. A student says "six is fewer than ten, so this cannot be mutawatir." Is the student's reasoning correct?

No. Ibn Hajar and other authorities hold that no specific number is a strict requirement; what matters is whether the report actually produces certainty in the mind of the recipient. The numeric thresholds some scholars proposed are practical guides only. Six independent narrators at every generation, with no possibility of collusion, could well produce that certainty; the student's fixed cutoff is exactly the mistake this section warns against.

Question 3A report has fifteen narrators at the Companion generation, twelve at the generation after, but only two narrators at the third generation, then twenty again at the fourth. How is this report classified overall, and which of the four conditions does it fail?

The Golden Rule governs this: the lowest number of narrators at any single generation determines the classification of the whole chain, not the highest or the average. Since only two narrators carry it at the third generation, the report is classified as aziz overall, regardless of how many narrate it elsewhere. It fails the continuity condition for mutawatir, since the required multiplicity has to hold at every layer, not just most of them.

Question 4Two reports are both narrated by exactly two narrators at one generation. Report A has three or more narrators at every other generation. Report B has only one narrator at every other generation. Classify each report.

By the Golden Rule, the weakest generation decides the classification. Report A's weakest point is exactly two narrators, so it is classified as aziz. Report B's weakest point is a single narrator, so despite having the same two-narrator generation as Report A, it is classified as gharib. Having one strong generation does not rescue a chain whose weakest link is a single narrator.

Question 5A report is narrated by four or more narrators at every generation for three generations, then abruptly narrows to a single narrator at the fourth and final generation before reaching the compiler. A student calls this "mashhur, and therefore also mutawatir, since mutawatir implies mashhur." What is wrong with this reasoning?

The implication only runs one direction: every mutawatir report is mashhur, but not every mashhur report is mutawatir. This report is indeed mashhur, given three or more narrators at several generations, but by the Golden Rule its single narrator at the final generation makes the whole chain gharib, not mutawatir. The student has the relationship backward, treating the weaker category (mashhur) as if it guaranteed the stronger one (mutawatir).

Question 6A hadith is narrated by only one reliable narrator at one point in its chain, yet it is found in Sahih al-Bukhari and accepted by the whole Muslim community without objection. Can it be used as a basis for a matter of creed?

Yes. Although the report is technically ahad, even gharib, in its chain, reception by the community (talaqqi al-ummah bi al-qabul) supplies the corroboration needed to elevate it to practical certainty. Its presence in Sahih al-Bukhari combined with unbroken communal acceptance is treated as sufficient for the certainty a matter of creed requires.
