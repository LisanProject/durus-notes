Islamic Epistemology: Naqli and Aqli Knowledge

Session with Sidi Shaykh Muhyiddin Lashen.

Two Categories of Knowledge

1. Naqli (Transmitted) Knowledge is information acquired through reliable transmission, such as reports, narrations, and documented traditions, hadith and historical records among them.

(1)

2. 'Aqli (Intellectual) Knowledge is gleaned through reason, reflection, and direct intellectual effort, the way mathematics and logic are.

(2)

Criteria for Accepting Transmitted Knowledge

Transmitted knowledge is not judged by pure reason, personal emotion, or contemporary cultural standards. Its authenticity depends instead on the reliability and veracity of its chain of transmission (isnad). Abu Bakr al-Siddiq's unwavering acceptance of the Prophet's report about the Night Journey (al-Isra' wal-Mi'raj), based solely on his confidence in the truthful source rather than on independent verification of the event itself, is held up as the model for this kind of acceptance.

This cuts the other way too: modern trends that reject sound, well-established narrations, such as objections raised against the hadith describing 'A'isha's marriage, based on subjective or emotional discomfort rather than on the actual process of verifying transmission, are criticized on the same grounds. The correct basis for accepting or rejecting a report is proof about the reliability of its transmission from a credible source, not personal opinion or the standards of the surrounding culture.

Question 1Explain the two main types of knowledge in Islamic epistemology. Give an example of each.

Naqli (transmitted) knowledge is acquired through reliable transmission, such as hadith and historical records. 'Aqli (intellectual) knowledge is gleaned through reason and direct intellectual effort, the way mathematics and logic are.

Question 2Why is it incorrect, from an Islamic methodological perspective, to judge transmitted reports by personal emotion or pure reason alone?

The authenticity of transmitted knowledge depends on the reliability and veracity of its chain of transmission (isnad), not on whether it fits personal reasoning, emotional comfort, or contemporary cultural standards. Judging it by those other criteria abandons the actual methodology built to evaluate it.

Question 3How did Abu Bakr al-Siddiq's response to the report of the Isra' and Mi'raj exemplify the correct acceptance of transmitted reports?

He accepted the report unwaveringly based solely on his confidence in the truthfulness of the source, the Prophet ﷺ, rather than requiring independent verification of the event itself. That is the model: authenticity judged by reliability of transmission, not by whether the content seems plausible on its own.
