Liban's Online Notes

Tuhfat al-Atfal

تحفة الأطفال في تجويد القرآن · The Gift for Children on the Tajwid of the Qur'an

Sulayman al-Jamzuri, a 61-line didactic poem on the foundational rules of Tajwid, composed 1198 AH

Notes on This Section

Tuhfat al-Atfal is the standard entry point into formal Tajwid study, despite its title. The name means "The Gift for Children," but this describes its accessibility, not its audience. Beginners of every age typically start here, and even advanced reciters return to it as a review of the core rules before moving on to more detailed texts like al-Jazariyyah.

Al-Jamzuri wrote the poem so its rulings could be memorized as verse and recalled line by line whenever a student forgets a rule mid-recitation. That is the same reason these notes keep the original Arabic of each line front and center rather than treating it as decoration; the verse itself is the tool, and the commentary exists to unpack what it already contains.

Tajwid is fundamentally an oral science. These notes can explain a rule and show where it applies, but they cannot correct your makhraj or your ghunnah. A qualified teacher with a connected chain of transmission (isnad) is not optional for this subject, and the notes below should be read as a companion to that instruction, not a substitute for it.

وَمَا تَوْفِيقِى إِلَّا بِٱللَّهِ ۚ عَلَيْهِ تَوَكَّلْتُ وَإِلَيْهِ أُنِيبُ

And my success comes only through Allah. In Him I trust and to Him I turn.

Chapters

Here is a list of the chapters covered in this book.

Chapter 1: Introduction: The opening lines of the poem, its title and purpose, and a short note on the author and his teacher.

Chapter 2: Rules of the Noon Sakinah and Tanween: The four rulings that govern a silent noon or tanween depending on the letter that follows it: idhhar, idgham, iqlab, and ikhfa'.

Chapter 3: Rules of the Meem Sakinah: The three rulings that govern a silent meem: ikhfa' shafawi, idgham shafawi, and izhar shafawi.

Chapter 4: The Lam of Al and the Lam of the Verb: Lam qamariyyah and lam shamsiyyah for the definite article, and why the lam inside a verb follows a completely different rule.

Chapter 5: Mithlayn, Mutajanisayn, and Mutaqaribayn: The three degrees of closeness between any two adjacent letters, and how each splits further into a minor and major form.

Chapter 6: The Types of Madd: Madd asli (natural), the three letters of madd and their conditions, and the related category of madd al-leen.

Chapter 7: The Rulings of the Madd: Madd wajib, madd jaiz (munfasil, 'arid li-l-sukun, and badal), and madd lazim.

Chapter 8: The Types of Madd Lazim: Kilmi and harfi, mukhaffaf and muthaqqal, and a full working through the huruf muqatta'at that open certain surahs.

Chapter 9: Conclusion of the Poem: The closing praise and salutations, and the abjad calculation encoding the poem's verse count and date of composition.

All 61 verses of the poem are now covered above.
