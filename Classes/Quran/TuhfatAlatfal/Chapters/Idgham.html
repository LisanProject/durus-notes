Idgham الإدغام

Text
وَالثَّانِيْ إِدْغَامٌ بِسِتَّةٍ أَتَتْ *** فِيْ يَرْمَلُوْنَ عِنْدَهُمْ قَدْ ثَبَتَتْ
لَكِنَّهُ قِسْمَانِ: قِسْمٌ يُدْغَمَا *** فِيْهِ بِغُنَّةٍ بِيَنْمُوْ عُلِمَا
إِلَّا إِذَا كَانَ بِكِلْمَةٍ فَلَا *** تُدْغَمْ كَدُنْيَا ثُمَّ صِنْوَانٍ تَلَا
وَالثَّانِيْ إِدْغَامٌ بِغَيْرِ غُنَّةِ *** فِي اللَّامِ وَالرَّا ثُمَّ كَرِّرَنَّهُ
Vocabulary
  • الْإِدْغَامassimilation; literally "insertion," merging the noon sakinah or tanween into the following letter
  • يَرْمَلُوْنَthe mnemonic word gathering all six idgham letters: ya, ra, mim, lam, waw, noon
  • بِغُنَّةٍwith nasalization; the ghunnah sound is retained through the merge
  • يَنْمُوْthe mnemonic word gathering the four idgham-with-ghunnah letters: ya, noon, mim, waw
  • بِكِلْمَةٍwithin a single word; the condition under which idgham is blocked and idhhar mutlaq applies instead
  • بِغَيْرِ غُنَّةٍwithout nasalization; the merge is complete, with no nasal trace remaining
Translation
The second ruling is idgham, with six letters gathered in "yarmaluna," established among the scholars. But it has two types: one type is merged with ghunnah, gathered in "yanmu," known (as such), unless it occurs within a single word, in which case it is not merged, as in "dunya" and "sinwan" that follows. The second type is merged without ghunnah, with lam and ra, so repeat it (i.e., apply it consistently).
Commentary

Idgham literally means "insertion," the same word used for fitting a bit into a horse's mouth or a body into a grave; here it describes the noon sakinah or tanween being absorbed into the letter that follows so completely that the two sounds flow together as one.

Idgham merges a noon sakinah or tanween with the following letter, so that the noon or tanween is not pronounced separately at all. Six letters trigger idgham, split into two groups depending on whether the ghunnah (nasal hum) is kept through the merge.

(2)
The Two Types of Idgham
TypeLettersMnemonic
Idgham with ghunnahي ن م ويَنْمُوْ
Idgham without ghunnahل ر—

Within idgham with ghunnah there is a further distinction reciters learn early: with noon and meem the merge is "complete" (kamil), losing the noon sound entirely and marked in the mushaf with a shaddah; with ya and waw the merge is "incomplete" (naqis), where the ghunnah is still audible even though no shaddah is written. Idgham without ghunnah, by contrast, is always a full and complete merge into lam or ra, with no nasal quality left over at all.

The Four Exceptions (Idhhar Mutlaq): If a noon sakinah and one of the idgham letters occur together inside a single word rather than across two separate words, idgham does not apply, and the noon is pronounced clearly instead. This happens in only four words in the entire Qur'an: دُنْيَا, صِنْوَانٌ, قِنْوَانٌ (and its plural أَقْنَاءٌ), and بُنْيَانٌ. This exception is called idhhar mutlaq, "absolute clarification," to distinguish it from idhhar halqi in the previous section.

(3)

Here are worked examples of both idgham types, plus the single-word exception.

Example 1 (Idgham with ghunnah, ya)Identify the noon sakinah or tanween, the letter it merges with, and whether ghunnah is retained.مَن يَقُولُ

"whoever says"

The noon sakinah in مَنْ merges into the ya of يَقُولُ. Ya belongs to the ghunnah group (yanmu), so the noon disappears as a separate sound but its nasal quality carries into the ya.

Example 2 (Idgham with ghunnah, mim, complete merge)Identify the noon sakinah or tanween, the letter it merges with, and whether ghunnah is retained.مِن مَّاءٍ

"from water"

The noon sakinah in مِنْ merges fully into the meem of مَّاءٍ. Since meem and noon are the "complete" idgham letters, the result is a single elongated, fully nasalized meem, usually marked with a shaddah in the mushaf.

Example 3 (Idgham without ghunnah, lam)Identify the noon sakinah or tanween, the letter it merges with, and whether ghunnah is retained.مِن لَّدُنْهُ

"from Him" / "from His presence"

The noon sakinah in مِنْ merges into the lam of لَّدُنْهُ. Since lam belongs to the without-ghunnah group, the merge is complete with no nasal trace: the result is simply a doubled lam sound.

Example 4 (Idgham without ghunnah, ra)Identify the noon sakinah or tanween, the letter it merges with, and whether ghunnah is retained.مِن رَّبِّهِمْ

"from their Lord"

The noon sakinah in مِنْ merges into the ra of رَّبِّهِمْ, again with no ghunnah, since ra is in the without-ghunnah group alongside lam.

Example 5 (The exception: idhhar mutlaq)Explain why idgham does not apply here even though noon meets a ya, one of the idgham letters.الدُّنْيَا

"the worldly life"

The noon sakinah and the ya both occur inside the single word دُنْيَا, not across two separate words. This is one of the four words in the entire Qur'an where idgham is blocked and idhhar mutlaq applies instead: the noon is pronounced clearly, exactly as it is spelled, with no merging into the ya at all.

Example phrases above are illustrative of the idgham letters and their two subtypes rather than citations of specific ayat; always verify wording and application against the mushaf and a qualified reciter.
