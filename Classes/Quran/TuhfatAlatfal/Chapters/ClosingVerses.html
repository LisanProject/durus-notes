Closing Verses and the Abjad Calculation خاتمة التحفة

Text
وَتَمَّ ذَا النَّظْمُ بِحَمْدِ اللَّهِ عَلَى تَمَامِهِ بِلَا تَنَاهِي
Vocabulary
  • وَتَمَّcompleted, ended
  • هَذَا النَّظْمُthis poem, this text
  • بِحَمْدِ اللَّهِwith the praise of Allah
  • تَمَامِهِits completion
  • بِلَا تَنَاهِيwithout end, continuous
Translation
And this text is completed with the continuous praise of Allah on its completion.
Commentary

After explaining every rule he intended to cover, al-Jamzuri closes the poem exactly as he opened it: praising and thanking Allah, since nothing is accomplished except through His will and support.

Text
أَبْيَاتُهَا نَدٌّ بَدَا لِذِي النُّهَى تَارِيخُهَا بُشْرَى لِمَنْ يُتْقِنُهَا
Vocabulary
  • أَبْيَاتُهَاits verses
  • نَدٌّ بَدَاclear incense
  • لِذِي النُّهَىfor him who has intellect
  • تَارِيخُهَاits date (of compilation)
  • بُشْرَى لِمَنْ يُتْقِنُهَاglad tidings for him who perfects/masters it
Translation
Its verses are clear incense for him who has intellect. Its date (of compilation) is glad tidings for him who masters it.
Commentary

Al-Jamzuri likens the knowledge in this poem to sweet incense: just as a person enjoys and is refreshed by a pleasant fragrance, so too will a student benefit from grasping what this text contains. He then hints that this very line encodes two numbers using the abjad system, a classical method of assigning a numerical value to every Arabic letter.

Abjad Encoding: adding up the numeral value of each letter in "nadd bada" (ن + د + ة + د + ا) gives 50 + 4 + 2 + 4 + 1 = 61, the exact number of verses in the poem. Adding up "bushra liman yutqinuha" gives a total of 1198, the year (AH) the poem was composed.

(1)

In some printed editions, this verse is treated as the poem's actual final line, with the two remaining verses of salutation appended as a closing supplication rather than counted among the 61.

Text
ثُمَّ الصَّلَاةُ وَالسَّلَامُ أَبَدَا عَلَى خِتَامِ الْأَنْبِيَاءِ أَحْمَدَا
وَآلِهِ وَصَحْبِهِ وَكُلِّ تَابِعِ وَكُلِّ قَارِئٍ وَكُلِّ سَامِعِ
Vocabulary
  • الصَّلَاةُ وَالسَّلَامُsalutations and peace
  • خِتَامِ الْأَنْبِيَاءِthe seal of the Prophets
  • أَحْمَدَاAhmad; another name for the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him
  • وَآلِهِ وَصَحْبِهِand his family and his companions
  • كُلِّ تَابِعِevery follower
  • كُلِّ قَارِئٍ وَكُلِّ سَامِعِevery reciter and every listener (of the Qur'an)
Translation
Then salutations and peace always upon the seal of the Prophets, Ahmad, and his family, his companions, and every follower, and every reciter and every listener (of the Qur'an).
Commentary

The poem's final act mirrors its opening: it began with Salawat upon the Prophet, and it ends the same way, extended now to his family, his companions, and everyone who follows in their path, whether by reciting the Qur'an correctly or simply by listening to it recited. This closing circle, from the author's own hope for Allah's mercy in the first line, to a shared blessing for every reciter and listener in the last, frames the entire poem as an act of worship rather than a purely technical manual.

Verses, vocabulary, translation, and the abjad calculation above are checked against Saleem Gaibie's Sharh Tuhfat al-Atfal (Madbut Writers and Translators, 3rd printing, 2014); commentary is paraphrased rather than quoted.
