Saghir and Kabir الصغير والكبير

Text
بِالْمُتَجَانِسَيْنِ ثُمَّ إِنْ سَكَنْ أَوَّلُ كُلٍّ فَالصَّغِيرَ سَمِّيَنْ
Vocabulary
  • إِنْ سَكَنْif it has a sukun
  • أَوَّلُ كُلٍّthe first of each (of the three groups)
  • فَالصَّغِيرَ سَمِّيَنْthen be sure to name it minor
Translation
Then if the first of each (of the above-mentioned groups) has a sukun, then be sure to name it minor (idgham saghir).
Commentary

Each of the three relationships from the previous section, mithlayn, mutajanisayn, and mutaqaribayn, further splits into two forms depending on the vowel status of the two letters involved. If the first letter is sakin (still) and the second is vowelled, the pair is "saghir" (small/minor), and idgham is made.

Saghir (Minor): the first of the two related letters carries no vowel and the second does. In this state, idgham is made across all three relationships.

(4)
Text
أَوْ حُرِّكَ الْحَرْفَانِ فِي كُلٍّ فَقُلْ كُلٌّ كَبِيرٌ وَافْهَمَنْهُ بِالْمُثُلْ
Vocabulary
  • حُرِّكَ الْحَرْفَانِthe two letters have a harakah (vowel)
  • كَبِيرٌbig/major
  • افْهَمَنْهُ بِالْمُثُلْunderstand it with examples
Translation
Or the two letters have a harakah in each (of these three groups), then say all (of them) are major (kabir), and understand it with examples.
Commentary

If both letters in the pair are vowelled (mutaharrik), the pair is "kabir" (large/major) instead. Kabir occurs far less often in the Qur'an than saghir, and while some reciters allow idgham in specific kabir cases through certain riwayat (notably in the reading of Abu 'Amr), the default and by far the more common ruling is izhar: the two letters are pronounced separately.

Kabir (Major): both of the two related letters carry a vowel. This form is read with izhar as the general rule, with idgham as a narration-specific exception found in particular riwayat rather than the standard reading most students learn first.

(5)

The two verses together give a simple test: look at the first letter of the pair. If it is still, apply idgham (saghir). If both letters carry a vowel, the pair is kabir and the default is izhar. Al-Jamzuri's closing instruction, to understand this "with examples," is a reminder that this entire chapter is more of a classification system than a set of new pronunciation techniques: the actual mechanics of idgham were already covered under the noon sakinah and meem sakinah earlier in the poem.

Verses, vocabulary, and translation above are checked against Saleem Gaibie's Sharh Tuhfat al-Atfal (Madbut Writers and Translators, 3rd printing, 2014); commentary is paraphrased rather than quoted.
