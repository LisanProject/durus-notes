The Opening of the Poem

The Author's Introduction مطلع الناظم

Text
يَقُوْلُ رَاجِي رَحْمَةِ الْغَفُوْرِ *** دَوْمًا سُلَيْمَانُ هُوَ الْجَمْزُوْرِي
Vocabulary
  • يَقُوْلُhe says; the poet speaking of himself in the third person, a common device in classical didactic poetry
  • رَاجِيone who hopes for; hoping for something attainable, as distinct from tamanni, wishing for something remote or unattainable
  • رَحْمَةِ الْغَفُوْرِthe mercy of the Oft-Forgiving; a name of Allah paired here with the hoped-for mercy
  • دَوْمًاalways, constantly; a poetic form of da'iman, describing the constancy of his hope
  • الْجَمْزُوْرِيal-Jamzuri; the author's nisbah, an attribution to his home village of Janzur
Translation
He who always hopes for the mercy of the Oft-Forgiving says: I am Sulayman al-Jamzuri.
Commentary

Al-Jamzuri opens by naming himself, a standard convention in classical Islamic scholarship that lets later students trace exactly who wrote what they are studying. He does so through the lens of hope in Allah's mercy rather than by simply stating his name outright, which sets the tone of humility expected from someone teaching sacred material.

Raja' vs. Tamanni: Arabic distinguishes between raja' (hoping for something attainable, within reasonable expectation) and tamanni (wishing for something remote, difficult, or effectively out of reach, whether or not it is truly impossible). Al-Jamzuri describes himself with raja', since Allah's forgiveness for a sincere believer is something realistically attainable, not a distant wish held with no real expectation of it coming to pass.

(1)

Al-Jamzuri is a nisbah, an attribution to a place of origin, following the same pattern as "al-Bukhari" (attributed to Bukhara). Al-Jamzuri was born in Tanta, Egypt, and takes his nisbah from his home village of Janzur. He studied Tajwid under Shaykh Nooruddin Ali al-Mihi, whom he honors later in the poem.

Praise of Allah and the Prophet ﷺ الحمد والصلاة

Text
الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ مُصَلِّيًا عَلَى *** مُحَمَّدٍ وَآلِهِ وَمَنْ تَلَا
Vocabulary
  • الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِall praise is due to Allah; the Hamdalah
  • مُصَلِّيًاsending blessings; the act of Salawat upon the Prophet ﷺ
  • وَآلِهِand his family
  • وَمَنْ تَلَاand whoever follows (his path); extended by some scholars to include the Companions and all believers
Translation
All praise is due to Allah, and I send blessings upon Muhammad, his family, and whoever follows.
Commentary

This line follows the traditional three-part opening expected of classical Islamic works: the Basmala (understood as preceding the poem, though not always written into the verse itself), the Hamdalah, and the Salawat. Al-Jamzuri compresses the Hamdalah and Salawat into a single line, a common technique in didactic poetry where meter forces economy of words.

What "Salat" (Blessing) Means, by Who Performs It
  • From Allah: mercy (rahmah) bestowed upon the one blessed.
  • From the Angels: seeking forgiveness (istighfar) on that person's behalf.
  • From a Human Being: supplication (du'a), as when one says "Allahumma salli 'ala Muhammad," asking Allah to bless the Prophet.

ReflectionAl-Jamzuri could have opened the poem with his own name and moved straight to the subject matter. Why do you think classical authors insist on this three-part opening (Basmala, Hamdalah, Salawat) even in a short technical poem about pronunciation rules?

The convention reflects a view that knowledge is not a neutral technical transaction but an act that should be entered into with the right disposition. A narration states that any important matter not begun with the Basmala is cut off from blessing. Beginning even a short poem on pronunciation rules this way reflects the belief that correct recitation of the Qur'an is itself an act of worship, not merely a technical skill, so the manner of entering into its study matters.

The Book's Title and Purpose تسمية الكتاب وغرضه

Text
وَبَعْدُ هَذَا النَّظْمُ لِلْمُرِيدِ *** فِي النُّوْنِ وَالتَّنْوِيْنِ وَالْمُدُوْدِ
سَمَّيْتُهُ بِتُحْفَةِ الْأَطْفَالِ *** عَنْ شَيْخِنَا الْمِيْهِيِّ ذِي الْكَمَالِ
Vocabulary
  • وَبَعْدُas for what follows; the formal transition marking the end of the introduction, common in khutbahs and classical books
  • النَّظْمthe versified composition; the poem itself
  • لِلْمُرِيدِfor the one who seeks (this knowledge); the intended student
  • الْمُدُوْدthe elongations; plural of madd, the rules of vocal prolongation covered later in the poem
  • سَمَّيْتُهُI named it
  • تُحْفَةِ الْأَطْفَالِThe Gift for Children, the poem's title
  • شَيْخِنَا الْمِيْهِيِّour teacher al-Mihi; Shaykh Nooruddin Ali al-Mihi, al-Jamzuri's own Tajwid teacher
Translation
As for what follows: this composition is for the seeker, on the noon, the tanween, and the elongations. I have named it "The Gift for Children," after our teacher al-Mihi, possessor of perfection.
Commentary

"Wa ba'du" is a fixed formula in classical Arabic prose and poetry, marking the shift from the introductory praise into the substance of the work. It functions the same way "amma ba'du" (as for what follows) does at the start of a khutbah.

The Title's Real Meaning: "Tuhfat" means gift, and "al-atfal" means children, so the title reads literally as "The Gift for Children." This describes the book's accessibility, not an exclusive audience. It is the standard starting point for beginners of every age, and its usefulness extends to intermediate and advanced students who return to it as a review.

(2)

Al-Jamzuri also honors his own teacher, Shaykh Nooruddin Ali al-Mihi, in this line, describing him as "possessor of perfection" (dhul-kamali), though some manuscript variants give "possessor of beauty" (dhil-jamali). Honoring one's teacher in the opening lines of a text is itself a classical convention, reflecting the isnad-based culture of Islamic scholarship: the text does not stand alone, but is presented as received knowledge, traceable back through a named chain of teachers.

The Author's Hope for the Book دعاء الناظم

Text
أَرْجُو بِهِ أَنْ يَنْفَعَ الطُّلَّابَا *** وَالْأَجْرَ وَالْقَبُولَ وَالثَّوَابَا
Vocabulary
  • أَرْجُوI hope
  • بِهِwith it; referring to the book
  • يَنْفَعَit will benefit
  • الطُّلَّابَاthe students; the same group referred to as "al-murid" in the previous line
  • الْأَجْرَrecompense
  • الْقَبُولَacceptance
  • الثَّوَابَاreward
Translation
I hope that it will benefit the students, and (I hope for) recompense, acceptance, and reward.
Commentary

Having named the book and its purpose, al-Jamzuri closes his introduction with a personal du'a rather than moving straight into the rulings. He hopes for two distinct things at once: that the book itself succeeds in benefiting students, and that he personally is rewarded by Allah for having written it.

Al-Talab (the Students): The word used here for "students" covers three levels at once: the beginner (al-mubtadi'), who cannot yet study a text independently; the intermediate (al-mutawassit), who has learned enough to guide his own further study; and the expert (al-muntahi), who is capable of understanding the text and its deeper meanings on his own. Al-Jamzuri's hope for benefit is addressed to all three levels, not just beginners, which matches how the book is actually used today.

(3)

Some commentators distinguish ajr (recompense) from thawab (reward): ajr is the compensation earned for a specific deed, while thawab is a reward Allah grants purely out of His mercy and grace, independent of any single action. Others treat the two words as synonyms used together here simply for the sake of the poem's rhyme. Note also the extra alif at the end of الطُّلَّابَا and الثَّوَابَا: this is alif al-itlaq, a "free" alif added purely to preserve the poem's rhyme scheme and meter, with no effect on the word's grammar or meaning.
