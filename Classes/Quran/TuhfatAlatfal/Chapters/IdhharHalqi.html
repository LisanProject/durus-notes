Idhhar Halqi الإظهار الحلقي

Text
لِلنُّوْنِ إِنْ تَسْكُنْ وَلِلتَّنْوِيْنِ *** أَرْبَعُ أَحْكَامٍ فَخُذْ تَبْيِيْنِي
فَالْأَوَّلُ الْإِظْهَارُ قَبْلَ أَحْرُفِ *** لِلْحَلْقِ سِتٍّ رُتِّبَتْ فَلْتُعْرَفِ
هَمْزٌ فَهَاءٌ ثُمَّ عَيْنٌ حَاءُ *** مُهْمَلَتَانِ ثُمَّ غَيْنٌ خَاءُ
Vocabulary
  • النُّوْنُ إِنْ تَسْكُنْthe noon, if it becomes still (sakinah); a noon with no vowel
  • التَّنْوِيْنtanween; the double vowel mark at the end of an indefinite noun, pronounced as an added n sound
  • أَحْكَامrulings; the four possible outcomes covered in this chapter
  • الْإِظْهَارclarification; pronouncing a letter plainly, without merging or nasalizing it into what follows
  • الْحَلْقthe throat; the point of articulation shared by the six idhhar letters
  • مُهْمَلَتَانِthe two undotted (letters); referring to ح and ع, which lack the diacritical dot that distinguishes خ and غ
Translation
For the noon, if it is still, and for tanween, there are four rulings, so take this explanation from me. The first is idhhar, before letters of the throat, six in number, arranged so you may know them: hamza, then ha, then 'ayn, ha, the two undotted letters, then ghayn, kha.
Commentary

A noon sakinah is a noon that appears in the actual spelling of a word and carries no vowel of its own, for example the noon in مِنْ. Tanween is different: it is not a written letter at all, but a doubled vowel diacritic at the end of an indefinite noun that is pronounced as an added n sound, for example the final sound in كِتَابٌ. Despite this difference, both are governed by the exact same four rules whenever they are followed by another letter, because in both cases the reciter is producing the same n sound and needs to know how to handle it.

Idhhar Halqi means pronouncing the noon sakinah or tanween clearly and separately, with no nasalization beyond its ordinary sound and no blending into the following letter. It applies specifically when one of six "throat letters" follows.

The Six Throat Letters
LetterName
ءHamzah
هHa'
ع'Ayn
حHa
غGhayn
خKha

These six letters are called "halqi" (of the throat) because all six are articulated from the throat itself, at three different depths: hamzah and ha' from the deepest part, 'ayn and ha from the middle, and ghayn and kha from the point closest to the mouth. The rule is considered the default outcome for a simple reason: the articulation point of noon (the tip of the tongue) sits about as far as possible from the articulation point of any throat letter, so blending the two sounds together would be physically awkward. Where two letters are already far apart, clear separation is the natural result, not merging.

Here are worked examples covering all six throat letters. Try to identify the noon sakinah or tanween and the throat letter triggering idhhar before revealing each solution.

Example 1 (Hamzah)Identify the noon sakinah or tanween and the idhhar letter that follows it.مَنْ آمَنَ

"whoever believes"

The noon sakinah in مَنْ is followed by hamzah at the start of آمَنَ. Since hamzah is a throat letter, the noon is pronounced clearly and fully, with no nasal blending into the hamzah.

Example 2 (Ha')Identify the noon sakinah or tanween and the idhhar letter that follows it.مِنْ هَادٍ

"any guide"

The noon sakinah in مِنْ meets ha' at the start of هَادٍ. Ha' is the second throat letter, so the same clear, unmerged pronunciation applies.

Example 3 ('Ayn, within a word)Identify the noon sakinah or tanween and the idhhar letter that follows it.أَنْعَمْتَ

"You have favored"

Here the noon sakinah and the throat letter occur inside a single word rather than across two words: the noon in أَنْ is immediately followed by 'ayn in عَمْتَ. Idhhar applies exactly the same way whether the noon sakinah and the triggering letter are in the same word or in two separate words.

Example 4 (Ha)Identify the noon sakinah or tanween and the idhhar letter that follows it.مِنْ حَكِيمٍ

"from a Wise (one)"

The noon sakinah in مِنْ is followed by ha in حَكِيمٍ. Note that this word also ends in tanween, which is simply left as it is at the end of the phrase and does not itself trigger a new ruling unless another word follows.

Example 5 (Ghayn)Identify the noon sakinah or tanween and the idhhar letter that follows it.مِنْ غِلٍّ

"of rancor"

The noon sakinah in مِنْ is followed by ghayn in غِلٍّ. Ghayn and kha are the two dotted throat letters, distinguished from ha and 'ayn, which are undotted (muhmalatan), the term used explicitly in the poem's third line above.

Example 6 (Kha)Identify the noon sakinah or tanween and the idhhar letter that follows it.مَنْ خَشِيَ

"whoever feared"

The noon sakinah in مَنْ is followed by kha in خَشِيَ, completing all six throat letters across these examples.

Example phrases above are illustrative of the six throat letters and their pronunciation rather than citations of specific ayat; always verify wording and application against the mushaf and a qualified reciter.
