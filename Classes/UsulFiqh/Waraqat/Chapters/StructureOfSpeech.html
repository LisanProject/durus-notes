The Structure of Speech

Text
فَأَقَلُّ مَا يَتَرَكَّبُ مِنْهُ الْكَلَامُ اسْمَانِ، أَوِ اسْمٌ وَفِعْلٌ، أَوْ فِعْلٌ وَحَرْفٌ، أَوِ اسْمٌ وَحَرْفٌ.
Translation
The least that speech can be composed of is two nouns, or a noun and a verb, or a verb and a particle, or a noun and a particle.
Commentary

This is the same ground the Ajrumiyyah covers when it defines kalam as a composed, meaningful utterance, applied here specifically to the minimum combinations that can form a complete unit of speech. Two nouns can form a sentence on their own, زَيْدٌ قَائِمٌ, Zayd is standing. A noun and a verb likewise form a complete sentence, قَامَ زَيْدٌ. The remaining combinations, verb with particle or noun with particle, work the same way once you allow for an implied subject or predicate.

Text
وَالْكَلَامُ يَنْقَسِمُ إِلَى: أَمْرٍ، وَنَهْيٍ، وَخَبَرٍ، وَاسْتِخْبَارٍ، وَيَنْقَسِمُ أَيْضاً إِلَى تَمَنٍّ، وَعَرْضٍ، وَقَسَمٍ.
Vocabulary
  • أَمْرcommand
  • نَهْيprohibition
  • خَبَرa statement of fact
  • اسْتِخْبَارa question
  • تَمَنٍّa wish
  • عَرْضan offer or gentle request
  • قَسَمan oath
Translation
Speech divides into: command, prohibition, a statement of fact, and a question. It also divides into a wish, an offer, and an oath.
Commentary

Two chapters of usul al-fiqh, command and prohibition, get built directly on top of the first two categories here. The remaining categories, statement, question, wish, offer, and oath, matter mainly because legal texts are not only commands and prohibitions. A hadith phrased as a simple statement of fact can still carry legal weight, and distinguishing a genuine command from what is only grammatically shaped like one, an offer or a wish, is part of what usul al-fiqh trains you to notice.
