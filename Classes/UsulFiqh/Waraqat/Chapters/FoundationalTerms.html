Foundational Terms

Text
أَمَا بَعْدُ فَهَذِهِ وَرَقَاتٌ تَشْتَمِلُ عَلَى مَعْرِفَةِ فُصُولٍ مِنْ أُصُولِ الْفِقْهِ، وَذَلِكَ مُؤَلَّفٌ مِنْ جُزْءَيْنِ مُفْرَدَيْنِ، فَالْأَصْلُ: مَا يُبْنَى عَلَيْهِ غَيْرُهُ، وَالْفَرْعُ: مَا يُبْنَى عَلَى غَيْرِهِ، وَالْفِقْهُ: مَعْرِفَةُ الْأَحْكَامِ الشَّرْعِيَّةِ الَّتِي طَرِيقُهَا الِاجْتِهَادُ.
Vocabulary
  • وَرَقَاتpapers, leaves; the title of the text, meaning a short treatise
  • الْأَصْلthe foundation; that upon which something else is built
  • الْفَرْعthe branch; that which is built upon something else
  • الْفِقْهjurisprudence; knowledge of the shar'i rulings arrived at through ijtihad
  • الِاجْتِهَادindependent legal reasoning; the disciplined effort to derive a ruling from the sources
Translation
As for what follows: these are papers covering knowledge of chapters from the foundations of fiqh, composed of two separate parts. Al-asl is that upon which something else is built, and al-far' is that which is built upon something else. Al-fiqh is knowledge of the shar'i rulings whose path is ijtihad.
Commentary

Al-Juwayni opens by defining the two words in his own title before anything else.

1. Al-Asl (The Foundation) is a foundation, in the literal sense a wall's footing or a tree's root, and by extension anything that other things are built on top of.

(1)

2. Al-Far' (The Branch) is whatever gets built on top of something else, a branch on a tree, or a specific derived ruling built on top of a foundational principle.

(2)

Fiqh itself gets a precise definition here, not just "Islamic law" in a loose sense.

3. Al-Fiqh (Jurisprudence) is knowledge of the shar'i rulings specifically arrived at through ijtihad. That last qualifier matters. A ruling everyone already agrees on, such as the obligation of the five daily prayers, is not "fiqh" in this precise technical sense, since there was never any disagreement to reason through. Fiqh in the sense usul al-fiqh cares about only shows up where scholars actually have to reason from evidence to a conclusion, which is exactly why disagreement (ikhtilaf) is the natural home of fiqh, not a flaw in it.

(3)

Usul al-fiqh, as a discipline, studies the asl side of things, the methodology and general principles, not the far', the specific case-by-case rulings that make up fiqh proper. A student who only memorizes furu' rulings without understanding the usul behind them can recite conclusions but cannot explain why two schools of law reach different answers from the same text, or work out a new case that no one has directly addressed before.
