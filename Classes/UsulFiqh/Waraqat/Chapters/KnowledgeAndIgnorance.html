Knowledge and Ignorance

Text
وَالْفِقْهُ: أَخَصُّ مِنَ الْعِلْمِ، وَالْعِلْمُ: مَعْرِفَةُ الْمَعْلُومِ عَلَى مَا هُوَ بِهِ فِي الْوَاقِعِ. وَالْجَهْلُ: تَصَوُّرُ الشَّيْءِ عَلَى خِلَافِ مَا هُوَ فِي الْوَاقِعِ.
Vocabulary
  • الْعِلْمknowledge; perceiving a thing as it actually is
  • الْجَهْلignorance; conceiving a thing contrary to how it actually is
  • أَخَصّmore specific; fiqh is a narrower category within the broader category of knowledge
Translation
Fiqh is more specific than knowledge. Knowledge is perceiving the known thing as it actually is. Ignorance is conceiving a thing contrary to how it actually is in reality.
Commentary

1. Al-'Ilm (Knowledge) is perceiving the known thing as it actually is.

(1)

2. Al-Jahl (Ignorance) is conceiving a thing contrary to how it actually is in reality.

(2)

Fiqh is a subset of knowledge specifically, more narrow and more tied to action than knowledge in general. But the definition of knowledge itself given here is worth sitting with. An older, more common definition, "knowledge is recognition of the known," was pointed out in the lesson as circular: it just defines the word using a close synonym of itself, the same problem you would run into trying to define "ma'rifa" in terms of "'ilm" and back again. "Perceiving a thing as it actually is" avoids that trap, since it grounds the definition in reality rather than in another undefined term.

A useful example of real knowledge: knowing that a human being is a rational animal. A useful example of the opposite: some philosophers' claim that the world is eternal, which is treated as jahl, since observable change in the world is itself evidence of its being temporal, contradicting the claim.

Beyond knowledge and ignorance, the lesson lays out a fuller scale: full knowledge (precise alignment with reality), probable assumption (zann), doubt (shakk, where two possibilities are equally weighted), delusion (wahm, a strong but baseless belief), and ignorance (jahl, either simply not knowing, or actively misjudging). Simple ignorance is admitting you do not know something. Compound ignorance is worse: confidently believing something false, without realizing your belief is wrong.

Text
وَالْعِلْمُ الضَّرُورِيُّ: مَا لَا يَقَعُ عَنْ نَظَرٍ وَاسْتِدْلَالٍ. وَأَمَّا الْعِلْمُ الْمُكْتَسَبُ: فَهُوَ الْمَوْقُوفُ عَلَى النَّظَرِ وَالِاسْتِدْلَالِ.
Vocabulary
  • الْعِلْمُ الضَّرُورِيّnecessary knowledge; knowledge that does not depend on reflection or inference
  • الْعِلْمُ الْمُكْتَسَبacquired knowledge; knowledge that depends on reflection and inference
Translation
Necessary knowledge is that which does not come about through reflection or inference. Acquired knowledge is that which depends on reflection and inference.
Commentary

3. Al-'Ilm al-Daruri (Necessary Knowledge) is the kind you get immediately, through the senses, without having to think your way to it, that fire burns, or that the sky is above you.

(3)

4. Al-'Ilm al-Muktasab (Acquired Knowledge) requires actual mental work to reach, which is exactly the category fiqh belongs to, since legal rulings are derived, not immediately obvious.

(4)
Text
وَالنَّظَرُ هُوَ الْفِكْرُ فِي الْمَنْظُورِ فِيهِ. وَالِاسْتِدْلَالُ: طَلَبُ الدَّلِيلِ، وَالدَّلِيلُ هُوَ: الْمُرْشِدُ إِلَى الْمَطْلُوبِ.
Vocabulary
  • النَّظَرreflection; deliberate mental consideration of what is being examined
  • الْفِكْرthought; the movement of the mind in the process of reasoning
  • الِاسْتِدْلَالinference; the act of seeking out evidence
  • الدَّلِيلevidence; whatever guides you toward what you are seeking
Translation
Reflection is thought applied to the thing being examined. Inference is the seeking of evidence, and evidence is that which guides toward what is sought.
Commentary

5. Al-Nazar (Reflection) is the deliberate act of turning your attention to something, distinct from al-Fikr (Thought), the actual movement of the mind as it reasons through it.

(5)

6. Al-Istidlal (Inference) is what you are doing when you go looking for a dalil (evidence), and dalil itself is a broad enough term to mean either a source text directly, the Quran or a hadith, or the method by which a ruling gets derived from that text.

(6)
Text
وَالظَّنُّ: تَجْوِيزُ أَمْرَيْنِ أَحَدُهُمَا أَظْهَرُ مِنَ الْآخَرِ. وَالشَّكُّ: تَجْوِيزُ أَمْرَيْنِ لَا مَزِيَّةَ لِأَحَدِهِمَا عَلَى الْآخَرِ.
Vocabulary
  • الظَّنّprobable conjecture; leaning toward one of two possibilities
  • الشَّكّdoubt; regarding two possibilities as equally likely
Translation
Zann is allowing for two possibilities where one is more apparent than the other. Shakk is allowing for two possibilities where neither has any advantage over the other.
Commentary

7. Al-Zann (Probable Conjecture) is allowing for two possibilities where one is more apparent than the other.

(7)

8. Al-Shakk (Doubt) is allowing for two possibilities where neither has any advantage over the other.

(8)

This distinction is not just abstract logic, it is the actual epistemic condition most of fiqh operates in. Legal judgment, ijtihad, works in the space of zann, not certainty. This is exactly the point behind the well known hadith: if a judge exercises ijtihad and is correct, he receives two rewards; if he errs, he still receives one. That hadith only makes sense once you accept that ijtihad is reasoning under zann, not a guarantee of arriving at the single correct answer every time.

This also shapes how courts actually function. A judge rules based on external evidence and process, not on private personal certainty, which is the basis for the principle "al-bayyina 'ala al-mudda'i, wa-l-yamin 'ala man ankara," the burden of proof is on the one making the claim, and an oath is imposed on the one who denies it.
