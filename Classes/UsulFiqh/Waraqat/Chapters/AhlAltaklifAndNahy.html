Who Is Addressed by Command, and Al-Nahy

Text
اَلَّذِي يَدْخُلُ فِي الْأَمْرِ وَالنَّهْيِ وَمَا لَا يَدْخُلُ. يَدْخُلُ فِي خِطَابِ اللَّهِ تَعَالَى: ﴿الْمُؤْمِنُونَ﴾، وَالسَّاهِي وَالصَّبِيُّ وَالْمَجْنُونُ غَيْرُ دَاخِلِينَ.
Vocabulary
  • خِطَاب اللَّهkhitab Allah, the address of Allah, meaning the scope of who is actually being spoken to and held accountable by revealed command and prohibition
  • السَّاهِيal-sahi, one who is forgetful or unaware at the moment in question
  • الصَّبِيّal-sabi, a child who has not yet reached puberty
  • الْمَجْنُونal-majnun, a person who is insane
Translation
What falls under command and prohibition, and what does not. The believers fall under Allah's address. The forgetful, the child, and the insane do not fall under it.
Commentary

This section defines ahl al-taklif, who is actually addressed by a legal command or prohibition and therefore accountable to it, before the discussion of amr can move any further.

Who Is Addressed. Believers fall squarely under Allah's address, meaning they are the ones a command or prohibition is actually directed at and accountable to.

(1)

Who Is Excluded. Three categories are not addressed, and so are not held accountable in the moment: al-sahi (someone forgetful or unaware at that moment), al-sabi (a child before puberty), and al-majnun (someone insane). Exclusion here is not permanent for all three in the same way. Al-sahi is excluded only for the duration of the forgetfulness itself, and is required to make up an obligation, such as a missed prayer, once that awareness returns. This is a temporary lapse in address, not a standing exemption the way sabi and majnun are while the underlying condition lasts.

(2)

This distinction also shapes how a real fiqh dispute plays out. In the Shafi'i school, zakah is still owed on the wealth of a child or an insane person once it reaches nisab and a full year has passed, since the obligation is treated as attached to the wealth itself rather than to the taklif of its owner. The Hanafi school ties the obligation to bulugh (puberty) and 'aql (sanity) directly, and does not require zakah in that case. Both positions agree on who is and is not addressed by taklif in general; they differ on whether zakah specifically tracks the wealth or the person.

Text
وَالْكُفَّارُ مُخَاطَبُونَ بِفُرُوعِ الشَّرِيعَةِ، وَبِمَا لَا تَصِحُّ إِلَّا بِهِ، وَهُوَ الْإِسْلَامُ، لِقَوْلِهِ تَعَالَى: ﴿قَالُوا لَمْ نَكُ مِنَ الْمُصَلِّينَ﴾. وَالْأَمْرُ بِالشَّيْءِ نَهْيٌ عَنْ ضِدِّهِ، وَالنَّهْيُ عَنِ الشَّيْءِ أَمْرٌ بِضِدِّهِ.
Vocabulary
  • فُرُوع الشَّرِيعَةfuru' al-shari'ah, the branches of the law, meaning its specific practical rulings rather than its theological foundations
  • ضِدّdidd, the opposite of a given thing
Translation
Disbelievers are addressed by the branches of the law, and by that without which those branches cannot be valid, which is Islam, on account of Allah's statement: "They said, we were not among those who prayed." A command to a thing is a prohibition of its opposite, and a prohibition of a thing is a command to its opposite.
Commentary

Two more pieces close out this section: a clarification of how disbelievers relate to legal address despite falling outside the community of belief, and the reciprocal relationship between amr and nahy themselves.

Disbelievers and Furu' al-Shari'ah. Disbelievers are still addressed by the practical branches of the law, meaning they are genuinely blameworthy for not praying, not fasting, and so on, and not merely for disbelief in isolation. The verse cited has the people of the Fire naming their failure to pray specifically as one of the reasons for their punishment, alongside their disbelief, which only makes sense if they were genuinely addressed by that obligation in the first place. At the same time, none of those branches can actually be validly performed without the one precondition that makes any of them valid at all: Islam itself. A disbeliever is blameworthy for not praying, but cannot discharge that obligation by praying while still outside Islam.

(1)

Amr and Nahy Are Reciprocal. A command to do something functions, at the same time, as a prohibition of its opposite, and a prohibition of something functions as a command to its opposite. A command to speak the truth is, by the same act, a prohibition of lying; a prohibition of lying is, by the same act, a command to speak the truth. The two are not separate rulings that happen to align; they are two descriptions of a single ruling.

(2)
Text
وَالنَّهْيُ: اسْتِدْعَاءُ التَّرْكِ بِالْقَوْلِ مِمَّنْ هُوَ دُونَهُ عَلَى سَبِيلِ الْوُجُوبِ، وَيَدُلُّ عَلَى فَسَادِ الْمَنْهِيِّ عَنْهُ.
Vocabulary
  • النَّهْيal-nahy, prohibition, the mirror image of al-amr
  • التَّرْكal-tark, the leaving off or non-performance of an act
  • فَسَادfasad, corruption or invalidity, here of the act being prohibited
Translation
Nahy is the seeking of abstention, by speech, from someone of lesser standing than the speaker, on the basis of obligation, and it indicates the corruption of the thing prohibited.
Commentary

Nahy mirrors the definition of amr given earlier almost word for word, with tark (leaving off an act) substituted for fi'l (performing an act). This parallel structure is deliberate: amr and nahy are being defined as structurally symmetrical categories, which is also why the "reciprocal" rule above is able to move so cleanly between them.

Nahy Indicates Corruption of the Act. Beyond simply obligating abstention, a prohibition also signals that the prohibited act itself is fasid, defective or invalid, when performed. This is why an act done in violation of a clear prohibition is not merely sinful but typically treated as legally void or unsound in its own right, rather than sinful yet still valid.
