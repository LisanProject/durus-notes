Usul al-Fiqh and Its Chapters

Text
وَأُصُولُ الْفِقْهِ: طُرُقُهُ عَلَى سَبِيلِ الْإِجْمَالِ، وَكَيْفِيَّةُ الِاسْتِدْلَالِ بِهَا.
Vocabulary
  • طُرُقpaths, methods; plural of طَرِيق
  • الْإِجْمَالsummary form; in general terms, as opposed to detailed case-by-case treatment
Translation
Usul al-fiqh is its methods in summary form, and how inference is drawn using them.
Commentary

Usul al-Fiqh is its methods in summary form, and how inference is drawn using them.

(1)

This is worth restating in plainer terms: usul al-fiqh is the summarized study of the ways jurists derive legal rulings from the Islamic texts. It focuses on methodology and general rules, not on the specific case-by-case conclusions, furu', that make up fiqh proper. The discipline is concerned with how a ruling gets constructed out of Quran, Sunnah, ijma', and qiyas, and with the machinery around that process: legal language, how conflicting evidence gets resolved, and so on.

A example worth sitting with: "aqimu al-salah," establish the prayer. Usul asks a question fiqh alone does not: does a command like this always mean strict obligation, or can the same grammatical form indicate something weaker, like encouragement, depending on context? Another example: the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, praying inside the Ka'bah. Is that action evidence for a ruling at all, and if so, does it carry the weight of an obligation, a recommendation, or mere permissibility? These are exactly the kinds of meta-questions usul al -fiqh exists to answer, questions about how evidence works, not what the evidence says in any one case.

Text
وَأَبْوَابُ أُصُولِ الْفِقْهِ: أَقْسَامُ الْكَلَامِ، وَالْأَمْرُ وَالنَّهْيُ، وَالْعَامُّ وَالْخَاصُّ، وَالْمُجْمَلُ وَالْمُبَيَّنُ، وَالنَّصُّ وَالظَّاهِرُ، وَالْأَفْعَالُ، وَالنَّاسِخُ وَالْمَنْسُوخُ، وَالْإِجْمَاعُ، وَالْأَخْبَارُ، وَالْقِيَاسُ، وَالْحَظْرُ وَالْإِبَاحَةُ، وَتَرْتِيبُ الْأَدِلَّةِ، وَصِفَةُ الْمُفْتِي وَالْمُسْتَفْتِي، وَأَحْكَامُ الْمُجْتَهِدِينَ.
Translation
The chapters of usul al-fiqh are: the divisions of speech; command and prohibition; the general and the specific; the ambiguous and the clarified; the explicit text and the apparent meaning; actions; the abrogating and the abrogated; consensus; reports; analogy; prohibition and permissibility; the ordering of evidences; the character of the mufti and the one seeking a verdict; and the rulings governing those who exercise independent legal reasoning.
Commentary

This list is effectively the table of contents for the rest of the book, and for the rest of this course. Two things are worth noting about the sources it eventually gets to. The Quran and the Sunnah are accepted by every school without dispute. Ijma' and qiyas are accepted by the mainline schools, Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali, though even among them there is real debate about exactly how far each one extends and how it is defined. The Zahiris and some within the Salafi tradition dispute the authority or scope of qiyas more directly. Understanding which sources a given school accepts, and why, is close to indispensable for understanding why that school reaches the rulings it does.

Before going further, it is worth being honest about what this subject assumes you already have some grounding in: aqida and kalam, nahw and sarf, balagha, mantiq, and enough hadith science, such as al-Bayquniyyah, to evaluate the reliability of a report. Usul al-fiqh does not develop these from scratch. A student who tries to study it without at least a working foundation in Arabic grammar and morphology in particular will keep hitting a wall, since so much of the discipline is about how Arabic legal language actually functions.
