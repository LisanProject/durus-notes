The Seven Ahkam

Text
وَالْأَحْكَامُ سَبْعَةٌ: الْوَاجِبُ، وَالْمَنْدُوبُ، وَالْمُبَاحُ، وَالْمَحْظُورُ، وَالْمَكْرُوهُ، وَالصَّحِيحُ، وَالْفَاسِدُ.
Translation
The rulings are seven: the obligatory, the recommended, the permissible, the prohibited, the disliked, the valid, and the invalid.
Commentary

Before defining each one, it is worth being precise about what a hukm shar'i actually is: it is Allah's address concerning the actions of the morally responsible person (mukallaf), nothing more and nothing less. This is what separates fiqh from aqida. Aqida concerns belief, not action, so creedal points are deliberately excluded from this framework even though the two disciplines are obviously connected.

It also matters that wajib, mandub, and the rest describe a trait of the ACT, not the ruling itself. The actual verdict or command from the divine address is called ijab. Keeping this distinction in mind, here is what each of the seven means, with the reward or punishment attached to doing or leaving it.

1. Al-Wajib (The Obligatory) is rewarded for doing it, punished for leaving it. The five daily prayers are the standard example.

(1)

2. Al-Mandub (The Recommended) is rewarded for doing it, but not punished for leaving it.

(2)

3. Al-Mubah (The Permissible) is neither rewarded nor punished, whether you do it or leave it.

(3)

4. Al-Mahzur (The Prohibited / Haram) is rewarded for leaving it, punished for doing it. The reward for leaving it only applies when the avoidance is intentional obedience, imtithalan, not just happening to never have done it.

(4)

5. Al-Makruh (The Disliked) is rewarded for abstaining, with intention, but not punished for doing it. Makruh sits opposite mandub the same way haram sits opposite wajib.

(5)

6. Al-Sahih (The Valid) is valid: it is counted toward fulfilling the obligation and it actually takes legal effect. Two technical terms come up here, nufudh, meaning the ruling actually executes (mainly used for transactions), and i'tidad, meaning it counts as having discharged your duty (mainly used for acts of worship). A prayer performed with all its actual conditions met, wudu, facing the qiblah, and so on, is sahih. In a sale contract, sahih means ownership actually transfers.

(6)

7. Al-Fasid (The Invalid / Batil) is invalid: it has no legal effect and does not discharge the obligation. Praying without wudu, or a sale contract missing a required condition, are both fasid for this reason.

(7)

It is worth flagging one theological point that comes up alongside this discussion: it is never correct to say Allah "must" do something. Where a hadith uses language that sounds like an obligation on Allah, such as "the right of Allah over His servants," that language is understood in light of the principle that Allah is not questioned about what He does. The apparent obligation language describes what Allah has promised out of His own grace, not a binding requirement upon Him in the way ahkam bind the mukallaf.
