Haqiqa and Majaz

Text
وَمِنْ وَجْهٍ آخَرَ يَنْقَسِمُ إِلَى حَقِيقَةٍ وَمَجَازٍ. فَالْحَقِيقَةُ: مَا بَقِيَ فِي الِاسْتِعْمَالِ عَلَى مَوْضُوعِهِ. وَقِيلَ: مَا اسْتُعْمِلَ فِيمَا اصْطُلِحَ عَلَيْهِ مِنَ الْمُخَاطَبَةِ. وَالْمَجَازُ: مَا تُجُوِّزَ بِهِ عَنْ مَوْضُوعِهِ.
Vocabulary
  • حَقِيقَةliteral usage; a word kept to its original established meaning
  • مَجَازfigurative usage; a word carried beyond its original established meaning
  • مَوْضُوعthe thing a word was originally coined for
Translation
From another angle, speech divides into the literal and the figurative. The literal is what remains, in usage, on its original meaning, and it has also been said: what is used according to what has been conventionally agreed upon in communication. The figurative is what has been carried beyond its original meaning.
Commentary

This distinction matters constantly in legal reasoning, since a ruling can shift entirely depending on whether a word in a text is read literally or figuratively. Al-Juwayni further divides haqiqa into three kinds: lughawiyya, the plain linguistic meaning; shar'iyya, a meaning the Shari'ah has assigned to a word beyond its ordinary linguistic sense, such as "salah" meaning the ritual prayer rather than just linguistic "supplication"; and 'urfiyya, a meaning fixed by common custom.

Text
وَالْمَجَازُ: إِمَّا أَنْ يَكُونَ بِزِيَادَةٍ، أَوْ نُقْصَانٍ، أَوْ نَقْلٍ، أَوِ اسْتِعَارَةٍ.
Translation
Figurative usage is either by addition, omission, transference, or metaphor.
Commentary

Each of the four types of majaz gets a real Quranic example in the text, which is worth working through individually rather than just memorizing the category names.

Majaz by Addition لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِ شَيْءٌ

"There is nothing like unto Him" (al-Shura 42:11). Taken with full literalness, "like unto Him" would imply Allah has a likeness, which is precisely what the verse is negating. The word كَ (like) is treated as an added element within the negation, since the verse means "there is nothing like Him," full stop, not "there is nothing like His likeness."

Majaz by Omission وَاسْأَلِ الْقَرْيَةَ

"And ask the town" (Yusuf 12:82). A town cannot literally be asked anything. The word "people of" (أَهْلَ) has been omitted: the actual meaning is "ask the people of the town," with the omission being covered by majaz rather than requiring the sentence to be false or meaningless.

Majaz by Transference الْغَائِط

Al-gha'it literally means low-lying ground. Usage transferred the word to mean human waste, since people would go to low-lying ground for privacy. The word kept its new meaning even once separated from the original literal referent.

Majaz by Metaphor جِدَارًا يُرِيدُ أَنْ يَنْقَضَّ

"A wall that wanted to collapse" (al-Kahf 18:77). A wall cannot literally want anything. The verse borrows the language of intention, normally reserved for a being with a will, and applies it metaphorically to the wall's leaning, collapsing state.
