Clarifying Positions on Ash'ariyyah

A running theme across the Aqidah notes is addressing common misunderstandings about the Ash'ari school directly, rather than leaving them unaddressed. This page collects those points in one place. Most trace back to unfamiliarity with the primary texts of the school, or to reading it through the lens of secondary sources rather than the actual works of figures like Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari and his students. Each entry links back to the chapter it is drawn from for the fuller context.

Position to Clarify: That Ash'aris deny the divine attributes.

(1)

This mistakes what the school actually teaches. The school does not deny the attributes; it affirms them in general as certainly as any other Sunni school, and affirms specific ones in detail wherever revelation or sound reasoning establishes them, while declining to specify an exact modality (kayfiyyah) it has no independent access to. Denying real meaning altogether (ta'til) is exactly what the tradition rejects, not what it teaches; affirming without specifying "how" (ithbat bila kayf) is a very different position from denial. Where a term like "hand" appears, the three classical approaches within the school, ta'wil, ithbat, and tafwid, all reject ta'til together; they differ only in how far each is willing to go in specifying meaning, not in whether the attribute is affirmed. (See: Certainty, the Ahad Hadith, and Common Misconceptions, Mukhalafah lil Hawadith and Qiyam bi Nafsih)

Position to Clarify: That Ash'aris rank the Qur'an below the Prophet ﷺ in status.

(2)

Precision in terminology resolves the apparent tension. If "the Qur'an" refers to the eternal attribute of Kalam Allah, nothing in creation is comparable to it, full stop, and no comparison with the Prophet ﷺ is even coherent to make. If instead "the Qur'an" refers to the physical mushaf, or to the act of reciting it, then yes, the Prophet ﷺ, as a created being of the highest possible rank, is considered superior to a created copy or a created act of recitation. Scholars have discussed the same question with respect to the Prophet's own body relative to other honored created things, such as the Ka'bah or the Divine Throne, with the consistent answer that his sanctified person outranks them; the point in every case is distinguishing what is eternal and incomparable from what is created and can meaningfully be ranked. (See: Certainty, the Ahad Hadith, and Common Misconceptions, As-Sam', Al-Basar, and Al-Kalam)

Position to Clarify: That Ash'aris teach Allah has "only twenty attributes," as though the tradition were artificially capping the divine.

(3)

This is a misreading. Every perfection is affirmed in general; the twenty are simply the ones proven in enough detail, by revelation and reason together, to be required knowledge. Nothing in the framework denies or limits what Allah is in Himself; it describes what a finite human mind is capable of establishing in detail, not a ceiling on Allah's actual perfection. The twenty are a pedagogical minimum for the student, not a theological maximum for Allah. (See: The Twenty Attributes)

Position to Clarify: That Ash'aris believe the Qur'an is created.

(4)

This collapses a distinction the school actually insists on. Ash'aris hold that Allah's Speech (kalam) has two sides. Kalam nafsi, the essential Speech, is Allah's eternal, uncreated attribute itself, not composed of letters, sounds, or sequence, and always true of Him. Kalam lafzi, the verbal speech, is the created expression of that attribute, the specific letters and sounds recited, and the ink and paper of a physical mushaf. The recitation and the physical copy are created and point to the eternal attribute without being identical to it, so calling the recitation or the mushaf created casts no doubt on the attribute's own eternity. Musa, peace be upon him, illustrates the distinction concretely. He "heard the ancient Speech," meaning Allah unveiled His eternal Speech to him directly, not through the ordinary human mechanism of letters and audible sound the way a reciter today produces sound waves that travel through air. Qur'an 42:51 supports this reading, stating that it is not possible for any human being that Allah should speak to him except by revelation, from behind a veil, or by sending a messenger, a verse that itself shows divine speech reaching a human being need not occur through audible letters and sounds in the ordinary sense at all. (See: As-Sam', Al-Basar, and Al-Kalam)
