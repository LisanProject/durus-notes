Arabic Sciences (Nahw and Sarf)

Al-Ajrumiyyah

Lesson I: Introduction to Grammar

Identify each word below as ism, fi'l, or harf, and give the reason.

1. بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَنِ الرَّحِيمِ

بِ is a harf, a preposition attaching to the noun that follows it. اسْمِ is an ism, identified by the khafd it takes after the preposition. اللَّهِ is an ism, a proper noun majrur by annexation to اسْمِ. الرَّحْمَنِ and الرَّحِيمِ are both ism, identified by the definite article ال, and stand as descriptions (na't) of اللَّهِ, majrur to agree with it.

2. قَدْ يَكْتُبُ زَيْدٌ

قَدْ is a harf, since it attaches only to verbs. يَكْتُبُ is a fi'l, identified precisely by qad entering upon it. زَيْدٌ is an ism, identified by tanwin.

Lesson III: Signs of I'rab

Identify the sign of i'rab on the underlined word and name the category it belongs to.

3. قَالَ اللَّهُ تَعَالَى

اللَّهُ is marfu' as the fa'il of قَالَ. The sign is damma, since it is a singular noun.

4. الْمُسْلِمُونَ صَادِقُونَ

الْمُسْلِمُونَ is marfu' as mubtada. The sign is waw, since it is a sound masculine plural.

5. رَأَيْتُ الْمُسْلِمِينَ

الْمُسْلِمِينَ is mansub as maf'ul bihi. The sign is ya, since it is a sound masculine plural, which takes ya rather than fatha in nasb.

6. رَأَيْتُ الْمُسْلِمَاتِ

الْمُسْلِمَاتِ is mansub as maf'ul bihi. The sign is kasra, since it is a sound feminine plural, which takes kasra rather than fatha in nasb.

7. Surat al-Hajj 22:2 contains وَتَرَى النَّاسَ سُكَارَىٰ. Identify the case of سُكَارَىٰ and explain why its sign is not visible.

سُكَارَىٰ is mansub as the second object of تَرَى. It is a broken plural, and its sign of nasb is an implicit fatha, since a word ending in alif maqsura cannot physically carry a visible fatha.

8. Surat al-Nur 24:32 contains وَأَنكِحُوا الْأَيَامَىٰ مِنكُمْ. What category does الْأَيَامَىٰ belong to, and why is its fatha implicit rather than visible?

الْأَيَامَىٰ is a broken plural, mansub as the object of أَنكِحُوا. As with سُكَارَىٰ above, the fatha is implicit because the word ends in alif maqsura, which cannot take a visible case vowel.

9. Surat Taha 20:91 contains لَن نَّبْرَحَ عَلَيْهِ عَاكِفِينَ. Identify what makes نَّبْرَحَ mansub and name its sign.

نَّبْرَحَ is a present tense verb made mansub by the particle لَن, which governs the accusative in a following verb. Since nothing is attached to its end, its sign is a visible fatha.

The remaining questions below are translated directly from al-Tuhfat al-Saniyyah's own review questions for this section (translated here from the Arabic, not from any published English edition).

10. In how many places is the fatha a sign of nasb?

Three: the singular noun, the broken plural, and the present tense verb preceded by a nasib with nothing attached to its end.

11. Give four examples of a mansub singular noun: one masculine with explicit fatha, one masculine with implicit fatha, one feminine with explicit fatha, and one feminine with implicit fatha.

Masculine, explicit: لَقِيتُ عَلِيًّا. Masculine, implicit: لَقِيتُ الْفَتَى. Feminine, explicit: قَابَلْتُ هِنْدًا. Feminine, implicit: وَدَّعْتُ لَيْلَى.

12. Give four different examples of a mansub broken plural.

صَاحَبْتُ الرِّجَالَ، رَعَيْتُ الْهُنُودَ (both explicit fatha), وَتَرَى النَّاسَ سُكَارَىٰ، وَأَنكِحُوا الْأَيَامَىٰ (both implicit fatha, since both end in alif maqsura).

13. When is the mudari' verb made mansub by fatha?

When it is preceded by a nasib particle, such as لَنْ or أَنْ, and nothing else is attached to its end.

14. Give two different examples of a mansub mudari' verb.

لَن نَّبْرَحَ عَلَيْهِ عَاكِفِينَ (explicit fatha) and يُرْضِينِي أَنْ تَسْعَى إِلَى الْمَجْدِ (implicit fatha).

15. What sign of nasb does the mudari' verb take when the alif of duality is attached to its end?

Dropping the nun rather than fatha, e.g. لَنْ يَضْرِبَا.

16. If a mudari' verb preceded by a nasib has a confirming nun attached to its end, what is its ruling?

It becomes fixed (mabni) on fatha while remaining in the position of nasb, e.g. وَاللَّهِ لَنْ تَذْهَبَنَّ.

Usul al-Fiqh

Al-Waraqat

Chapter 1: Al-Muqaddima

1. Distinguish al-asl from al-far', and give your own example of each.

Al-asl is that upon which something else is built; al-far' is that which is built upon something else. Example: the obligation of wudu before prayer is an asl; the specific ruling that wiping over socks (khuffayn) can substitute for washing the feet is a far' built on it.

2. Why does the definition of fiqh specifically say "whose path is ijtihad"? What would be excluded if that phrase were removed?

Without that qualifier, universally agreed-upon rulings, such as the obligation of the five daily prayers, would count as "fiqh" too. The qualifier restricts fiqh to rulings reached through reasoning where disagreement is actually possible.

3. A person prays without wudu. Using the vocabulary of this chapter, is the prayer sahih or fasid, and why?

Fasid. It has no legal effect (nufudh) and does not count as discharging the obligation (i'tidad), since a required condition, purification, was not met.

4. Explain why "knowledge is recognition of the known" was criticized as a definition, and give the improved definition used instead.

It is circular, defining "knowledge" using "recognition," a near-synonym of the word being defined. The improved definition, "perceiving a thing as it actually is," grounds the definition in reality instead of another undefined term.

5. Distinguish simple ignorance from compound ignorance.

Simple ignorance is admitting you do not know something. Compound ignorance is confidently believing something false without realizing the belief is wrong, which is worse, since there is no awareness of the gap to correct.

6. A judge exercises ijtihad and reaches the wrong conclusion. According to the hadith cited in this chapter, is he rewarded or punished, and why does this only make sense in light of zann rather than certainty?

He is still rewarded, with one reward instead of two. This only makes sense because ijtihad operates in the space of zann, probable conjecture, not certainty. A judge cannot be blamed for missing a certainty that was never available to him in the first place; he is rewarded for the sincere effort of reasoning under genuine uncertainty.

7. Name the four sources of law that all major schools accept without dispute, and the two that generate the most debate about their scope.

Quran and Sunnah are accepted without dispute. Ijma' and qiyas are accepted by the mainline schools but debated in scope and definition, and rejected or restricted more sharply by the Zahiris and some within the Salafi tradition.

Chapter 2: Aqsam al-Kalam

8. Surat al-Shura 42:11 contains لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِ شَيْءٌ. Which type of majaz does this illustrate, and why can it not be read with full literalness?

Majaz by addition. Read with full literalness, "like unto Him" would imply Allah has a likeness, which the verse is negating, not asserting. The كَ is treated as an added element within the negation rather than literal.

9. Give an original example, not from the lesson, of a word that has undergone majaz by transference (naql).

Answers will vary. A commonly cited example beyond al-gha'it is the word "salah" itself, which linguistically means supplication (du'a) but was transferred by the Shari'ah to mean the specific ritual prayer.

Chapter 3: Al-Amr wa'l-Nahy

10. Someone is commanded "give charity" with no further context. Does this by itself require giving charity repeatedly, or only once? What would change that default?

On the correct view, a bare command does not imply repetition, so once would fulfill it. This default only changes if separate evidence indicates repetition was intended, such as zakah being tied to a recurring condition (a full lunar year passing on wealth above the nisab).

11. Explain the principle of "muqaddima al-wajib" using the example of prayer and wudu.

Whatever an obligation cannot be completed without is itself obligatory. Since valid prayer depends on wudu, the command to pray implicitly commands wudu as well, even though the verse commanding prayer never mentions wudu directly.
