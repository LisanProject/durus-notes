How to Study the Islamic Sciences

Before getting into specific tips let me say that everyone studies differently, and there is no single right way to study these sciences. Some of what is here will not apply to you, or you will not have the time to do all of it, and that is fine. All any of us can do is our best with the time we actually have. My hope with these notes is to help you do the best you can with the time you have got.

If you already have a study routine you are happy with, this may still be an interesting comparison. If you are not happy with how your studies are going, there are a few general ideas to get out of the way before the specific tips.

Most people who feel stuck fall into a few categories. Some simply have not developed good study habits for this kind of material yet. Others put in real hours but the hours are spent inefficiently, reading passively instead of reviewing actively. And some are not spending enough time at all, often for real reasons, work, family, other obligations. I cannot fix that last one for you, but hopefully a more efficient method makes the time you do have go further.

With that out of the way, here are three broad ideas that everything else below falls under.

Knowledge Is Not a Spectator Sport

You cannot learn these sciences by only sitting in a circle and listening. You have to attend, pay attention, and take a real set of notes. You have to review on a regular schedule, not just before you see your teacher again. You have to recite back what you are memorizing out loud, not just read it silently. Passive exposure to a text is not the same as knowing it.

Work to Understand, Not Just to Memorize

Memorization matters a great deal in this tradition, and I do not say this to diminish it. But memorizing the wording of a matn without understanding what it means, or how a rule actually applies to a real sentence or a real ruling, leaves you with words you cannot use. The goal is to be able to apply what you have memorized, not just recite it.

Knowledge Is Cumulative

Everything you study builds on what came before it, and this is deliberate. The classical curriculum is sequenced the way it is for a reason, the Ajrumiyyah before Qatr al-Nada, Qatr al-Nada before the Alfiyyah. Skipping ahead because a later text seems more interesting usually means struggling with vocabulary and concepts the text assumes you already have.
