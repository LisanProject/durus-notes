How to Study the Islamic Sciences

Intention

Before anything practical, intention comes first. There is a well known verse of poetry attributed to Imam Abu Hanifa on this point: the one who seeks knowledge for the sake of the Hereafter genuinely benefits and grows in righteousness, while the one who seeks it to gain standing over other people ends up in real loss, whatever they gain outwardly.

Imam al-Haddad's own statement of intention is worth returning to before any session of study. In short, it comes down to this: to learn in order to act and to teach, to take a reminder and to give one, to hold fast to the Quran and the Sunnah of the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, and to encourage others toward that same holding fast, calling to guidance while seeking nothing from it but Allah's pleasure and His reward.

Imam al-Dhahabi makes the practical difference plain: a sincere seeker of knowledge grows in humility, in fear of Allah, and in freedom from pride, while someone whose intention is worldly tends to grow arrogant, quarrelsome, and eager for praise. It is worth checking your own intention against this before diving into method.

With that in mind, here is a practical structure, organized around three habits that matter most: general study habits, memorization, and ongoing review.

General Tips

  • Study on a schedule, not in bursts. A short review most days beats a long cram once a week, especially for anything you are also memorizing.
  • Engage actively, do not just read. Read Arabic text out loud. Work through a problem yourself before checking how someone else solved it.
  • Prepare before a teaching session at roughly a two to one ratio. For every hour you expect to spend with a teacher, spend about two hours beforehand reading the relevant material, noting where sources differ, and writing down a short summary along with your actual points of confusion, so you arrive with real questions rather than encountering the material cold.
  • Ask why, not just what. Once a rule is memorized, ask why it exists. A rule you can only recite is not the same as a rule you understand well enough to apply somewhere new. Bring genuine confusion to your teacher rather than sitting on it unresolved.
  • Ground every rule in real usage. These sciences describe how real texts are actually put together, not an arbitrary set of conventions. Spend most of your time on what shows up constantly rather than a rare exception that appears once or twice.

For Nahw

Keep a separate notebook, or a separate Scrivener binder, for each individual matn rather than mixing everything together. For anything with nested divisions, sketch a simple tree diagram, kalam branching into ism, fi'l, and harf, or i'rab branching into raf', nasb, khafd, and jazm, with the signs of each underneath. Keep the diagram sparse, just the branching structure, no prose. Redraw it from memory before checking it against your notes. Wherever a branch is missing, that is exactly where the gap in your understanding is.

It also helps to read slightly above your current level alongside whatever you are studying formally. While working through a section of a matn with a teacher, read the same section in a commentary on your own, then sample the equivalent section in a more advanced text once you are comfortable. Seeing the same rule stated three ways, bare matn, a clear commentary, and a more advanced treatment, does more to fix it in your understanding than rereading the same source three times.

For Fiqh (Shafi'i Example)

The same notebook-per-matn principle applies here. If you are working through Matn Abi Shuja, keep it in its own binder rather than folding it into general fiqh notes, so the structure of the matn itself stays visible. Tree diagrams work just as well for fiqh classifications as they do for grammar, water divided into tahir mutahhir, tahir ghayr mutahhir, and najis, or the five categories of hukm taklifi, wajib, mandub, mubah, makruh, and haram, branching out from a single governing category the same way kalam or i'rab does in Nahw.

Reading slightly above your level applies here too. While working through Abu Shuja, read the same section in Fath al-Qarib al-Mujib, the standard commentary, on your own, then sample the equivalent section of a more detailed Shafi'i text once you are comfortable. Seeing a ruling stated bare, then explained, then treated in more depth fixes it far better than reading the matn alone three times.

Memorization

  • Memorize in small, consistent chunks, reviewed daily. A little material reviewed every day beats a large passage memorized once and left to decay.
  • Recite out loud. Hearing your own voice say something correctly reinforces it far more than silent repetition.
  • Test yourself rather than rereading. Recite from memory and only check the text when you actually get stuck. Reading along while "reviewing" feels productive but does much less for real retention.
  • Layer your review as material ages. New material needs daily review. Once it is solid, it can move to a weekly cycle, and eventually a monthly one.

For Nahw

Rather than reviewing everything at once, rotate your focus across the week. As one example: one day for i'rab and the signs of raf', another for the signs of nasb and khafd, another for the signs of jazm and the categories of nouns, another for particles and what each one governs, and a final day for a full review pass with no new material, just recall. Beyond the weekly rhythm, give any matn you have already finished a complete review at least once every two to four months, not only at the point you first finished it.

For Fiqh (Shafi'i Example)

Rotate your focus by chapter rather than trying to hold the whole matn in view at once. As one example, working through the early chapters of Abu Shuja: one day for the rulings on water and najasah, another for wudu and its nullifiers, another for ghusl and tayammum, another for the conditions and pillars of salah, and a final day for a full review pass, no new material, just recall. As with Nahw, give any completed chapter, and eventually the full matn, a complete review every two to four months rather than only reviewing it once and moving on.

Review (Muraja'ah)

  • Set a fixed weekly review slot. Ongoing review is what keeps earlier material from quietly fading while your attention is on what is current.
  • Explain a concept out loud or in writing without looking. If you cannot do that cleanly, that is exactly the gap review should close.
  • Revisit old material periodically, not only new material. Something you knew well months ago can erode just as easily as something you never fully learned.

For Nahw

Apply rules to text you already know well, an ayah or hadith you have memorized, rather than an invented sentence, so the only real difficulty is the grammar itself and not unfamiliar vocabulary. Take a phrase and work through it in full: identify each word's role, then justify its case ending from the category it belongs to, a sound masculine plural taking waw, a bare singular noun taking damma, a word after a preposition taking kasra. Do this weekly with a new piece of text so the recognition becomes automatic rather than something you have to reason through every time.

Once you finish a matn, it is worth compiling everything into a single reference sheet, in your own words and your own layout, something you can return to later without rereading the whole commentary. Done properly, this is also something you can eventually hand to other students.

For Fiqh (Shafi'i Example)

Apply rulings to a real, specific scenario rather than reviewing them as abstract statements. Instead of just recalling that standing water becomes najis once it changes in taste, color, or smell, work an actual case: a small amount of standing water has a dead insect in it with no change in taste, color, or smell, is it still tahir, and why. Walk through the governing category, the condition, and the ruling every time, the same way you would justify an i'rab case ending, rather than reciting the ruling from memory without the reasoning behind it.

Once you finish a chapter of Abu Shuja, compile its masa'il into your own reference sheet, in your own words, the same way you would after finishing a matn in Nahw. This is what actually gets returned to later, not the original commentary.
