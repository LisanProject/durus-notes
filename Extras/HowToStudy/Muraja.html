Review and Muraja'ah

Muraja'ah, ongoing review, is what keeps material from quietly slipping away after you have moved on to the next lesson. Islamic scholarship does not really have a final exam the way a university class does, but it does have the ongoing test of whether you can still actually use what you learned months or years ago.

  • Set a fixed weekly review slot. A regular time to go back over recent lessons, not just new material, keeps earlier texts from fading while you focus on what is current.
  • Review in the same structure you took notes in. If your notes consistently separate Arabic, translation, vocabulary, and commentary, reviewing becomes much faster because you already know exactly where to look for each piece.
  • Explain a concept out loud or in writing without looking. If you can teach a rule of grammar or a fiqh ruling back clearly without checking your notes, you actually know it. If you cannot, that is exactly the gap review should be closing.
  • Revisit old review questions periodically. Questions you answered easily months ago are worth rechecking. Material you have not touched in a while erodes even if you once knew it well.
  • Do not treat review as optional once you feel busy. Review is usually the first thing to get cut when time is tight, and it is also the thing whose absence you notice least immediately and regret most later.
