Taking Notes

Good notes are not a transcript of what was said. They are a structured record you can actually review later, which means some organization has to happen while you are taking them, not just after.

  • Write the Arabic first, then the translation, then commentary. Keeping this structure consistent across every lesson makes review much faster, since you always know where to look for a given piece of information.
  • Note vocabulary separately. A running vocabulary list per text, rather than vocabulary buried inside sentence notes, is far easier to drill later.
  • Mark what you did not understand. Do not smooth over confusion in your notes just to make them look complete. A note that says "unclear, ask about this" is more useful than a note that quietly gets the point wrong.
  • Leave room for correction. You will get things wrong, especially with i'rab and grammar. Leave space to update a note rather than needing to rewrite the whole page.
  • Review your notes within a day. Notes taken in a session fade fast. A quick pass the same day or the next, cleaning up handwriting or filling in gaps, cements a lot more than letting them sit untouched for a week.
